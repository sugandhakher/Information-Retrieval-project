<p>A <b>tanker</b> (or <b>tank ship</b> or <b>tankship</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Merchant_vessel" title="Merchant vessel">merchant vessel</a> designed to transport <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquids" title="Liquids">liquids</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gases</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bulk_liquids" title="Bulk liquids">bulk</a>. Major types of tankship include the <a href="/wiki/Oil_tanker" title="Oil tanker">oil tanker</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Chemical_tanker" title="Chemical tanker">chemical tanker</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Gas_carrier" title="Gas carrier">gas carrier</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Navy" title="United States Navy">United States Navy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Military_Sealift_Command" title="Military Sealift Command">Military Sealift Command</a>, any type of tanker used to refuel other ships is called an <i>oiler</i>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tanker_(ship)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tankers can range in size of capacity from several hundred <a href="/wiki/Tonnage" title="Tonnage">tons</a>, which includes vessels for servicing small harbours and coastal settlements, to several hundred thousand tons, for long-range haulage. Besides ocean- or seagoing tankers there are also specialized inland-waterway tankers which operate on rivers and canals with an average <a href="/wiki/Cargo" title="Cargo">cargo</a> capacity up to some thousand tons. A wide range of products are carried by tankers, including:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">hydrocarbon</a> products such as <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">oil</a>, <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_petroleum_gas" title="Liquefied petroleum gas">liquefied petroleum gas</a> (LPG), and <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">liquefied natural gas</a> (LNG)</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Chemical_substance" title="Chemical substance">chemicals</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">chlorine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Styrene" title="Styrene">styrene monomer</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Fresh_water" title="Fresh water">fresh water</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">wine</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Molasses" title="Molasses">molasses</a></li>
</ul>

<p>Tankers are a relatively new concept, dating from the later years of the 19th century. Before this, technology had simply not supported the idea of carrying bulk liquids. The market was also not geared towards transporting or selling cargo in bulk, therefore most ships carried a wide range of different products in different holds and traded outside fixed routes. Liquids were usually loaded in casks—hence the term "<a href="/wiki/Tonnage" title="Tonnage">tonnage</a>", which refers to the volume of the holds in terms of how many <a href="/wiki/Tun_(unit)" title="Tun (unit)">tuns</a> or casks of wine could be carried. Even potable water, vital for the survival of the crew, was stowed in casks. Carrying bulk liquids in earlier ships posed several problems:</p>
<ul>
<li>The holds: on timber ships the holds were not sufficiently water, oil or air-tight to prevent a liquid cargo from spoiling or leaking. The development of iron and steel hulls solved this problem.</li>
<li>Loading and discharging: Bulk liquids must be pumped - the development of efficient pumps and piping systems was vital to the development of the tanker. Steam engines were developed as prime-movers for early pumping systems. Dedicated cargo handling facilities were now required ashore too - as was a market for receiving a product in that quantity. Casks could be unloaded using ordinary cranes, and the awkward nature of the casks meant that the volume of liquid was always relatively small - therefore keeping the market more stable.</li>
<li><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_Surface_Effect" title="Free Surface Effect">Free Surface Effect</a>: a large body of liquid carried aboard a ship will impact on the ship's stability, particularly when the liquid is flowing around the hold or tank in response to the ship's movements. The effect was negligible in casks, but could cause capsizing if the tank extended the width of the ship; a problem solved by extensive subdivision of the tanks.</li>
</ul>
<p>Tankers were first used by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_industry" title="Oil industry">oil industry</a> to transfer refined fuel in bulk from refineries to customers. This would then be stored in large tanks ashore, and subdivided for delivery to individual locations. The use of tankers caught on because other liquids were also cheaper to transport in bulk, store in dedicated terminals, then subdivide. Even the <a href="/wiki/Guinness" title="Guinness">Guinness</a> brewery used tankers to transport the stout across the <a href="/wiki/Irish_Sea" title="Irish Sea">Irish Sea</a>.</p>

<p>Different products require different handling and transport, with specialised variants such as "<a href="/wiki/Chemical_tanker" title="Chemical tanker">chemical tankers</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Oil_tanker" title="Oil tanker">oil tankers</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/LNG_carrier" title="LNG carrier">LNG carriers</a>" developed to handle dangerous chemicals, oil and oil-derived products, and <a href="/wiki/Liquefied_natural_gas" title="Liquefied natural gas">liquefied natural gas</a> respectively. These broad variants may be further differentiated with respect to ability to carry only a single product or simultaneously transport mixed cargoes such as several different chemicals or refined petroleum products.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ot1_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ot1-1">[1]</a></sup> Among oil tankers, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supertanker" title="Supertanker">supertankers</a> are designed for transporting oil around the <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">Horn of Africa</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>. The supertanker <a href="/wiki/Seawise_Giant" title="Seawise Giant">Seawise Giant</a>, scrapped in 2010, was 458 meters (1,503 ft) in length and 69 meters (226 ft) wide. Supertankers are one of the three preferred methods for transporting large quantities of oil, along with <a href="/wiki/Pipeline_transport" title="Pipeline transport">pipeline transport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rail_transport" title="Rail transport">rail</a>.</p>
<p>Despite being highly regulated, tankers have been involved in environmental disasters resulting from <a href="/wiki/Oil_spill" title="Oil spill">oil spills</a>. See <i><a href="/wiki/Amoco_Cadiz" title="Amoco Cadiz">Amoco Cadiz</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/MV_Braer" title="MV Braer"><i>Braer</i></a>, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erika_(tanker)" title="Erika (tanker)">Erika</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Exxon_Valdez" title="Exxon Valdez">Exxon Valdez</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Prestige_oil_spill" title="Prestige oil spill">Prestige oil spill</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/SS_Torrey_Canyon" title="SS Torrey Canyon"><i>Torrey Canyon</i></a> for examples of coastal accidents.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Design_considerations">Design considerations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tanker_(ship)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design considerations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Many modern tankers are designed for a specific cargo and a specific route. <a href="/wiki/Draft_(hull)" title="Draft (hull)">Draft</a> is typically limited by the depth of water in loading and unloading harbors; and may be limited by the depth of <a href="/wiki/Strait" title="Strait">straits</a> along the preferred shipping route. Cargoes with high <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a> at ambient temperatures may require pressurized tanks or vapor recovery systems. Tank heaters may be required to maintain <a href="/wiki/Heavy_crude_oil" title="Heavy crude oil">heavy crude oil</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_Oil#Bunker_fuel" title="Fuel Oil">residual fuel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Asphalt" title="Asphalt">asphalt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wax" title="Wax">wax</a>, or molasses in a fluid state for offloading.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tanker_capacity">Tanker capacity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tanker_(ship)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tanker capacity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>In 1954, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shell_Oil" title="Shell Oil">Shell Oil</a> developed the <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Average_freight_rate_assessment&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Average freight rate assessment (page does not exist)">average freight rate assessment</a> (AFRA) system which classifies tankers of different sizes. To make it an independent instrument, Shell consulted the <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=London_Tanker_Brokers%E2%80%99_Panel&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="London Tanker Brokers’ Panel (page does not exist)">London Tanker Brokers’ Panel</a> (LTBP)</i>. At first, they divided the groups as <i>General Purpose</i> for tankers under 25,000  tons <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">deadweight</a> (DWT); <i>Medium Range</i> for ships between 25,000 and 45,000 <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a> and <i>Large Range</i> for the then-enormous ships that were larger than 45,000 <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a>. The ships became larger during the 1970s, and the list was extended, where the tons are <a href="/wiki/Long_ton" title="Long ton">long tons</a>:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<li>10,000–24,999 <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a>: General Purpose tanker</li>
<li>25,000–54,999 <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a>: Medium Range tanker</li>
<li>55,000–79,999 <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a>: Large Range 1 (LR1)</li>
<li>80,000–159,999 <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a>: Large Range 2 (LR2)</li>
<li>160,000–319,999 <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a>: Very Large Crude Carrier (VLCC)</li>
<li>320,000–549,999 <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a>: Ultra Large Crude Carrier (ULCC)</li>
</ul>

<p><b>Very Large Crude Carrier Size Range</b> There are more ships smaller in size.</p>
<p>At nearly 380 vessels in the size range 279,000 t <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a> to 320,000 t <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a>, these are by far the most popular size range among the larger VLCCs. Only seven vessels are larger than this, and approximately 90 between 220,000 t <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a> and 279,000 t <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fleets_of_the_world">Fleets of the world</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Tanker_(ship)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fleets of the world">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<dl>
<dt><b>Flag states</b></dt>
</dl>
<p>As of 2005, the United States Maritime Administration's statistics count 4,024 tankers of 10,000 LT <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">DWT</a> or greater worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marad_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-marad-5">[5]</a></sup> 2,582 of these are double-hulled. <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">Panama</a> is the leading <a href="/wiki/Flag_state" title="Flag state">flag state</a> of tankers with 592 registered ships. Five other flag states have more than two hundred registered tankers: <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">Liberia</a> (520), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Marshall_Islands" title="The Marshall Islands">The Marshall Islands</a> (323), <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">Greece</a> (233), <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">Singapore</a> (274) and <a href="/wiki/The_Bahamas" title="The Bahamas">The Bahamas</a> (215). These flag states are also the top six in terms of fleet size in terms of <a href="/wiki/Deadweight_tonnage" title="Deadweight tonnage">deadweight tonnage</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marad_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-marad-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><b>Largest fleets</b></dt>
</dl>
<p>Greece, Japan, and the United States are the top three owners of tankers (including those owned but <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_convenience" title="Flag of convenience">registered to other nations</a>), with 733, 394, and 311 vessels respectively. These three nations account for 1,438 vessels or over 36% of the world's fleet.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marad_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-marad-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<dl>
<dt><b>Builders</b></dt>
</dl>
<p>Asian companies dominate the construction of tankers. Of the world's 4,024 tankers, 2,822 or over 70% were built in South Korea, Japan or China.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-marad_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-marad-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
