<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Urban density</b> is a term used in <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">urban planning</a> and <a href="/wiki/Urban_design" title="Urban design">urban design</a> to refer to the number of people inhabiting a given <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urbanized_area" title="Urbanized area">urbanized area</a>. As such it is to be distinguished from other measures of <a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">population density</a>. Urban density is considered an important factor in understanding how cities function. Research related to urban density occurs across diverse areas, including economics, health, innovation, psychology and geography as well as <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainability">Sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_density&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Revised_petrol_use_urban_density.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="443" data-file-width="702" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/64/Revised_petrol_use_urban_density.JPG/220px-Revised_petrol_use_urban_density.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/64/Revised_petrol_use_urban_density.JPG/330px-Revised_petrol_use_urban_density.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/64/Revised_petrol_use_urban_density.JPG/440px-Revised_petrol_use_urban_density.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Revised_petrol_use_urban_density.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A graph showing the relationship between urban density and petrol use.</div>

<p>It is commonly asserted that higher density cities are more sustainable than low density cities. Much urban <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">planning theory</a>, particularly in North America, the UK, Australia and New Zealand has been developed premised on raising urban densities, such as <a href="/wiki/New_Urbanism" title="New Urbanism">New Urbanism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transit-oriented_development" title="Transit-oriented development">Transit-oriented development</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Smart_growth" title="Smart growth">Smart growth</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>However, the link between urban density and aspects of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> remains a contested area of planning theory<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Jan_Gehl" title="Jan Gehl">Jan Gehl</a>, prominent Urban Designer and expert on <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_urbanism" title="Sustainable urbanism">sustainable urbanism</a>, argues that low-density, dispersed cities are unsustainable as they are <a href="/wiki/Automobile_dependency" title="Automobile dependency">automobile dependent</a>. A minority, such as Randy O'Toole of the Libertarian <a href="/wiki/Cato_Institute" title="Cato Institute">Cato Institute</a>, counter that raising densities results in more expensive real estate, greater road congestion and more localized air pollution. Others counter that traffic congestion is a result not of population density but of parking capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> At a broader level, there is evidence to indicate a strong negative correlation between the total energy consumption of a city and its overall urban density, i.e. the lower the density, the more energy consumed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">Measurement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Urban_density&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Measurement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>Urban density</b> is a very specific measurement of the population of an urbanized area, excluding non-urban land-uses. Non-urban uses include regional open space, agriculture and water-bodies.</p>
<p>There are a variety of other ways of measuring the density of urban areas:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/Floor_area_ratio" title="Floor area ratio">Floor area ratio</a> - the total floor area of buildings divided by land area of the lot upon which the buildings are built</li>
<li>Residential density - the number of dwelling units in any given area</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Population_density" title="Population density">Population density</a> - the number of human persons in any given area</li>
<li>employment density - the number of jobs in any given area</li>
<li>Gross density - any density figure for a given area of land that includes uses not necessarily directly relevant to the figure (usually roads and other transport infrastructure)</li>
<li>Net density - a density figure for a given area of land that excludes land not directly related to the figure.</li>
<li>Weighted density - a density metric which measures the density at which the average citizen lives. It is determined by calculating the standard density of each census tract, assigning each a weight equal to its share of the total population, and then adding the segments.</li>
</ul>
