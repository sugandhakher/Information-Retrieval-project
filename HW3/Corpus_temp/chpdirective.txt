<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>This refers to the <b>Directive on the promotion of cogeneration based on a useful heat demand in the internal energy market and amending Directive 92/42/EEC</b>, officially 2004/8/EC and popularly better known as the '<a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">Combined Heat and Power (CHP)</a> Directive'</p>
<p>It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Union_directive" title="European Union directive">European Union directive</a> for promoting the use of <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> in order to increase the <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> and improve the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Security_of_supply" title="Security of supply">security of supply</a> of energy. This is intended to be achieved by creating a framework for the promotion and development of high efficiency cogeneration.</p>
<p>The directive entered into force in February 2004 and member states have been obliged to begin its implementation since 2006. (however due to delays resulting out of the <a href="/wiki/Comitology" title="Comitology">comitology</a> process, member states had to adopt the first obligations of the directive by 6 August 2007.)</p>
<p>It is intended that the directive will have a significant impact on the legislation and the diffusion of <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">CHP</a>/<a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> and <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> within the member states of the European Union.</p>
<p>In summary, the Member States are obliged to produce reports covering their analysis of the state of CHP in their own countries, to promote CHP and show what is being done to promote it, to report on and remove barriers, and to track progress of high-efficiency cogeneration within the energy market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>RES = Renewable energy sources<br/>
NG = Natural gas<br/>
Certificates: W = White certificates, R = Red certificates, Y = Yellow certificates, G = Green certificates<br/>
Notes:</p>
<ol>
<li>Grant support system to assist the deployment of small scale (less than 1 MWe) fossil-fired CHP and biomass (anaerobic digestion and wood residue) CHP systems + Financial tax incentives (Accelerated Capital Allowance Scheme)</li>
<li>Support schemes are under consideration in Malta</li>
<li>In Poland Red certificates are the equivalent of White certificates (for CHP) in other countries. Polish Authorities created also Yellow certificates (for Natural gas and CHP small systems below 1 MW of electric output) which compensate for relatively high cost of natural gas or high investment cost of distributed systems.</li>
<li>Under the Renewables Obligation, CHP plants using renewable energy fuel, such as biomass and biogas, benefit from a premium on each MWh of electricity produced. And new feed-in tariffs for renewables are expected to enter into force from April 2010.</li>
</ol>
