<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>The expression <b>sustainable art</b> has been promoted recently as an <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">art</a> term that can be distinguished from <a href="/wiki/Environmental_art" title="Environmental art">environmental art</a> that is in harmony with the key principles of <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a>, which include <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">social justice</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-violence" title="Non-violence">non-violence</a> and <a href="/wiki/Grassroots_democracy" title="Grassroots democracy">grassroots democracy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Sustainable art may also be understood as art that is produced with consideration for the wider impact of the work and its reception in relationship to its environments (social, economic, biophysical, historical and cultural).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_art&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the contemporary art historians and curators <a href="/wiki/Maja_and_Reuben_Fowkes" title="Maja and Reuben Fowkes">Maja and Reuben Fowkes</a>, the origins of sustainable art can be traced to the <a href="/wiki/Conceptual_art" title="Conceptual art">conceptual art</a> of the late 1960s and early 1970s, with its stress on dematerialisation and questioning of the functioning of the art system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> They also connect the rise of the concept of sustainability to the ending of the Cold War in 1989 and the emergence of a new awareness of the global character of ecological and social problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Sustainable art adopts, according to these authors, a critical position towards some key practitioners in the <a href="/wiki/Land_art" title="Land art">land art</a> movement of the 1960s, who showed little concern for the environmental consequences of e.g. treating the landscape like a giant canvas with a bulldozer for a brush.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> They have questioned the polemical division between 'autonomous' and 'instrumental' art originating with modernism, arguing that it is 'autonomy that gives art, as well as artists as social actors, the potential to be free and able to offer alternatives to dominant ideological paradigms.'<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Since 2005 there is a Sustainable Arts Biennale running at Ihlienworth near Hamburg, Germany, curated by the German conceptional artist and curator Samuel J. Fleiner. There are a range of interpretations over the relations between art and sustainability, besides the term 'sustainable art' promoted by Maja and Reuben Fowkes: Other authors prefer the broader notions of 'sustainability arts' or 'art and sustainability' (e.g. Kagan and Kirchberg).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Still others explicitly rejected the use of the term 'sustainable art', referring instead to 'artistic work that inspires us to think about sustainability" (Margot Käßmann).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Professional discussion of the relationship of contemporary art to notions of sustainability blossomed across Europe in the early years 2000, with e.g. the conference of the German Society for Political Culture (Instituts für Kulturpolitik der Kulturpolitischen Gesellschaft e.V.), in January 2002 at the Art Academy of Berlin, and the 'Tutzinger Manifest'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> An International Symposium on Sustainability and Contemporary Art took place at <a href="/wiki/Central_European_University" title="Central European University">Central European University</a>, in Budapest (Hungary) in March 2006. This was the first in a series of international symposia organised by <a href="/wiki/Maja_and_Reuben_Fowkes" title="Maja and Reuben Fowkes">Maja and Reuben Fowkes</a> bringing together contemporary artists, philosophers, environmental sciences and acvitists to explore common ground around issues such as 'Exit or Activism' (2008), 'Hard Realities and the New Materiality' (2009) and 'Art, Post-Fordism and Eco-Critique' (2010).<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.translocal.org/sustainability/index.htm" rel="nofollow">[1]</a>. In March–April 2007 at the <a href="/wiki/L%C3%BCneburg_University" title="Lüneburg University">Leuphana University Lüneburg</a>, the Arts Research Network of the European Sociological Association focused its attention on the recent movements and approaches to 'arts and sustainability' at its biennial conference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p>Key texts in the emerging field of sustainable art include 'Kultur - Kunst - Nachhaltigkeit' (2002) by Hildegard Kurt and Bernd Wagner,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> ‘The Principles of Sustainability in Contemporary Art’ (2006) by Maja and Reuben Fowkes <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and 'Art and Sustainability' (2011) by Sacha Kagan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> A collection of interdisciplinary analyses of the arts and cultures with relationship to sustainability is available in 'Sustainability: a new frontier for the arts and cultures' (2008) edited by Sacha Kagan and Volker Kirchberg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p>Exhibitions devoted explicitly to "sustainable art" include e.g. ‘Beyond Green: Towards a Sustainable Art’ at the Smart Museum in Chicago in November 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> For an analysis of the conflictual politics of sustainability and the ambiguity of the term sustainability (which oscillates between "ecological sustainability" and "economic sustainable development," see TJ Demos, “The Politics of Sustainability: Art and Ecology” (2009).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> For a recent account of the multi-faceted role of contemporary art in highlighting environmental issues, expressing criticism towards unsustainable factors in society, and offering imaginative solutions for the achievement of sustainability, see Maja and Reuben Fowkes's essay on 'Art and Sustainability' in Enough for All Forever (2012).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
