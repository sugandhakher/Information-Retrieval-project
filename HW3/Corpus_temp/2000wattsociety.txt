<p>The <b>2000-watt society</b> is an environmental vision, first introduced in 1998 by the <a href="/wiki/ETH_Zurich" title="ETH Zurich">Swiss Federal Institute of Technology in Zürich</a> (ETH Zurich), which pictures the average <a href="/wiki/First_World" title="First World">First World</a> citizen reducing their overall average <a href="/wiki/Primary_energy" title="Primary energy">primary energy</a> usage to no more than 2,000 <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">watts</a> (48 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Watt-hour" title="Watt-hour">kilowatt-hours</a> per day) by the year 2050 – and without lowering their <a href="/wiki/Standard_of_living" title="Standard of living">standard of living</a>.</p>
<p>The concept addresses not only personal or household energy use, but the total for the whole society, including <a href="/wiki/Embodied_energy" title="Embodied energy">embodied energy</a>, divided by the population.</p>
<p>Two thousand watts is approximately the current world average rate of total primary energy use. This compares to averages of around 6,000 watts in <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">western Europe</a>, 12,000 watts in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> 1,500 watts in <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>, 1,000 watts in India, 500 watts in South Africa and only 300 watts in Bangladesh.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kol_2008_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-kol_2008-2">[2]</a></sup> Switzerland itself, currently using an average of around 5,000 watts, was last a 2000-watt society in the 1960s.</p>
<p>It is further envisaged that the use of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">carbon based fuels</a> would be ultimately cut to no more than 500 watts per person within 50 to 100 years.</p>
<p>The vision was developed in response to concerns about <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_security" title="Energy security">energy security</a>, and the future availability of energy supplies. It is supported by the Swiss Federal Office of Energy,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> the Association of Swiss Architects and Engineers, and other bodies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Implications">Implications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2000-watt_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Implications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Researchers in Switzerland believe that this vision is achievable, despite a projected 65% increase in economic growth by 2050, by using new low-carbon technologies and techniques.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is envisaged that achieving the aim of a 2000-watt society will require, amongst other measures, a complete reinvestment in the country's capital assets; refurbishment of the nation's building stock to bring it up to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_energy_building" title="Low energy building">low energy building</a> standards; significant improvements in the efficiency of <a href="/wiki/Road_transport" title="Road transport">road transport</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aviation" title="Aviation">aviation</a> and energy-intensive material use; the possible introduction of high-speed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maglev_train" title="Maglev train">maglev trains</a>; the use of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sources, <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a>, <a href="/wiki/Microgeneration" title="Microgeneration">microgeneration</a> and related technologies; and a refocusing of research into new priority areas.</p>
<p>As a result of the intensified research and development effort required, it is hoped that Switzerland will become a leader in the technologies involved. Indeed, the idea has a great deal of government backing, due to fears about climate change.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Basel_pilot_region">The Basel pilot region</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=2000-watt_society&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Basel pilot region">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Launched in 2001 and located in the metropolitan area of <a href="/wiki/Basel" title="Basel">Basel</a>, 'Pilot Region Basel' aims to develop and commercialise some of the technologies involved. The pilot is a partnership between industry, universities, research institutes and the authorities, coordinated by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Novatlantis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Novatlantis (page does not exist)">Novatlantis</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Participation is not restricted to locally based organisations. The city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zurich" title="Zurich">Zurich</a> joined the project in 2005 and the canton of <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">Geneva</a> declared its interest in 2008.</p>
<p>Within the pilot region the projects in progress include demonstration buildings constructed to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MINERGIE" title="MINERGIE">MINERGIE</a></i> or <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passivhaus" title="Passivhaus">Passivhaus</a></i> standards, <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> generation from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sources, and vehicles using <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_economy" title="Hydrogen economy">hydrogen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a>. The aim is to put research into practice, seek continuous improvements, and to communicate progress to all interested parties, including the public.</p>
