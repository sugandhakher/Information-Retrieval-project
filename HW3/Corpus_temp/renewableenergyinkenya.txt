<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>The renewable energy sector in Kenya</b> is among the most active in Africa. In <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">Kenya</a>, investment grew from virtually zero in 2009 to US$1.3 billion in 2010 across technologies such as wind, geothermal, small-scale hydro and biofuels. This is not saying that Kenya was never active in the renewable energy sector. Kenya is Africa's first geothermal power producer<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> and the world leader in the number of solar power systems installed per capita. It is still the largest producer of geothermal power in Africa today at 200 MW <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> with only one other African country producing geothermal power, Ethiopia. Connectivity to the national grid in Kenya currently stands at 28%. In 2011, Kenya was also the first country in Africa to open a carbon exchange.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geothermal_power">Geothermal power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Kenya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geothermal power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Worker_in_Olkaria_Kenya.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="600" height="333" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Worker_in_Olkaria_Kenya.jpg/250px-Worker_in_Olkaria_Kenya.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Worker_in_Olkaria_Kenya.jpg/375px-Worker_in_Olkaria_Kenya.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Worker_in_Olkaria_Kenya.jpg/500px-Worker_in_Olkaria_Kenya.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<dl>
<dd><i>See main article: <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power_in_Kenya" title="Geothermal power in Kenya">Geothermal power in Kenya</a></i></dd>
</dl>
<p>Currently geothermal energy accounts for 20% of total installed capacity of the Kenyan grid. Kenya is the first African country to tap geothermal power and the largest producer of geo-energy, harnessing power from steam released by hot rocks beneath the Rift valley. The abundant sun and wind are also being harnessed in a variety of projects: of these, the <a href="/wiki/Lake_Turkana" title="Lake Turkana">Lake Turkana</a> project is the most audacious, both because of the scale and the location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Kenya has the capacity to produce 10 GW of geothermal energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Geothermal has a prominent place in Kenya’s overarching development plans. These include the Vision 2030, the National Climate Change Action Plan (NCCAP), and the current ‘5000+ MW in 40 months initiative’. Geothermal power has the potential to provide reliable, cost-competitive, baseload power with a small carbon footprint, and reduces vulnerability to climate by diversifying power supply away from hydropower, which currently provides the majority of Kenya’s electricity. Kenya has set out ambitious targets for geothermal energy. It aims to expand its geothermal power production capacity to 5,000 MW by 2030, with a medium-term target of installing 1,887 MW by 2017. As of October 2014, Kenya has an installed geothermal capacity of approximately 340 MW. Although there is significant political will and ambition, reaching these ambitions is a major challenge <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_power">Solar power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Kenya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solar power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Kenyans are a world leader in the number of solar power systems installed per capita. More Kenyans are turning to solar power rather than connections to the country’s electric grid. This is due to relatively high connectivity costs to the grid and the abundance of solar power in Kenya.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Kenya has several notable solar power distributors including <a href="/wiki/M-kopa" title="M-kopa">M-Kopa</a> with locally adopted payment schemes including pay-as-you-go and <a href="/wiki/Microfinance_in_Kenya" title="Microfinance in Kenya">microfinance</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> and <a href="/wiki/International_Finance_Corporation" title="International Finance Corporation">International Finance Corporation (IFC)</a> have partnered as part of a Lighting Africa initiative.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> Western companies have also helped sponsor efforts to introduce decentralized solar power solutions in the country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
