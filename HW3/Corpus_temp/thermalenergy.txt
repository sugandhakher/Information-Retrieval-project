<a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">Thermal radiation</a> in visible light can be seen on this hot metalwork. Thermal energy would ideally be the amount of heat required to warm the metal to its temperature, but this quantity is not well-defined, as there are many ways to obtain a given body at a given temperature, and each of them may require a different amount of total <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a> input. Thermal energy, unlike <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">internal energy</a>, is therefore not a state function.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, <b>thermal energy</b> refers to the <a href="/wiki/Internal_energy" title="Internal energy">internal energy</a> present in a system due to its <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">temperature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-eb-1">[1]</a></sup> The concept is not well-defined or broadly accepted in <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">physics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">thermodynamics</a>, because the internal energy can be changed without changing the temperature, and there is no way to distinguish which part of the internal energy is "thermal". Instead, the appropriate thermodynamic concept is <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">heat</a>, defined as a <i>transfer</i> of energy (just as <a href="/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)" title="Work (thermodynamics)">work</a> is another type of transfer of energy). Heat and work therefore depend on the path of transfer and are not state functions, whereas internal energy is a state function.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_to_heat_and_internal_energy">Relation to heat and internal energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Relation to heat and internal energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">Heat</a> is energy transferred spontaneously from a hotter to a colder system or body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speyer_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-speyer-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-leland_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-leland-3">[3]</a></sup> Heat is energy in transfer, not a property of the system; it is not <i>contained</i> within the boundary of the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-speyer_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-speyer-2">[2]</a></sup> On the other hand, internal energy is a property of a system, and exists on both sides of a boundary. In an <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">ideal gas</a>, the internal energy is the statistical mean of the kinetic energy of the gas particles, and it is this kinetic motion that is the source and the effect of the transfer of heat across a system boundary. The internal energy of an ideal gas can in this sense be regarded as "thermal energy". In this case, however, thermal energy and internal energy are identical.</p>
<p>Systems that are more complex than ideal gases (such as real gases) can undergo <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phase_transitions" title="Phase transitions">phase transitions</a>. Phase transitions can change the internal energy of the system without changing its temperature. Therefore, the thermal energy cannot be defined solely by the temperature. Thermal energy also cannot be defined by the difference between the internal energy and the net heat transfer in or out of the system, because it is easy to construct thermodynamic cycles where the system begins and ends in exactly the same state, but there is a net flow of heat in or out during the cycle.</p>
<p>For these reasons, the concept of the thermal energy of a system is ill-defined and is not used in thermodynamics.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_context">Historical context</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Thermal_energy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical context">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In an 1847 lecture entitled <i>On Matter, Living Force, and Heat</i>, <a href="/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule" title="James Prescott Joule">James Prescott Joule</a> characterized various terms that are closely related to thermal energy and heat. He identified the terms <a href="/wiki/Latent_heat" title="Latent heat">latent heat</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sensible_heat" title="Sensible heat">sensible heat</a> as forms of heat each effecting distinct physical phenomena, namely the potential and kinetic energy of particles, respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> He describes latent energy as the energy of interaction in a given configuration of particles, i.e. a form of <a href="/wiki/Potential_energy" title="Potential energy">potential energy</a>, and the sensible heat as an energy affecting temperature measured by the thermometer due to the thermal energy, which he called the <i>living force</i>.</p>
