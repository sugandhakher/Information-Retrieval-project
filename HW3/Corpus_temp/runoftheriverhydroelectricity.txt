<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Run-of-the-river hydroelectricity</b> (<b>ROR</b>) is a type of <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity" title="Hydroelectricity">hydroelectric</a> generation plant whereby little or no water storage is provided. Run-of-the-river power plants may have no water storage at all or a limited amount of storage, in which case the storage reservoir is referred to as <a href="/wiki/Pondage" title="Pondage">pondage</a>. A plant without pondage has no water storage and is, therefore, subject to seasonal river flows. Thus, the plant will operate as an <a href="/wiki/Intermittent_energy_source" title="Intermittent energy source">intermittent energy source</a> while a plant with pondage can regulate the water flow at all times and can serve as a <a href="/wiki/Peaking_power_plant" title="Peaking power plant">peaking power plant</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Base_load_power_plant" title="Base load power plant">base load power plant</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">Concept</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Concept">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Run-of-the-river or ROR hydroelectricity is considered ideal for streams or rivers that can sustain a minimum flow or those regulated by a lake or reservoir upstream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dwivedi_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dwivedi-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-raghun_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-raghun-2">[2]</a></sup> A small dam is usually built to create a headpond ensuring that there is enough water entering the <a href="/wiki/Penstock" title="Penstock">penstock</a> pipes that lead to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turbines" title="Turbines">turbines</a> which are at a lower elevation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-two-3">[3]</a></sup> Projects with pondage, as opposed to those without pondage, can store water for daily load demands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dwivedi_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dwivedi-1">[1]</a></sup> In general, projects divert some or most of a river's flow (up to 95% of mean annual discharge)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-one_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-one-4">[4]</a></sup> through a pipe and/or tunnel leading to electricity-generating turbines, then return the water back to the river downstream.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-two-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>ROR projects are dramatically different in design and appearance from conventional hydroelectric projects. Traditional hydro dams store enormous quantities of water in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reservoirs" title="Reservoirs">reservoirs</a>, sometimes flooding large tracts of land. In contrast, run-of-river projects do not have most of the <a href="/wiki/Hydroelectricity#Disadvantages" title="Hydroelectricity">disadvantages associated with dams and reservoirs</a>, which is why they are often considered environmentally friendly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-three_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-three-5">[5]</a></sup> In Canada no large hydroelectric reservoir has been created since the 1980s.</p>
<p>The use of the term "run-of-the-river" for power projects varies around the world. Some may consider a project ROR if power is produced with no water storage while limited storage is considered ROR by others. Developers may mislabel a project ROR to soothe public perception about its environmental or social effects. The <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Indian_Standards" title="Bureau of Indian Standards">Bureau of Indian Standards</a> describes run-of-the-river hydroelectricity as:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INDDAM_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-INDDAM-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<blockquote>
<p>A power station utilizing the run of the river flows for generation of power with sufficient pondage for supplying water for meeting diurnal or weekly fluctuations of demand. In such stations, the normal course of the river is not materially altered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-INDDAM_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-INDDAM-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Many of the larger ROR projects have been designed to a scale and generating capacity rivaling some traditional hydro dams.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-four_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-four-7">[7]</a></sup> For example, the <a href="/wiki/Beauharnois_Hydroelectric_Generating_Station" title="Beauharnois Hydroelectric Generating Station">Beauharnois Hydroelectric Generating Station</a> in Quebec is rated at 1,853 MW<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and a 2006 proposal in <a href="/wiki/British_Columbia" title="British Columbia">British Columbia</a>, Canada has been designed to generate 1027 megawatts capacity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-five-9">[9]</a></sup> Some run of the river projects are downstream of other dams and reservoirs. The run of the river project didn't build the reservoir, but does take advantage of the water supplied by it. An example would be the 1995, 1,436 MW <a href="/wiki/La_Grande-1_generating_station" title="La Grande-1 generating station">La Grande-1 generating station</a>, previous upstream dams and reservoirs are part of the 1980s <a href="/wiki/James_Bay_Project" title="James Bay Project">James Bay Project</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">Advantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Advantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>When developed with care to footprint size and location, ROR hydro projects can create sustainable energy minimizing impacts to the surrounding environment and nearby communities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-two-3">[3]</a></sup> Advantages include:</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleaner_power.2C_fewer_greenhouse_gases">Cleaner power, fewer greenhouse gases</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cleaner power, fewer greenhouse gases">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Like all hydro-electric power, run-of-the-river hydro harnesses the natural potential energy of water, eliminating the need to burn coal or <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> to generate the electricity needed by consumers and industry.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Less_flooding.2Freservoirs">Less flooding/reservoirs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Less flooding/reservoirs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Substantial flooding of the upper part of the river is not required for run-of-river projects as a large reservoir is not required. As a result, people living at or near the river don't need to be relocated and natural habitats and productive farmlands are not wiped out.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disadvantages">Disadvantages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Disadvantages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id=".22Unfirm.22_power">"Unfirm" power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: "Unfirm" power'>edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Run-of-the-River power is considered an "unfirm" source of power: a run-of-the-river project has little or no capacity for energy storage<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-six_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-six-10">[10]</a></sup> and hence can't co-ordinate the output of electricity generation to match consumer demand. It thus generates much more power during times when seasonal river flows are high (i.e., spring freshet),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-five-9">[9]</a></sup> and depending on location, much less during drier summer months or frozen winter months.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Availability_of_sites">Availability of sites</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Availability of sites">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The potential power at a site is a result of the <a href="/wiki/Hydraulic_head" title="Hydraulic head">head</a> and flow of water. By damming a river, the head is available to generate power at the face of the dam. Where a dam may create a reservoir hundreds of kilometers long, in run of the river the head is usually delivered by a canal, pipe or tunnel constructed upstream of the power house. Due to the cost of upstream construction, a steep drop in the river is desirable.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impacts">Environmental impacts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Environmental impacts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Small, well-sited ROR projects can be developed with minimal environmental impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-two-3">[3]</a></sup> Larger projects have more environmental concerns. For example, Plutonic Power Corp.'s canceled <a href="/wiki/Bute_Inlet" title="Bute Inlet">Bute Inlet</a> Hydroelectric Project in BC would have seen three clusters of run-of-river projects with 17 river diversions;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-four_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-four-7">[7]</a></sup> as proposed, this run-of-river project would divert over 90 kilometres of streams and rivers into tunnels and pipelines, requiring 443 km of new transmission line, 267 km of permanent roads, and 142 bridges to be built in wilderness areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seven_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-seven-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>British Columbia's mountainous terrain and wealth of big rivers have made it a global testing ground for run-of-river technology. As of March 2010, there were 628 applications pending for new water licences solely for the purposes of power generation – representing more than 750 potential points of river diversion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eight_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-eight-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Concerns">Concerns</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Run-of-the-river_hydroelectricity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Concerns">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>Diverting large amounts of river water reduces river flows, affecting water velocity and depth, minimizing habitat quality for fish and aquatic organisms; reduced flows can lead to excessively warm water for salmon and other fish in summer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-two-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>New access roads and transmission lines can cause extensive habitat fragmentation for many species, allowing the introduction of invasive species and increases in undesirable human activities, like illegal hunting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-two_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-two-3">[3]</a></sup></li>
<li>The lack of reservoir storage may result in intermittent operation, reducing the project's viability.</li>
</ul>
