<p><b>Flue-gas emissions from fossil-fuel combustion</b> refers to the <a href="/wiki/Combustion" title="Combustion">combustion</a>-product gas resulting from the burning of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuels</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-1">[1]</a></sup> Most fossil fuels are combusted with ambient air (as differentiated from combustion with pure <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a>). Since ambient air contains about 79 volume percent gaseous <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">nitrogen</a> (N<sub>2</sub>),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> which is essentially non-combustible, the largest part of the <a href="/wiki/Flue_gas" title="Flue gas">flue gas</a> from most fossil-fuel combustion is uncombusted nitrogen. <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">Carbon dioxide</a> (CO<sub>2</sub>), the next largest part of flue gas, can be as much as 10−25 volume percent or more of the flue gas. This is closely followed in volume by water vapor (H<sub>2</sub>O) created by the combustion of the hydrogen in the fuel with atmospheric oxygen. Much of the 'smoke' seen pouring from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flue_gas_stack" title="Flue gas stack">flue gas stacks</a> is this water vapor forming a cloud as it contacts cool air.</p>
<p>A typical flue gas from the combustion of fossil fuels contains very small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide" title="Nitrogen oxide">nitrogen oxides</a> (NOx), <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a> (SO<sub>2</sub>) and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter" title="Atmospheric particulate matter">particulate matter</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EPA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-1">[1]</a></sup> The nitrogen oxides are derived from the nitrogen in the ambient air as well as from any nitrogen-containing compounds in the fossil fuel. The sulfur dioxide is derived from any <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">sulfur</a>-containing compounds in the fuels. The particulate matter is composed of very small particles of solid materials and very small liquid droplets which give flue gases their smoky appearance.</p>
<p>The steam generators in large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant">power plants</a> and the process <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnaces</a> in large <a href="/wiki/Oil_refinery" title="Oil refinery">refineries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petrochemical" title="Petrochemical">petrochemical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chemical_plant" title="Chemical plant">chemical plants</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incinerator" title="Incinerator">incinerators</a> burn considerable amounts of fossil fuels and therefore emit large amounts of flue gas to the ambient atmosphere. The table below presents the total amounts of flue gas typically generated by the burning of fossil fuels such as <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fuel_oil" title="Fuel oil">fuel oil</a> and <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>. The data were obtained by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stoichometry" title="Stoichometry">stoichiometric</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> calculations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>It is of interest to note that the total amount of flue gas generated by coal combustion is only 10 percent higher than the flue gas generated by natural-gas combustion.</p>
