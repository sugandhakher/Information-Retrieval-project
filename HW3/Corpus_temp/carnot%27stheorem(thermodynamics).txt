<p><b>Carnot's theorem</b>, developed in 1824 by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_L%C3%A9onard_Sadi_Carnot" title="Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot">Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot</a>, also called <b>Carnot's rule</b>, is a principle that specifies limits on the maximum efficiency any <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a> can obtain. The efficiency of a Carnot engine depends solely on the difference between the hot and cold temperature reservoirs.</p>
<p>Carnot's theorem states:</p>
<ul>
<li>All heat engines between two heat reservoirs are less efficient than a <a href="/wiki/Carnot_heat_engine" title="Carnot heat engine">Carnot heat engine</a> operating between the same reservoirs.</li>
<li>Every Carnot heat engine between a pair of heat reservoirs is equally efficient, regardless of the working substance employed or the operation details.</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Proof">Proof</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Proof">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="394" data-file-width="632" height="187" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg/300px-Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg/450px-Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg/600px-Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg 2x" width="300"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Carnot_theorem_paradox.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
An impossible situation: A heat engine cannot drive a less efficient (reversible) heat engine without violating the second law of thermodynamics.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The proof of the Carnot theorem is a <a href="/wiki/Proof_by_contradiction" title="Proof by contradiction">proof by contradiction</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Reductio_ad_absurdum" title="Reductio ad absurdum">reductio ad absurdum</a>, as illustrated by the figure showing two heat engines operating between two reservoirs of different temperature. The heat engine with more efficiency (<span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applicability_to_fuel_cells_and_batteries">Applicability to fuel cells and batteries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Applicability to fuel cells and batteries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a> and <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">batteries</a> can generate useful power when all components of the system are at the same temperature (<span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<mi>T</mi>
<mo>=</mo>
<msub>
<mi>T</mi>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mi>H</mi>
</mrow>
</msub>
<mo>=</mo>
<msub>
<mi>T</mi>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mi>C</mi>
</mrow>
</msub>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle T=T_{H}=T_{C}}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="T=T_{H}=T_{C}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/233890e553656f326f6eda99cd3062dcf3a8d354" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:13.788ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span>), they are clearly not limited by Carnot's theorem, which states that no power can be generated when <span><span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
<semantics>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
<msub>
<mi>T</mi>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mi>H</mi>
</mrow>
</msub>
<mo>=</mo>
<msub>
<mi>T</mi>
<mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
<mi>C</mi>
</mrow>
</msub>
</mstyle>
</mrow>
<annotation encoding="application/x-tex">{\displaystyle T_{H}=T_{C}}</annotation>
</semantics>
</math></span><img alt="T_{H}=T_{C}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7151385fd41ac1c8a940b4b767efe36c8c3b2d2" style="vertical-align: -0.671ex; width:9.032ex; height:2.509ex;"/></span>. This is because Carnot's theorem applies to engines converting thermal energy to work, whereas fuel cells and batteries instead convert chemical energy to work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Nevertheless, the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a> still provides restrictions on fuel cell and battery energy conversion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
