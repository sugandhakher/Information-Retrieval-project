The specific objectives and actions vary between funding periods. In FP6 and FP7 focus was still in technological research, in Horizon 2020 the focus is in innovation, delivering economic growth faster and delivering solutions to end users that are often governmental agencies.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Framework_Programmes_for_Research_and_Technological_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Conducting European research policies and implementing European research programmes is an obligation under the <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Treaty" title="Amsterdam Treaty">Amsterdam Treaty</a>, which includes a chapter on research and technological development. The programmes are defined by Commission civil servants that are aided by various official advisory group and lobby groups. E.g. to advise the <a href="/wiki/European_Commission" title="European Commission">European Commission</a> on the overall strategy to be followed in carrying out the Information and Communication Technology thematic priority, the Information Society Technologies Advisory Group (ISTAG) was set up.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Funding_instruments">Funding instruments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Framework_Programmes_for_Research_and_Technological_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Funding instruments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="FP6_and_FP7">FP6 and FP7</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Framework_Programmes_for_Research_and_Technological_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: FP6 and FP7">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><span id="Framework_Programme_7"></span> Framework Programme 6 and 7 (2002–13) projects were generally funded through <b>instruments</b>, the most important of which are listed below.</p>
<ul>
<li>Integrating Project (IP)
<ul>
<li>Medium- to large-sized collaborative research projects funded in FP6 and FP7. They are composed of a minimum of three partners coming from three different countries from <a href="/wiki/Associated_state" title="Associated state">Associated states</a> but can join several tens of partners. The typical duration of such projects is three to five years but there is not a defined upper limit. The budget granted by the Commission can reach several tens of million euros, paid as a fraction of the actual costs spent by the participants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></li>
<li>IPs specifically aim at fostering European competitiveness in <a href="/wiki/Basic_research" title="Basic research">basic research</a> and <a href="/wiki/Applied_science" title="Applied science">applied science</a> with a focus on "addressing major needs in society" defined by the Priority Themes of the Framework Programme. Like STRePs <i>(see below)</i>, IPs ask for a strong participation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Small_and_Medium-sized_Enterprise" title="Small and Medium-sized Enterprise">small or medium-sized enterprises (SMEs)</a> to ascertain the translation of research results into commercially viable products or services.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Network of Excellence (NoE)
<ul>
<li>Medium-sized research projects co-funded by the European Commission in FP6 and FP7. These projects are "designed to strengthen scientific and technological excellence on a particular research topic through the durable integration of the research capacities of the participants."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></li>
<li>NoE projects require the minimum participation of three different EU member nations, however, projects are usually expected to involve at least six countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdf_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-pdf-15">[15]</a></sup> Projects are provided grants for a maximum of seven years. The budget granted by the Commission is €1–6 million per year depending upon the number of researchers involved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdf_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-pdf-15">[15]</a></sup></li>
<li>An NoE project should not strictly be considered as a research project, since its aim is not to conduct research, but rather to contribute to the clarification of the concepts in the covered field.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>Specific Targeted Research Projects (STReP)
<ul>
<li>Medium-sized research projects funded by the European Commission in the FP6 and FP7 funding programs. STReP projects are composed by a minimum of three partners coming from three different countries from Associated states. The typical duration of such projects is two to three years. In FP6, they generally involved between six and 15 partners. The budget granted by the Commission is in average around €2 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Horizon_2020">Horizon 2020</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Framework_Programmes_for_Research_and_Technological_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Horizon 2020">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Horizon 2020 is the eighth framework programme funding research, technological development, and innovation. The programme's name has been modified to "Framework Programme for Research and Innovation". The framework programme is implemented by the European Commission, the executive body of the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, either by various internal directorate general (DGs), such as the directorate general for research and innovation (<a class="external text" href="http://ec.europa.eu/research/index.cfm?pg=dg" rel="nofollow">DG RTD</a>) or the directorate general for communications networks, content and Technology, or by executive agencies such as the Research Executive Agency (REA), the Executive Agency for SMEs (EASME), or the ERC Executive Agency (ERCEA). The framework programme's objective is to complete the <a href="/wiki/European_Research_Area" title="European Research Area">European Research Area</a> (ERA) by coordinating national research policies and pooling research funding in some areas to avoid duplication. Horizon 2020 itself is seen as a policy instrument to implement other high-level policy initiatives of the European Union, such as <a href="/wiki/Europe_2020" title="Europe 2020">Europe 2020</a> and Innovation Union. The programme runs from 2014–20 and provides an estimated <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%E2%82%AC" title="€">€</a>80 billion of funding,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-grove_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-grove-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amos_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-amos-18">[18]</a></sup> an increase of 23 per cent on the previous phase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rabesandratana_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-rabesandratana-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>Horizon 2020 provides grants to research and innovation projects through open and competitive calls for proposals. Legal entities from any country are eligible to submit project proposals to these calls. Participation from outside the European Union is explicitly encouraged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Participants from European Union member states and countries associated to Horizon 2020 are automatically funded. Associated countries have signed an association agreement for the purposes of this framework programme. To date, 14 countries are associated to Horizon 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> Switzerland is considered as "partly associated" due to the <a href="/wiki/Swiss_referendums,_2014" title="Swiss referendums, 2014">2014 referendums</a> held by <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a>, which free movement of workers between Switzerland and the EU. Swiss organisations continue to be active participants in Horizon 2020, however, their participation is sometimes covered by national funding. <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> is an "associated country" to Horizon 2020. A central point of negotiation was funding to projects beyond the <a href="/wiki/Green_Line_(Israel)" title="Green Line (Israel)">Green Line</a>;.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elis_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-elis-22">[22]</a></sup> Israel published its views in an Appendix to the official documents. Horizon 2020 supports <a href="/wiki/Open_access" title="Open access">Open access</a> to research results,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-openaccess_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-openaccess-23">[23]</a></sup> in order to create greater efficiency, improve transparency and accelerate innovation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guideopenaccess_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-guideopenaccess-24">[24]</a></sup></p>
<p>Horizon 2020 is also implementing the <a href="/wiki/European_environmental_research_and_innovation_policy" title="European environmental research and innovation policy">European environmental research and innovation policy</a>, which is aimed at defining and turning into reality a transformative agenda for greening the economy and the society as a whole so as to achieve a truly sustainable development.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">Structure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Framework_Programmes_for_Research_and_Technological_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Structure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>The programme consists of three main research areas that are called "pillars".</p>
<ul>
<li>The first pillar, "Excellent Science", focuses on basic science. It has a budget of 24 billion euro. The <a href="/wiki/European_Research_Council" title="European Research Council">European Research Council</a> (ERC) delivers 13 billion euro to researchers and teams of researchers based on scientific excellence of the applications. This pillar funds future and emerging technologies (FET, €2.7 billion) and researcher mobility (<a href="/wiki/Marie_Curie" title="Marie Curie">Marie Sklodowska-Curie</a> Action (MSCA), €6.1 billion) and large European research infrastructures (€2.5 billion).
<ul>
<li>MSCA supports the career development and training of researchers at all stages of their careers. It is the main EU programme for doctoral training funding 25,000 PhDs during seven years. It co-funds national PhD programmes. Other MSCA funding targets research networks, fellowships for individual researchers, research staff exchanges and arrange "European Researchers' Night"-event annually on the last Friday of September.</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>The second pillar is "Industrial Leadership", with a budget of 14 billion euro. It is managed by DG Enterprise and based on Europe 2020 and Innovation Union strategies. The pillar consists of six sub-programmes within "Leadership in Enabling and Industrial Technologies":
<ul>
<li>Information and communication technologies</li>
<li>Nanotechnologies</li>
<li>Advanced materials</li>
<li>Advanced manufacturing and processing</li>
<li>Biotechnology</li>
<li>Space</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<dl>
<dd>These technologies all have European technology platforms (ETP; fora for research communities to meet) with their respective strategic research agendas (SRA). Some technologies have long term funding instruments, such as joint technology initiatives (JTI). Some technologies are labelled key enabling technologies (KET).</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>This pillar contains special efforts to SME funding and gives also risk financing (2.8 billion euro) e.g. through loans of the European Investment Bank.</dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>The third pillar funds potential solutions to social and economic problems, "Societal challenges" (SC), in the following seven sub-programmes:
<ul>
<li>Health (€7.5 billion)</li>
<li>Food, water, forestry, bioeconomy (€3.8 billion)</li>
<li>Energy (€5.9 billion)</li>
<li>Transport (€6.3 billion)</li>
<li>Climate action, environment, resource efficiency, and raw materials (€3.1 billion)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></li>
<li>European society (€1.3 billion)</li>
<li>Security (€1.7 billion)</li>
<li>This pillar also funds themes names as "Science with and for society" (€0.5 billion) and "Spreading excellence and widening participation" (€0.8 billion).</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<p>The structure follows the previous framework programme (FP7, 2007–13) to the level of the sub-programmes under the pillars. In the industrial pillar the goal is to find ways to modernize European industries that have suffered from a fragmented European market. In societal challenges the goal is implementation of solutions, less on technology development.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Illustrative_projects">Illustrative projects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Framework_Programmes_for_Research_and_Technological_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Illustrative projects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="OpenAIRE">OpenAIRE</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Framework_Programmes_for_Research_and_Technological_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: OpenAIRE">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A network of Open Access repositories, archives and journals that support Open Access policies. The <b>OpenAIRE Consortium</b> is a <a href="#Horizon_2020">Horizon 2020</a> (FP8) project, aimed to support the implementation of the <a href="/wiki/European_Council" title="European Council">EC</a> and <a href="/wiki/European_Research_Council" title="European Research Council">ERC</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_Access" title="Open Access">Open Access</a> policies.</p>
<dl>
<dd>"</dd>
<dd>Its successor OpenAIREplus is aimed at linking the aggregated research publications to the accompanying research and project information, datasets and author information.</dd>
<dd>Open access to scientific peer reviewed publications has evolved from a pilot project with limited scope in FP7 to an underlying principle in the Horizon 2020 funding scheme, obligatory for all H2020 funded projects.</dd>
<dd>The goal is to make as much European funded research output as possible available to all, via the OpenAIRE portal.</dd>
<dd>", openaire.eu FAQ<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Zenodo" title="Zenodo">Zenodo</a> research data repository is a product of OpenAIRE. The OpenAIRE portal is at <a class="external free" href="https://www.openaire.eu" rel="nofollow">https://www.openaire.eu</a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_the_programmes">Criticism of the programmes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Framework_Programmes_for_Research_and_Technological_Development&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Criticism of the programmes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The programmes have been criticized on various grounds, such as actually diminishing Europe's industrial competitiveness<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> and failing to deliver fundamental excellence and global economic competitiveness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In 2010, the <a href="/wiki/Austrian_Research_Promotion_Agency" title="Austrian Research Promotion Agency">Austrian Research Promotion Agency</a> launched a petition calling for a simplification of administrative procedures, which attracted over 13,000 signatories.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup> The numerous other criticisms of the petitioners were later distilled into a Green Paper.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> In Horizon 2020 there are significant simplifications: e.g. fewer funding rates (increasing the funding rates of the large companies), less reporting, less auditing, shorter time from proposal to project kick-off.</p>
