<p><b>Building energy simulation</b>, also called <b>building energy modeling</b> (or <b>energy modeling</b> in context), is the use of software to predict the energy use of a building.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_energy_simulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A typical energy model will have inputs for climate; envelope; internal gains from lighting, equipment, and occupants; heating, cooling, and ventilation systems; schedules of occupants, equipment, and lighting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bldgreen_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-bldgreen-1">[1]</a></sup> Energy models will output building energy use predictions in typical end-use categories: heating, cooling, lighting, fan, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Plug_Load&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Plug Load (page does not exist)">plug</a>, process. In addition to energy units, most software includes utility rates input, and can predict energy costs.</p>
<p>Energy-savings measures can be calculated using simple spreadsheets<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> and a wide variety of bespoke software applications are available.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">Applications</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_energy_simulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><b>Building design</b>: Many modern commercial or residential building codes require minimum energy performance. Energy modeling can be used to demonstrate compliance, or predict energy consumption of proposed developments.</li>
<li><b><a href="/wiki/Life-cycle_cost_analysis" title="Life-cycle cost analysis">Life-cycle cost analysis</a></b>: Comparing different building design alternatives to determine which is the least cost, including capital costs and (at a minimum) energy costs. Sometimes referred to as <b>Total Cost of Ownership</b> analysis.</li>
<li><b>Energy retrofit analysis</b>: In conjunction with an <a href="/wiki/Energy_audit" title="Energy audit">energy audit</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Deep_energy_retrofit" title="Deep energy retrofit">deep energy retrofit</a> an energy model can be used to predict savings associated to proposed energy cost measures (often called ECMs).</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accuracy_of_building_simulation">Accuracy of building simulation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_energy_simulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Accuracy of building simulation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are normally occurring <a href="/wiki/Uncertainties_in_building_design_and_building_energy_assessment" title="Uncertainties in building design and building energy assessment">uncertainties in building design and building energy assessment</a>. Yezioro, Dong and Leite<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> developed an artificial intelligence approach towards assessing building performance simulation results and found that more detailed simulation tools have the best simulation performance in terms of heating and cooling electricity consumption within 3% of mean absolute error.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Standards_and_Practices">Standards and Practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Building_energy_simulation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Standards and Practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/ASHRAE_90.1" title="ASHRAE 90.1">ASHRAE 90.1</a>: In the US, ASHRAE 90.1's Energy Cost Budget (ECB) method is a commonly used baseline for energy modeling comparisons.</li>
</ul>
