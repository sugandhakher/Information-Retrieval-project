<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Nuclear transmutation</b> is the conversion of one <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">chemical element</a> or an <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotope</a> into another.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Because any element (isotope) is defined by its number of <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">protons</a> (and <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a>) in its <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">atoms</a>, i.e. in the <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">atomic nucleus</a>, nuclear transmutation occurs in any process where this number is changed.</p>
<p>A transmutation can be achieved either by <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">nuclear reactions</a> (in which an outside particle reacts with a nucleus) or by <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> (where no outside particle is needed).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_and_artificial_transmutation">Natural and artificial transmutation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_transmutation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Natural and artificial transmutation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Although all transmutation is caused by either decay or nuclear reaction, not all of these processes cause transmutation. E.g., <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_decay" title="Gamma decay">gamma decay</a>, <a href="/wiki/Internal_conversion" title="Internal conversion">internal conversion</a> do not transmute the affected atom.</p>
<p>Natural transmutation by <a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">stellar nucleosynthesis</a> in the past created most of the heavier chemical elements in the universe, see the corresponding section below.</p>
<p>One type of natural transmutation observable in the present occurs when certain <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactive" title="Radioactive">radioactive</a> elements present in nature spontaneously decay by a process that causes transmutation, such as <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha</a> or <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a>. An example is the natural decay of <a href="/wiki/Potassium-40" title="Potassium-40">potassium-40</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Argon-40" title="Argon-40">argon-40</a>, which forms most of the <a href="/wiki/Argon" title="Argon">argon</a> in air. Also on Earth, natural transmutations from the different mechanism of <i>natural <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">nuclear reactions</a></i> occur, due to <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic ray</a> bombardment of elements (for example, to form <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">carbon-14</a>), and also occasionally from natural neutron bombardment (for example, see <a href="/wiki/Natural_nuclear_fission_reactor" title="Natural nuclear fission reactor">natural nuclear fission reactor</a>).</p>
<p>Artificial transmutation may occur in machinery that has enough energy to cause changes in the nuclear structure of the elements. Such machines include <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">particle accelerators</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tokamak" title="Tokamak">tokamak</a> reactors. Conventional <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission#Fission_reactors" title="Nuclear fission">fission power reactors</a> also cause artificial transmutation, not from the power of the machine, but by exposing elements to <a href="/wiki/Neutron" title="Neutron">neutrons</a> produced by fission from an artificially produced <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_chain_reaction" title="Nuclear chain reaction">nuclear chain reaction</a>. For instance, when a uranium atom is bombarded with slow neutrons, fission takes place. This releases, on average, 3 neutrons and a large amount of energy. The released neutrons then cause fission of other uranium atoms, until all of the available uranium is exhausted. This is called a chain reaction.</p>
<p>Artificial nuclear transmutation has been considered as a possible mechanism for reducing the volume and hazard of <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">radioactive waste</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_transmutation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Alchemy">Alchemy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_transmutation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alchemy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The term <i>transmutation</i> dates back to <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a>. Alchemists pursued the <a href="/wiki/Philosopher%27s_stone" title="Philosopher's stone">philosopher's stone</a>, capable of <a href="/wiki/Chrysopoeia" title="Chrysopoeia">chrysopoeia</a> â€“ the transformation of <a href="/wiki/Base_metal" title="Base metal">base metals</a> into gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> While alchemists often understood chrysopoeia as a metaphor for a mystical, or religious process, some practitioners adopted a literal interpretation, and tried to make gold through physical experiment. The impossibility of the metallic transmutation had been debated amongst alchemists, philosophers and scientists since the Middle Ages. Pseudo-alchemical transmutation was outlawed<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and publicly mocked beginning in the fourteenth century. Alchemists like <a href="/wiki/Michael_Maier" title="Michael Maier">Michael Maier</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Khunrath" title="Heinrich Khunrath">Heinrich Khunrath</a> wrote tracts exposing fraudulent claims of gold making. By the 1720s, there were no longer any respectable figures pursuing the physical transmutation of substances into gold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier" title="Antoine Lavoisier">Antoine Lavoisier</a>, in the 18th century, replaced the <a href="/wiki/Classical_element#Elements_in_Medieval_alchemy" title="Classical element">alchemical theory of elements</a> with the modern theory of chemical elements, and <a href="/wiki/John_Dalton" title="John Dalton">John Dalton</a> further developed the notion of atoms (from the alchemical theory of <a href="/wiki/Corpuscularianism" title="Corpuscularianism">corpuscles</a>) to explain various chemical processes. The disintegration of atoms is a distinct process involving much greater energies than could be achieved by alchemists.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_physics">Modern physics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_transmutation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Modern physics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It was first consciously applied to modern physics by <a href="/wiki/Frederick_Soddy" title="Frederick Soddy">Frederick Soddy</a> when he, along with <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">Ernest Rutherford</a>, discovered that radioactive <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">thorium</a> was converting itself into <a href="/wiki/Radium" title="Radium">radium</a> in 1901. At the moment of realization, Soddy later recalled, he shouted out: "Rutherford, this is transmutation!" Rutherford snapped back, "For Christ's sake, Soddy, don't call it <i>transmutation</i>. They'll have our heads off as alchemists."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Rutherford and Soddy were observing natural transmutation as a part of <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">radioactive decay</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Alpha_decay" title="Alpha decay">alpha decay</a> type. However in 1919, Rutherford was able to accomplish transmutation of nitrogen into <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-17" title="Oxygen-17">oxygen</a>, using alpha particles directed at nitrogen <sup>14</sup>N + Î± â†’ <a href="/wiki/Oxygen-17" title="Oxygen-17"><sup>17</sup>O</a> + p.Â  This was the first observation of a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reaction" title="Nuclear reaction">nuclear reaction</a>, that is, a reaction in which particles from one decay are used to transform another atomic nucleus. Eventually, in 1932, a fully artificial nuclear reaction and nuclear transmutation was achieved by Rutherford's colleagues <a href="/wiki/John_Cockcroft" title="John Cockcroft">John Cockcroft</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Walton" title="Ernest Walton">Ernest Walton</a>, who used artificially accelerated protons against lithium-7 to split the nucleus into two alpha particles. The feat was popularly known as "splitting the atom," although it was not the modern <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a> reaction discovered in 1938 by <a href="/wiki/Otto_Hahn" title="Otto Hahn">Otto Hahn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lise_Meitner" title="Lise Meitner">Lise Meitner</a> and their assistant Fritz Strassmann in heavy elements.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>Later in the twentieth century the transmutation of elements within stars was elaborated, accounting for the relative abundance of heavier elements in the universe. Save for the first five elements, which were produced in the Big Bang and other <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">cosmic ray</a> processes, stellar nucleosynthesis accounted for the abundance of all elements heavier than boron. In their 1957 paper <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synthesis_of_the_Elements_in_Stars" title="Synthesis of the Elements in Stars">Synthesis of the Elements in Stars</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/William_Alfred_Fowler" title="William Alfred Fowler">William Alfred Fowler</a>, <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Burbidge" title="Margaret Burbidge">Margaret Burbidge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Burbidge" title="Geoffrey Burbidge">Geoffrey Burbidge</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hoyle" title="Fred Hoyle">Fred Hoyle</a> explained how the abundances of essentially all but the lightest chemical elements could be explained by the process of <a href="/wiki/Nucleosynthesis" title="Nucleosynthesis">nucleosynthesis</a> in stars.</p>
<p>It transpired that, under true nuclear transmutation, it is far easier to turn gold into lead than the reverse reaction, which was the one the alchemists had ardently pursued. Nuclear experiments have successfully transmuted lead into gold, but the expense far exceeds any gain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> It would be easier to convert gold into lead via <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture" title="Neutron capture">neutron capture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">beta decay</a> by leaving gold in a nuclear reactor for a long period of time.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Glenn_T._Seaborg#Return_to_California" title="Glenn T. Seaborg">Glenn Seaborg produced several thousand atoms of gold from bismuth</a>, but at a net loss.</p>
<p>More information on gold synthesis, see <a href="/wiki/Synthesis_of_precious_metals" title="Synthesis of precious metals">Synthesis of precious metals</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transmutation_in_the_universe">Transmutation in the universe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_transmutation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Transmutation in the universe">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Nucleosynthesis" title="Nucleosynthesis">nucleosynthesis</a></div>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Big_Bang" title="Big Bang">Big Bang</a> is thought to be the origin of the hydrogen (including all <a href="/wiki/Deuterium" title="Deuterium">deuterium</a>) and helium in the universe. Hydrogen and helium together account for 98% of the mass of ordinary matter in the universe. The Big Bang also produced small amounts of <a href="/wiki/Lithium" title="Lithium">lithium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Beryllium" title="Beryllium">beryllium</a> and perhaps <a href="/wiki/Boron" title="Boron">boron</a>. More lithium, beryllium and boron were produced later, in a natural nuclear reaction, <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray_spallation" title="Cosmic ray spallation">cosmic ray spallation</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">Stellar nucleosynthesis</a> is responsible for all of the other elements occurring naturally in the universe as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stable_isotopes" title="Stable isotopes">stable isotopes</a> and <a href="/wiki/Primordial_nuclide" title="Primordial nuclide">primordial nuclide</a>, from <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> to <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a>. These occurred after the Big Bang, during star formation. Some lighter elements from carbon to iron were formed in stars and released into space by <a href="/wiki/Asymptotic_giant_branch" title="Asymptotic giant branch">asymptotic giant branch</a> (AGB) stars. These are a type of red giant that "puffs" off its outer atmosphere, containing some elements from carbon to nickel and iron. All elements with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_weight" title="Atomic weight">atomic weight</a> greater than 64 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">atomic mass units</a> are produced in <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">supernova</a> stars by means of nuclear reaction of lighter nuclei called <a href="/wiki/Neutron_capture" title="Neutron capture">neutron capture</a>, which sub-divides into two processes: <a href="/wiki/R-process" title="R-process">r-process</a> and <a href="/wiki/S-process" title="S-process">s-process</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">Solar System</a> is thought to have condensed approximately 4.6 billion years before the present, from a cloud of hydrogen and helium containing heavier elements in dust grains formed previously by a large number of such stars. These grains contained the heavier elements formed by transmutation earlier in the history of the universe.</p>
<p>All of these natural processes of transmutation in stars are continuing today, in our own galaxy and in others. For example, the observed light curves of supernova stars such as <a href="/wiki/SN_1987A" title="SN 1987A">SN 1987A</a> show them blasting large amounts (comparable to the mass of Earth) of radioactive nickel and cobalt into space. However, little of this material reaches Earth. Most natural transmutation on the Earth today is mediated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_rays" title="Cosmic rays">cosmic rays</a> (such as production of <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">carbon-14</a>) and by the radioactive decay of radioactive <a href="/wiki/Primordial_nuclide" title="Primordial nuclide">primordial nuclides</a> left over from the initial formation of the solar system (such as <a href="/wiki/Potassium-40" title="Potassium-40">potassium-40</a>, uranium and thorium), plus the radioactive decay of products of these nucleides (radium, radon, polonium, etc.). See <a href="/wiki/Decay_chain" title="Decay chain">decay chain</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Artificial_transmutation_of_nuclear_waste">Artificial transmutation of nuclear waste</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_transmutation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Artificial transmutation of nuclear waste">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_transmutation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Overview">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Transmutation of <a href="/wiki/Transuranium_element" title="Transuranium element">transuranium elements</a> (TRUs, i.e. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actinides" title="Actinides">actinides</a> minus <a href="/wiki/Actinium" title="Actinium">actinium</a> to <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">uranium</a>) such as the <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">isotopes</a> of <a href="/wiki/Plutonium" title="Plutonium">plutonium</a> (about 1wt% in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_Water_Reactor" title="Light Water Reactor">Light Water Reactors</a>' used <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fuel" title="Nuclear fuel">nuclear fuel</a>) or the minor actinides (MAs, i.e. <a href="/wiki/Neptunium" title="Neptunium">neptunium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Americium" title="Americium">americium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Curium" title="Curium">curium</a>, about 0.1wt% each in LWRs' UNF) has the potential to help solve some problems posed by the management of <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_waste" title="Radioactive waste">radioactive waste</a> by reducing the proportion of long-lived isotopes it contains. (This does not rule-out the need for a Deep Geological Repository (DGR) for High radioactive Level Waste (HLW).) When irradiated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fast_neutrons" title="Fast neutrons">fast neutrons</a> in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">nuclear reactor</a>, these isotopes can undergo <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">nuclear fission</a>, destroying the original <a href="/wiki/Actinide" title="Actinide">actinide</a> isotope and producing a spectrum of radioactive and nonradioactive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fission_products" title="Fission products">fission products</a>.</p>
<p>Ceramic targets containing actinides can be bombarded with neutrons to induce transmutation reactions to remove the most difficult long-lived species. These can consist of actinide-containing solid solutions such as (Am,Zr)N, (Am,Y)N, (Zr,Cm)O<sub>2</sub>, (Zr,Cm,Am)O<sub>2</sub>, (Zr,Am,Y)O<sub>2</sub> or just actinide phases such as AmO<sub>2</sub>, NpO<sub>2</sub>, NpN, AmN mixed with some inert phases such as MgO, MgAl<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, (Zr,Y)O<sub>2</sub>, TiN and ZrN. The role of non-radioactive inert phases is mainly to provide stable mechanical behaviour to the target under neutron irradiation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reactor_types">Reactor types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_transmutation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Reactor types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>For instance, plutonium can be reprocessed into <a href="/wiki/MOX_fuel" title="MOX fuel">MOX fuels</a> and transmuted in standard reactors. The heavier elements could be transmuted in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fast_reactor" title="Fast reactor">fast reactors</a>, but probably more effectively in a subcritical reactor which is sometimes known as an <a href="/wiki/Energy_amplifier" title="Energy amplifier">energy amplifier</a> and which was devised by <a href="/wiki/Carlo_Rubbia" title="Carlo Rubbia">Carlo Rubbia</a>. <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">Fusion</a> <a href="/wiki/Neutron_source" title="Neutron source">neutron sources</a> have also been proposed as well suited.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_types">Fuel types</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_transmutation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fuel types">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are several fuels that can incorporate plutonium in their initial composition at beginning of cycle (BOC) and have a smaller amount of this element at the end of cycle (EOC). During the cycle, plutonium can be burnt in a power reactor, generating electricity. This process is not only interesting from a power generation standpoint, but also due to its capability of consuming the surplus weapons grade plutonium from the weapons program and plutonium resulting of <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reprocessing" title="Nuclear reprocessing">reprocessing</a> UNF.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/MOX_fuel" title="MOX fuel">Mixed oxide fuel (MOX)</a> is one of these. Its blend of oxides of plutonium and uranium constitutes an alternative to the low enriched uranium (LEU) fuel predominantly used in LWRs. Since uranium is present in MOX, although plutonium will be burnt, second generation plutonium will be produced through the radiative capture of U-238 and the two subsequent beta minus decays.</p>
<p>Fuels with plutonium and thorium are also an option. In these, the neutrons released in the fission of plutonium are captured by Th-232. After this radiative capture, Th-232 becomes Th-233, which undergoes two beta minus decays resulting in the production of the fissile isotope U-233. The radiative capture cross section for Th-232 is more than three times that of U-238, yielding a higher conversion to fissile fuel than that from U-238. Due to the absence of uranium in the fuel, there is no second generation plutonium produced, and the amount of plutonium burnt will be higher than in MOX fuels. However, U-233, which is fissile, will be present in the UNF. Weapons-grade and reactor-grade plutonium can be used in plutonium-thorium fuels, with weapons-grade plutonium being the one that shows a bigger reduction in the amount of Pu-239.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reasoning_behind_transmutation">Reasoning behind transmutation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Nuclear_transmutation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Reasoning behind transmutation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Isotopes of plutonium and other actinides tend to be long-lived with <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">half-lives</a> of many thousands of years, whereas radioactive fission products tend to be shorter-lived (most with half-lives of 30 years or less). From a waste management viewpoint, transmutation (or "burning" or "incineration") of actinides eliminates a very long-term radioactive hazard and replaces it with a much shorter-term one.</p>
<p>It is important to understand that the threat posed by a radioisotope is influenced by many factors including the <a href="/wiki/Physical_chemistry" title="Physical chemistry">physical</a> (e.g. heat -infrared photon radiation-, which is an advantage for the storage or disposal af radioactive waste), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical" title="Chemical">chemical</a> and <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biological</a> properties of the element. For instance <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">caesium</a> has a relatively short <a href="/wiki/Biological_half-life" title="Biological half-life">biological half-life</a> (1 to 4 months) while <a href="/wiki/Strontium" title="Strontium">strontium</a> and <a href="/wiki/Radium" title="Radium">radium</a> both have very long biological half-lives. As a result <a href="/wiki/Strontium-90" title="Strontium-90">strontium-90</a> and radium are much more able to cause harm than <a href="/wiki/Caesium-137" title="Caesium-137">caesium-137</a> when a given activity is ingested. <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">Insert a brief calculation of doses</span><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="Or insert a brief calculation of doses (February 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many of the actinides are very radiotoxic because they have long biological half-lives and are <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">alpha</a> emitters. In transmutation the intention is to convert the actinides into <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fission_products" title="Fission products">fission products</a>. The fission products are very radioactive, but the majority of the activity will decay away within a short time. The most worrying short-lived fission products are those that accumulate in the body, such as <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">iodine</a>-131 which accumulates in the thyroid gland, but it is hoped<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (March 2014)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> that by good design of the nuclear fuel and transmutation plant that such fission products can be isolated from humans and their environment and allowed to decay. In the medium term the fission products of highest concern are strontium-90 and caesium-137; both have a half-life of about 30 years. The caesium-137 is responsible for the majority of the external <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_radiation" title="Gamma radiation">gamma</a> dose experienced by workers in <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reprocessing" title="Nuclear reprocessing">nuclear reprocessing</a> plants<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> and, in 2005, to workers at the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl" title="Chernobyl">Chernobyl</a> site.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> When these medium-lived isotopes have decayed almost completely (usually after 10 half-lives) the remaining isotopes will pose a much smaller threat.</p>

<p>Some radioactive fission products can be converted into shorter-lived radioisotopes by transmutation. Transmutation of all fission products with half-life greater than one year is studied in Grenoble,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> with varying results.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sr-90" title="Sr-90">Sr-90</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cs-137" title="Cs-137">Cs-137</a>, with half-lives of about 30 years, are the largest radiation (including heat) emitters in used nuclear fuel on a scale of decades to ~305 years (Sn-121m is insignificant because of the low yield), and are not easily transmuted because they have low <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutron_absorption" title="Neutron absorption">neutron absorption</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutron_cross-section" title="Neutron cross-section">cross sections</a>. Instead, they should simply be stored until they decay. Given that this length of storage is necessary, the fission products with shorter half-lives can also be stored until they decay.</p>
<p>The next longer-lived fission product is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sm-151" title="Sm-151">Sm-151</a>, which has a half-life of 90 years, and is such a good neutron absorber that most of it is transmuted while the nuclear fuel is still being used; however, effectively transmuting the remaining Sm-151 in nuclear waste would require separation from other isotopes of <a href="/wiki/Samarium" title="Samarium">samarium</a>. Given the smaller quantities and its low-energy radioactivity, Sm-151 is less dangerous than Sr-90 and Cs-137 and can also be left to decay for ~970 years.</p>
<p>Finally, there are 7 <a href="/wiki/Long-lived_fission_product" title="Long-lived fission product">long-lived fission products</a>. They have much longer half-lives in the range 211,000 years to 15.7 million years. Two of them, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tc-99" title="Tc-99">Tc-99</a> and <a href="/wiki/Iodine-129" title="Iodine-129">I-129</a>, are mobile enough in the environment to be potential dangers, are free or mostly free of mixture with stable isotopes of the same element, and have neutron cross sections that are small but adequate to support transmutation. Also, Tc-99 can substitute for <a href="/wiki/Uranium-238" title="Uranium-238">U-238</a> in supplying <a href="/wiki/Doppler_broadening" title="Doppler broadening">Doppler broadening</a> for negative feedback for reactor stability.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> Most studies of proposed transmutation schemes have assumed <a href="/wiki/Technetium" title="Technetium"><sup>99</sup>Tc</a>, <a href="/wiki/Iodine-129" title="Iodine-129"><sup>129</sup>I</a>, and TRUs as the targets for transmutation, with other fission products, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Activation_products" title="Activation products">activation products</a>, and possibly <a href="/wiki/Reprocessed_uranium" title="Reprocessed uranium">reprocessed uranium</a> remaining as waste.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>Of the remaining 5 long-lived fission products, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Se-79" title="Se-79">Se-79</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sn-126" title="Sn-126">Sn-126</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pd-107" title="Pd-107">Pd-107</a> are produced only in small quantities (at least in today's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_neutron" title="Thermal neutron">thermal neutron</a>, <a href="/wiki/Uranium-235" title="Uranium-235">U-235</a>-burning <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_water_reactors" title="Light water reactors">light water reactors</a>) and the last two should be relatively inert. The other two, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zr-93" title="Zr-93">Zr-93</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cs-135" title="Cs-135">Cs-135</a>, are produced in larger quantities, but also not highly mobile in the environment. They are also mixed with larger quantities of other isotopes of the same element.</p>
