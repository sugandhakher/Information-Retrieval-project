<p>The <b>green paradox</b>, identified by German economist <a href="/wiki/Hans-Werner_Sinn" title="Hans-Werner Sinn">Hans-Werner Sinn</a>, is the observation that an <a href="/wiki/Environmentalism" title="Environmentalism">environmental</a> <a href="/wiki/Public_policy" title="Public policy">policy</a> that becomes greener with the passage of time acts like an <a href="/wiki/Expropriation" title="Expropriation">announced expropriation</a> for the owners of <a href="/wiki/Fossil_fuel" title="Fossil fuel">fossil fuel</a> resources, inducing them to accelerate <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resource_extraction" title="Resource extraction">resource extraction</a> and hence to accelerate <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Main_line_of_reasoning">Main line of reasoning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Main line of reasoning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The green paradox’s line of reasoning starts by recognizing a fundamental, unavoidable fact: every <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a> atom in the gas, coal or oil extracted from the ground to be used as fuel ends up in the atmosphere, in particular if high efficiency combustion processes ensure that no part of it ends up as soot. About a quarter of the emitted carbon will stay in the atmosphere practically forever, contributing to the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">greenhouse effect</a> that causes global warming.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Apart from <a href="/wiki/Afforestation" title="Afforestation">afforestation</a>, only two things can mitigate the accumulation of carbon in the atmosphere: either less carbon is extracted from the ground, or it is injected back underground after harvesting its energy.</p>
<p>Environmental policy efforts, however, in particular European ones, go in neither of these two directions, aiming instead at the promotion of alternative, CO<sub>2</sub>-free energy sources and a more efficient use of energy. In other words, they only address the demand side of the carbon market, neglecting the supply side. Despite considerable investment, the efforts to curtail demand have not entirely had the effect hoped for.</p>
<p>The reason behind this, according to Sinn, is that green policies, by heralding a gradual tightening of policy over the coming decades, exert a stronger downward pressure on future prices than on current ones, decreasing thus the rate of capital appreciation of the fossil fuel deposits. The owners of these resources regard this development with concern and react by increasing extraction volumes, converting the proceeds into investments in the capital markets, which offer higher yields. That is the green paradox: environmental policy slated to become greener over time acts as an announced expropriation that provokes owners to react by accelerating the rate of extraction of their fossil fuel stocks,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> thus accelerating climate change.</p>
<p>Countries that do not partake of the efforts to curb demand have a double advantage. They burn the carbon set free by the “green” countries (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_leakage" title="Carbon leakage">leakage effect</a>) and they also burn the additional carbon extracted as a reaction to the announced and expected price cuts resulting from the gradual greening of environmental policies (green paradox).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sinn writes in his abstract that: "[Demand reduction strategies] simply depress the world price of carbon and induce the environmental sinners to consume what the <a href="/wiki/Kyoto_Protocol" title="Kyoto Protocol">Kyoto countries</a> have economized on. Even worse, if suppliers feel threatened by a gradual greening of economic policies in the Kyoto countries that would damage their future prices, they will extract their stocks more rapidly, thus accelerating global warming."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>Sinn emphasizes that a condition for the green paradox is that the resource be scarce in the sense that its price will always be higher than the unit extraction and exploration costs combined. He points out that this condition is likely to be satisfied as backstop technologies will at best offer a perfect substitute for electricity, but not for fossil fuels. The prices of coal and crude oil are currently many times higher than the corresponding exploration and extraction costs combined.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Practicable_solutions">Practicable solutions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Green_paradox&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Practicable solutions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An effective climate policy must perforce focus on the hitherto neglected supply side of the carbon market in addition to the demand side. The ways proposed as practicable by Sinn include levying a withholding tax on the capital gains on the financial investments of fossil fuel resource owners, or the establishment of a seamless global emissions trading system that would effectively put a cap on worldwide fossil fuel consumption, thereby achieving the desired reduction in carbon extraction rates.</p>
<p>A suggestion for a solution might also be to pay suppliers for the destruction of fossil fuels (or transform them into raw material (not fuel), thus making sure that on the demand side the independancy for fossil fuels still pays off, while there is reduction in carbon extraction.</p>
