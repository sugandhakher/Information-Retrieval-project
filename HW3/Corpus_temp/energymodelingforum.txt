<p>The <b>Energy Modeling Forum</b> (<b>EMF</b>) is a structured forum for discussing important issues in energy and the environment. The EMF was established in 1976 at <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">Stanford University</a>. The EMF works through a series of ad hoc working groups, each focussing on a particular corporate or policy decision. The EMF provides a non-partisan platform that ensures objective consideration of opposing views. Participation is by invitation.</p>
<p>Since the late-1990s, the EMF has made contributions to the economics of <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, as witnessed in the reports of the <a href="/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change" title="Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change">Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change</a> (IPCC) and on <a href="/wiki/Integrated_assessment_modelling" title="Integrated assessment modelling">integrated assessment modeling</a> more generally.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Weyant" title="John Weyant">John Weyant</a> is the current director of the EMF.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ethos">Ethos</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_Modeling_Forum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ethos">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The EMF was convened in 1976 over concerns that the insights that large-scale <a href="/wiki/Energy_modeling" title="Energy modeling">energy models</a> could provide <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Policymaker" title="Policymaker">policymakers</a> were being overshadowed by the "plethora of detailed quantitative results" being disseminated and discussed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huntington-etal-1982_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-huntington-etal-1982-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:449</sup> As a result, the EMF sought to bring energy modelers together to provide a proper context for their work. Indeed, the EMF was "formed to foster better communication between the builders and users of energy models in energy planning and policy analysis".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huntington-etal-1982_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-huntington-etal-1982-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:449</sup> The EMF periodically establishes <span class="nowrap">ad hoc</span> working groups to conduct studies on selected energy topics. A working group then identifies relevant existing models and sets a series of tests to illuminate the basic structure and behavior of each model. Results are compared and the strengths and weaknesses of each model is documented in a (as of 1982) freely available report.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huntington-etal-1982_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-huntington-etal-1982-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
