<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>A <b>negative carbon dioxide emission</b> or <b>negative emission</b> or a process that is <b>carbon negative</b> gives a permanent removal of the <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gas</a> <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Obersteiner_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Obersteiner-2">[2]</a></sup> It is considered the direct opposite of carbon dioxide emission, hence its name. It is the result of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_removal" title="Carbon dioxide removal">carbon dioxide removal</a> technologies, such as <a href="/wiki/Bio-energy_with_carbon_capture_and_storage" title="Bio-energy with carbon capture and storage">bio-energy with carbon capture and storage</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biochar" title="Biochar">biochar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_scrubber" title="Carbon dioxide scrubber">direct air capture</a> or <a href="/wiki/Enhanced_weathering" title="Enhanced weathering">enhanced weathering</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RoyalSociety_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-RoyalSociety-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Negative emissions is different from reducing emissions, as the former produces an outlet of carbon dioxide from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">Earth's atmosphere</a>, whereas the latter decreases the inlet of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere">carbon dioxide to the atmosphere</a>. Both have the same momentary net effect, but for achieving carbon dioxide concentration levels below present levels, such as <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation_scenarios#350_ppm" title="Climate change mitigation scenarios">350 ppm</a>, negative emissions are critical. Also for meeting higher concentration levels, negative emissions are increasingly considered to be crucial as they provide the only possibility to fill the gap between needed reductions to meet <a href="/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation" title="Climate change mitigation">mitigation targets</a> and <a href="/wiki/File:Global_Warming_Observed_CO2_Emissions_from_fossil_fuel_burning_vs_IPCC_scenarios.jpg" title="File:Global Warming Observed CO2 Emissions from fossil fuel burning vs IPCC scenarios.jpg">global emission trends</a>.</p>
<p>In the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OECD" title="OECD">OECD</a> Environmental Outlook to 2050</i> released at the <a href="/wiki/2011_United_Nations_Climate_Change_Conference" title="2011 United Nations Climate Change Conference">2011 United Nations Climate Change Conference</a>, the authors commented on the need for negative emissions, stating "Achieving lower concentration targets (450 ppm) depends significantly on the use of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BECCS" title="BECCS">BECCS</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OECD_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-OECD-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Carbon_dioxide_sinks_and_negative_carbon_dioxide_emissions">Carbon dioxide sinks and negative carbon dioxide emissions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Negative_carbon_dioxide_emission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Carbon dioxide sinks and negative carbon dioxide emissions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_sink" title="Carbon dioxide sink">carbon dioxide sink</a> such as a concentrated group of plants or any other primary producer that binds <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a> into biomass, such as within forests and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelp_bed" title="Kelp bed">kelp beds</a>, is not carbon negative, as sinks are not permanent. A carbon dioxide sink of this type moves <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">carbon</a>, in the form of carbon dioxide, from the atmosphere or hydrosphere to the biosphere. This process could be undone, for example by <a href="/wiki/Wildfire" title="Wildfire">wildfires</a> or <a href="/wiki/Logging" title="Logging">logging</a>.</p>
<p>Carbon dioxide sinks that store carbon dioxide in the Earth's crust by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geologic_sequestration_of_CO2" title="Geologic sequestration of CO2">injecting it into the subsurface</a>, or in the form of insoluble <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">carbonate</a> salts (<a href="/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage#Mineral_storage" title="Carbon capture and storage">mineral sequestration</a>), are considered carbon negative. This is because they are removing carbon from the atmosphere and <a href="/wiki/Carbon_sequestration" title="Carbon sequestration">sequestering</a> it indefinitely and presumably for a considerable duration (thousands to millions of years).</p>
