<p>A <b>power outage</b> (also called a <b>power cut</b>, a <b>power blackout</b>, <b>power failure</b> or a <b>blackout</b>) is a short- or long-term loss of the <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a> to an area.</p>
<p>There are many causes of power failures in an electricity network. Examples of these causes include faults at <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power stations</a>, damage to <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">electric transmission lines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_substation" title="Electrical substation">substations</a> or other parts of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electricity_distribution" title="Electricity distribution">distribution</a> system, a <a href="/wiki/Short_circuit" title="Short circuit">short circuit</a>, or the overloading of electricity mains.</p>
<p>Power failures are particularly critical at sites where the environment and public safety are at risk. Institutions such as <a href="/wiki/Hospital" title="Hospital">hospitals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sewage_treatment" title="Sewage treatment">sewage treatment</a> plants, <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">mines</a>, shelters and the like will usually have backup power sources such as <a href="/wiki/Emergency_power_system" title="Emergency power system">standby generators</a>, which will automatically start up when electrical power is lost. Other critical systems, such as <a href="/wiki/Telecommunication" title="Telecommunication">telecommunication</a>, are also required to have emergency power. The <a href="/wiki/Battery_room" title="Battery room">battery room</a> of a telephone exchange usually has arrays of <a href="/wiki/Lead%E2%80%93acid_battery" title="Lead–acid battery">lead–acid batteries</a> for backup and also a socket for connecting a generator during extended periods of outage.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_power_outage">Types of power outage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_outage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of power outage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Power outages are categorized into three different phenomena, relating to the duration and effect of the outage:</p>
<ul>
<li>A <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Permanent_fault&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Permanent fault (page does not exist)">permanent fault</a> is a massive loss of power typically caused by a fault on a power line. Power is automatically restored once the fault is cleared.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Brownout_(electricity)" title="Brownout (electricity)">brownout</a> is a drop in <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">voltage</a> in an electrical power supply. The term brownout comes from the dimming experienced by lighting when the voltage sags. Brownouts can cause poor performance of equipment or even incorrect operation.</li>
<li>A blackout is the total loss of power to an area and is the most severe form of power outage that can occur. Blackouts which result from or result in <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power stations</a> tripping are particularly difficult to recover from quickly. Outages may last from a few minutes to a few weeks depending on the nature of the blackout and the configuration of the electrical network.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protecting_the_power_system_from_outages">Protecting the power system from outages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_outage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Protecting the power system from outages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crossed_wires.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="1200" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Crossed_wires.JPG/220px-Crossed_wires.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Crossed_wires.JPG/330px-Crossed_wires.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Crossed_wires.JPG/440px-Crossed_wires.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crossed_wires.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Tree limbs creating a short circuit in electrical lines during a storm. This typically results in a power outage in the area supplied by these lines</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grid_(electricity)" title="Grid (electricity)">power supply networks</a>, the power generation and the electrical load (demand) must be very close to equal every second to avoid overloading of network components, which can severely damage them. <a href="/wiki/Protective_relay" title="Protective relay">Protective relays</a> and <a href="/wiki/Fuse_(electrical)" title="Fuse (electrical)">fuses</a> are used to automatically detect overloads and to disconnect circuits at risk of damage.</p>
<p>Under certain conditions, a network component shutting down can cause current fluctuations in neighboring segments of the network leading to a <a href="/wiki/Cascading_failure" title="Cascading failure">cascading failure</a> of a larger section of the network. This may range from a building, to a block, to an entire city, to an entire <a href="/wiki/Electrical_grid" title="Electrical grid">electrical grid</a>.</p>
<p>Modern power systems are designed to be resistant to this sort of cascading failure, but it may be unavoidable (see below). Moreover, since there is no short-term economic benefit to preventing rare large-scale failures, researchers have expressed concern that there is a tendency to erode the resilience of the network over time, which is only corrected after a major failure occurs. In a 2003 publication, Carreras and co-authors claimed that reducing the likelihood of small outages only increases the likelihood of larger ones.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2003_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref2003-1">[1]</a></sup> In that case, the short-term economic benefit of keeping the individual customer happy increases the likelihood of large-scale blackouts.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Protecting_computer_systems_from_power_outages">Protecting computer systems from power outages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_outage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Protecting computer systems from power outages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Computer systems and other electronic devices containing logic circuitry are susceptible to data loss or hardware damage that can be caused by the sudden loss of power. These can include data networking equipment, video projectors, alarm systems as well as computers. To protect against this, the use of an <a href="/wiki/Uninterruptible_power_supply" title="Uninterruptible power supply">uninterruptible power supply</a> or UPS can provide a constant flow of electricity if a primary power supply becomes unavailable for a short period of time. To protect against surges (events where voltages increase for a few seconds), which can damage hardware when power is restored, a special device called a <a href="/wiki/Surge_protector" title="Surge protector">surge protector</a> that absorbs the excess voltage can be used.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Restoring_power_after_a_wide-area_outage">Restoring power after a wide-area outage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_outage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Restoring power after a wide-area outage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Restoring power after a wide-area outage can be difficult, as power stations need to be brought back on-line. Normally, this is done with the help of power from the rest of the grid. In the total absence of grid power, a so-called <a href="/wiki/Black_start" title="Black start">black start</a> needs to be performed to <a href="/wiki/Bootstrapping#Electric_power_grid" title="Bootstrapping">bootstrap</a> the power grid into operation. The means of doing so will depend greatly on local circumstances and operational policies, but typically <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">transmission</a> utilities will establish localized 'power islands' which are then progressively coupled together. To maintain supply frequencies within tolerable limits during this process, demand must be reconnected at the same pace that generation is restored, requiring close coordination between power stations, transmission and distribution organizations.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Blackout_inevitability_and_electric_sustainability">Blackout inevitability and electric sustainability</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_outage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Blackout inevitability and electric sustainability">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Self-organized_criticality">Self-organized criticality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_outage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Self-organized criticality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>It has been argued on the basis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_power_outages" title="List of power outages">historical data</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2002a_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref2002a-2">[2]</a></sup> and computer modeling<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2002b_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref2002b-3">[3]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hh2014_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-hh2014-4">[4]</a></sup> that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_grid" title="Power grid">power grids</a> are <a href="/wiki/Self-organized_criticality" title="Self-organized criticality">self-organized critical systems</a>. These systems exhibit unavoidable<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2000_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref2000-5">[5]</a></sup> disturbances of all sizes, up to the size of the entire system. This phenomenon has been attributed to steadily increasing demand/load, the economics of running a power company, and the limits of modern engineering.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2007_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-ref2007-6">[6]</a></sup> While blackout frequency has been shown to be reduced by operating it further from its critical point, it generally isn’t economically feasible, causing providers to increase the average load over time or upgrade less often resulting in the grid moving itself closer to its critical point. Conversely, a system past the critical point will experience too many blackouts leading to system-wide upgrades moving it back below the critical point. The term critical point of the system is used here in the sense of statistical physics and nonlinear dynamics, representing the point where a system undergoes a phase transition; in this case the transition from a steady reliable grid with few cascading failures to a very sporadic unreliable grid with common cascading failures. Near the critical point the relationship between blackout frequency and size follows a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_law_distribution" title="Power law distribution">power law distribution</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hh2014_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-hh2014-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2007_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-ref2007-6">[6]</a></sup> Other leaders are dismissive of system theories that conclude that blackouts are inevitable, but do agree that the basic operation of the grid must be changed. The <a href="/wiki/Electric_Power_Research_Institute" title="Electric Power Research Institute">Electric Power Research Institute</a> champions the use of <a href="/wiki/Smart_grid" title="Smart grid">smart grid</a> features such as power control devices employing <a href="/wiki/Phasor_measurement_unit" title="Phasor measurement unit">advanced sensors</a> to coordinate the grid. Others advocate greater use of electronically controlled <a href="/wiki/High-voltage_direct_current" title="High-voltage direct current">High-voltage direct current</a> (HVDC) firebreaks to prevent disturbances from cascading across AC lines in a <a href="/wiki/Wide_area_synchronous_grid" title="Wide area synchronous grid">wide area grid</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cascading_failure" title="Cascading failure">Cascading failure</a> becomes much more common close to this critical point. The power law relationship is seen in both historical data and model systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2007_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-ref2007-6">[6]</a></sup> The practice of operating these systems much closer to their maximum capacity leads to magnified effects of random, unavoidable disturbances due to aging, weather, human interaction etc. While near the critical point, these failures have a greater effect on the surrounding components due to individual components carrying a larger load. This results in the larger load from the failing component having to be redistributed in larger quantities across the system, making it more likely for additional components not directly affected by the disturbance to fail, igniting costly and dangerous cascading failures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2007_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-ref2007-6">[6]</a></sup> These initial disturbances causing blackouts are all the more unexpected and unavoidable due to actions of the power suppliers to prevent obvious disturbances (cutting back trees, separating lines in windy areas, replacing aging components etc.). The complexity of most power grids often makes the initial cause of a blackout extremely hard to identify.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="OPA_model">OPA model</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_outage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: OPA model">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2002, researchers at <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> (ORNL), Power System Engineering Research Center of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wisconsin_University" title="Wisconsin University">Wisconsin University</a> (PSerc),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alaska_Fairbanks" title="University of Alaska Fairbanks">Alaska University</a> proposed a mathematical model for the behavior of electrical distribution systems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-opa_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-opa-9">[9]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-initial_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-initial-10">[10]</a></sup> This model has become known as the OPA model, a reference to the names of the authors' institutions. OPA is a cascading failure model. Other cascading failure models include Manchester, Hidden failure, CASCADE, and Branching.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Mitigation_of_power_outage_frequency">Mitigation of power outage frequency</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power_outage&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Mitigation of power outage frequency">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The effects of trying to mitigate cascading failures near the critical point in an economically feasible fashion are often shown to not be beneficial and often even detrimental. Four mitigation methods have been tested using the <i>OPA</i> blackout model:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2003_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-ref2003-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Increase critical number of failures causing cascading blackouts – Shown to decrease the frequency of smaller blackouts but increase that of larger blackouts.</li>
<li>Increase individual power line max load – Shown to increase the frequency of smaller blackouts and decrease that of larger blackouts.</li>
<li>Combination of increasing critical number and max load of lines – Shown to have no significant effect on either size of blackout. The resulting minor reduction in the frequency of blackouts is projected to not be worth the cost of the implementation.</li>
<li>Increase the excess power available to the grid – Shown to decrease the frequency of smaller blackouts but increase that of larger blackouts.</li>
</ul>
<p>In addition to the finding of each mitigation strategy having a cost-benefit relationship with regards to frequency of small and large blackouts, the total number of blackout events was not significantly reduced by any of the above-mentioned mitigation measures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref2003_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-ref2003-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>A complex network-based model to control large <a href="/wiki/Cascading_failure" title="Cascading failure">cascading failures</a> (blackouts) <i>using local information only</i> was proposed by A. E. Motter.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Motter2004_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Motter2004-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
