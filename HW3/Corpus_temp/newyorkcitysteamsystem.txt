<p>The <b>New York City steam system</b> is a <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> system which takes <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">steam</a> produced by steam generating stations and carries it under the streets of <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> to heat, cool, or supply power to high rise buildings and businesses. Some New York businesses and facilities also use the steam for cleaning and disinfection.</p>
<p>The <b>New York Steam Company</b> began providing service in <a href="/wiki/Lower_Manhattan" title="Lower Manhattan">lower Manhattan</a> on March 3, 1882.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> Today, <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_Edison" title="Consolidated Edison">Consolidated Edison</a> operates the largest commercial <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">steam system</a> in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The organization within Con Edison that is responsible for the system's operation is known as <b>Steam Operations</b>, providing steam service to over 1,700 customers and serving commercial and residential establishments in Manhattan from <a href="/wiki/Battery_Park" title="Battery Park">Battery Park</a> to <a href="/wiki/96th_Street_(Manhattan)" title="96th Street (Manhattan)">96th Street</a> uptown on the West side and 89th Street on the East side of Manhattan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Roughly 24 billion pounds (11,000,000 t) of steam flow through the system every year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conedsteam_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-conedsteam-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_the_environment">Effects on the environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_City_steam_system&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effects on the environment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">Cogeneration</a> and Heat Recovery Steam Generation (HRSG) significantly increase the efficiency of fuel usage and thereby reduces the <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">emission of pollutants</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/NOx" title="NOx">NOx</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide" title="Sulfur dioxide">sulfur dioxide</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>, and particulate matter, and reduce the city's <a href="/wiki/Carbon_footprint" title="Carbon footprint">carbon footprint</a>. Approximately 30% of the ConEd steam system’s installed capacity and 50% of the annual steam generated comes from cogeneration.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> Con Edison is promoting the use of steam for cooling in the summer months, something that can be accomplished with the installation of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absorption_chiller" title="Absorption chiller">absorption chillers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> Such <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigeneration" title="Trigeneration">trigeneration</a> systems result in additional energy and pollution savings, while reducing peak electrical loads.</p>
<p>Clouds of condensation can sometimes be seen rising from <a href="/wiki/Manhole" title="Manhole">manholes</a> in Manhattan through orange and white "chimneys". This can be caused by external water being boiled by contact with the steam pipes or by leaks in the steam system itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conedsteam_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-conedsteam-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>At least 12 steam pipe explosions have occurred in New York City since 1987.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The most recent incident was the <a href="/wiki/2007_New_York_City_steam_explosion" title="2007 New York City steam explosion">2007 New York City steam explosion</a>. Another major incident occurred on June 28, 1996, at the plant on East 75th Street.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
