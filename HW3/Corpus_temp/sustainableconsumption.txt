<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Sustainable consumption</b> (SC) shares a number of common features with and is closely linked to the terms <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_product_development" title="Sustainable product development">sustainable production</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a>. Sustainable consumption as part of <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_development" title="Sustainable development">sustainable development</a> is a prerequisite in the world-wide struggle against <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">sustainability</a> challenges such as <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">climate change</a>, famines or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_pollution" title="Environmental pollution">environmental pollution</a>.</p>
<p>Sustainable development as well as sustainable consumption rely on certain premises such as</p>
<ul>
<li>Wise use of resources, and minimisation of waste and pollution;</li>
<li>Use of renewable resources within their capacity for renewal;</li>
<li>Fuller product life-cycles; and</li>
<li>Intergenerational and intragenerational equity</li>
</ul>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Oslo_definition">The Oslo definition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_consumption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The Oslo definition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The definition proposed by the 1994 Oslo Symposium on Sustainable Consumption defines it as "the use of services and related products which respond to basic needs and bring a better quality of life while minimizing the use of natural resources and toxic materials as well as emissions of waste and pollutants over the life cycle of the service or product so as not to jeopardize the needs of future generations."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Strong_and_weak_sustainable_consumption">Strong and weak sustainable consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_consumption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Strong and weak sustainable consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In order to achieve sustainable consumption, two developments have to take place: it requires both an increase in the efficiency of consumption as well as a change in consumption patterns and reductions in consumption levels in industrialized countries. The first prerequisite is not sufficient on its own and can be named weak sustainable consumption. Here, technological improvements and eco-efficiency support a necessary reduction in resource consumption. Once this aim has been met, the second prerequisite, the change in patterns and reduction of levels of consumption is indispensable. Strong sustainable consumption approaches also pay attention to the social dimension of well-being and assess the need for changes based on a risk-averse perspective.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In order to achieve what can be termed strong sustainable consumption, changes in infrastructures as well as the choices customers have are required. In the political arena, weak sustainable consumption has been discussed whereas strong sustainable consumption is missing from all debates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>Taking into consideration those two approaches to sustainable consumption, it is evident that individual consumers play a key role. A huge problem is the existence of a so-called attitude-behaviour or values-action gap, i.e. many consumers are well aware of the importance of their consumption choices and care about environmental issues, however, most of them do not translate their concerns into their consumption patterns as the purchase-decision making process is highly complicated and relies on e.g. social, political and psychological factors. Young et al. identified a lack of time for research, high prices, a lack of information and the cognitive effort needed as the main barriers when it comes to green consumption choices.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutionalising_sustainable_consumption">Institutionalising sustainable consumption</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_consumption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Institutionalising sustainable consumption">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Developments_of_global_sustainable_consumption_governance">Developments of global sustainable consumption governance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_consumption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Developments of global sustainable consumption governance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1992 - At the United Nations Conference on Environment and Development (UNCED) the concept of sustainable consumption is established in chapter 4 of the Agenda 21.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></li>
<li>1994 - Sustainable Consumption Symposium in Oslo</li>
<li>1995 – SC was requested to be incorporated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ECOSOC" title="ECOSOC">UN Economic and Social Council</a> (ECOSOC) into the UN Guidelines on Consumer Protection.</li>
<li>1997 – A major report on SC was produced by the OECD <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></li>
<li>1998 – United Nations Environment Program (UNEP) starts a SC program and SC is discussed in the <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Report" title="Human Development Report">Human Development Report</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNDP" title="UNDP">UN Development Program</a> (UNDP).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>2002 – Creation of a ten-year program on sustainable consumption and production (SCP) in the Plan of Implementation at the <a href="/wiki/Earth_Summit_2002" title="Earth Summit 2002">World Summit on Sustainable Development</a> (WSSD) in Johannesburg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
<li>2003 - The “Marrakech Process” was developed by co-ordination of a series a of meetings and other “multi-stakeholder” processes by UNEP and UNDESA following the WSSD.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_consumption_initiatives">Sustainable consumption initiatives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_consumption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sustainable consumption initiatives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centre_on_Sustainable_Consumption_and_Production" title="Centre on Sustainable Consumption and Production">Centre on Sustainable Consumption and Production</a> is one of the leading independent authorities, that is exploring the dimensions of consumption and production. Another scientific network focusing on sustainable consumption research is the Sustainable Consumption Research and Action Initiative (SCORAI) with more than 800 members from mostly academia but also the non-profit sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> In 2007, <a href="/wiki/Tesco" title="Tesco">Tesco</a>, the largest supermarket in the United Kingdom, established the <a class="external text" href="http://www.sci.manchester.ac.uk/" rel="nofollow">Sustainable Consumption Institute</a> (SCI) with a £25 million grant to The University of Manchester.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_consumption_teaching">Sustainable consumption teaching</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sustainable_consumption&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Sustainable consumption teaching">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>A benchmark of teaching activities on the topic of sustainable consumption has been carried out by the scientific network <a class="external text" href="http://scorai.org/" rel="nofollow">Sustainable Consumption Research and Action Initiative</a> (SCORAI) in February 2016.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> The main results for the identified courses are as follows. Most of the courses are fairly recent (some in early 2000; most established between 2012-2014). They are generally not mandatory and are designed for 2-3 hours per week over one semester, granting 2-5 credits (<a href="/wiki/ECTS_grading_scale" title="ECTS grading scale">ECTS</a>). The courses are balanced in number between undergraduate and graduate courses, with a small fraction focussing on teacher training and policy/NGO training. Most courses are small in class size (15-25) with some ranging between 60-100 students. 22 courses have been identified with a consumer/consumption specific focus; 11 with a social change focus; 6 with a policy focus; 4 with a business/marketing/design focus; 3 with an ecological impact and lifestyle focus.</p>
