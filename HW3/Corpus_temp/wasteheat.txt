<p><b>Waste heat</b> is by necessity produced both by <a href="/wiki/Machine" title="Machine">machines</a> that do <a href="/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics)" title="Work (thermodynamics)">work</a> and in other processes that use <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a>, for example in a refrigerator warming the room air or a combustion engine releasing heat into the environment. The need for many systems to reject heat as a by-product of their operation is fundamental to the <a href="/wiki/Laws_of_thermodynamics" title="Laws of thermodynamics">laws of thermodynamics</a>. Waste heat has lower utility (or in thermodynamics lexicon a lower <a href="/wiki/Exergy" title="Exergy">exergy</a> or higher <a href="/wiki/Entropy" title="Entropy">entropy</a>) than the original energy source. Sources of waste heat include all manner of human activities, natural systems, and all organisms. Rejection of unneeded cold<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> (as from a heat pump) is also a form of waste heat (i.e. the medium has heat, but at a lower temperature than is considered warm).</p>
<p>Instead of being "wasted" by release into the ambient environment, sometimes waste heat (or cold) can be utilized by another process, or a portion of heat that would otherwise be wasted can be reused in the same process if make-up heat is added to the system (as with <a href="/wiki/Heat_recovery_ventilation" title="Heat recovery ventilation">heat recovery ventilation</a> in a building).</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy_storage" title="Thermal energy storage">Thermal energy storage</a>, which includes technologies both for short- and long-term retention of heat or cold, can create or improve the utility of waste heat (or cold). One example is waste heat from air conditioning machinery stored in a buffer tank to aid in night time heating. Another is <a href="/wiki/Seasonal_thermal_energy_storage" title="Seasonal thermal energy storage">seasonal thermal energy storage</a> (STES) at a foundry in Sweden. The heat is stored in the bedrock surrounding a cluster of heat exchanger equipped boreholes, and is used for space heating in an adjacent factory as needed, even months later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> An example of using STES to utilize natural waste heat is the <a href="/wiki/Drake_Landing_Solar_Community" title="Drake Landing Solar Community">Drake Landing Solar Community</a> in <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">Alberta</a>, Canada, which, by using a cluster of boreholes in bedrock for interseasonal heat storage, obtains 97 percent of its year-round heat from solar thermal collectors on the garage roofs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Another STES application is storing winter cold underground, for summer air conditioning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>On a biological scale, all organisms reject waste heat as part of their <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolic processes</a>, and will die if the ambient temperature is too high to allow this.</p>
<p>Anthropogenic waste heat is thought by some to contribute to the <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">urban heat island</a> effect. The biggest point sources of waste heat originate from machines (such as electrical generators or industrial processes, such as steel or glass production) and heat loss through building envelopes. The burning of transport fuels is a major contribution to waste heat.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Conversion_of_energy">Conversion of energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Conversion of energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">Second law of thermodynamics</a></div>
<p>Machines <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_conversion" title="Energy conversion">converting energy</a> contained in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuels" title="Fuels">fuels</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" title="Mechanical work">mechanical work</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electric_energy" title="Electric energy">electric energy</a> produce heat as a by-product.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sources">Sources</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sources">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the majority of energy applications, energy is required in multiple forms. These energy forms typically include some combination of: <a href="/wiki/HVAC" title="HVAC">heating, ventilation, and air conditioning</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_energy" title="Mechanical energy">mechanical energy</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a>. Often, these additional forms of energy are produced by a <a href="/wiki/Heat_engine" title="Heat engine">heat engine</a>, running on a source of high-temperature heat. A heat engine can never have perfect efficiency, according to the <a href="/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics" title="Second law of thermodynamics">second law of thermodynamics</a>, therefore a heat engine will always produce a surplus of low-temperature heat. This is commonly referred to as waste heat or "secondary heat", or "low-grade heat". This heat is useful for the majority of heating applications, however, it is sometimes not practical to transport heat energy over long distances, unlike electricity or fuel energy. The largest proportions of total waste heat are from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_stations" title="Power stations">power stations</a> and vehicle engines.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The largest single sources are power stations and industrial plants such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_refineries" title="Oil refineries">oil refineries</a> and <a href="/wiki/Steelmaking" title="Steelmaking">steelmaking</a> plants.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Power_generation">Power generation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Power generation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Electrical_efficiency" title="Electrical efficiency">electrical efficiency</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermal_power_plant" title="Thermal power plant">thermal power plants</a> is defined as the ratio between the input and output energy. It is typically only 30%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> The images show <a href="/wiki/Cooling_tower" title="Cooling tower">cooling towers</a> which allow power stations to maintain the low side of the temperature difference essential for conversion of heat differences to other forms of energy. Discarded or "Waste" heat that is lost to the environment may instead be used to advantage.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_processes">Industrial processes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Industrial processes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Industrial processes, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_refining" title="Oil refining">oil refining</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steel_making" title="Steel making">steel making</a> or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glass_making" title="Glass making">glass making</a> are major sources of waste heat.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electronics">Electronics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electronics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Although small in terms of power, the disposal of waste heat from <a href="/wiki/Integrated_circuit" title="Integrated circuit">microchips</a> and other electronic components, represents a significant engineering challenge. This necessitates the use of fans, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heatsinks" title="Heatsinks">heatsinks</a>, etc. to dispose of the heat.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biological">Biological</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biological">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Animals, including humans, create heat as a result of <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>. In warm conditions, this heat exceeds a level required for <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a> in <a href="/wiki/Warm-blooded" title="Warm-blooded">warm-blooded</a> animals, and is disposed of by various <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">thermoregulation</a> methods such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sweating" title="Sweating">sweating</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thermoregulation" title="Thermoregulation">panting</a>. Fiala <i>et al.</i> modelled human thermoregulation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disposal">Disposal</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Disposal">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Low temperature heat contains very little capacity to do work (<a href="/wiki/Exergy" title="Exergy">Exergy</a>), so the heat is qualified as waste heat and rejected to the environment. Economically most convenient is the rejection of such heat to water from a <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">sea</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">lake</a> or <a href="/wiki/River" title="River">river</a>. If sufficient cooling water is not available, the plant has to be equipped with a <a href="/wiki/Cooling_tower" title="Cooling tower">cooling tower</a> to reject the waste heat into the atmosphere. In some cases it is possible to use waste heat, for instance in heating homes by <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a>. However, by slowing the release of the waste heat, these systems always entail a reduction of efficiency for the primary user of the heat energy.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cogeneration_and_trigeneration">Cogeneration and trigeneration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cogeneration and trigeneration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Waste of the by-product heat is reduced if a <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> system is used, also known as a Combined Heat and Power (CHP) system. Limitations to the use of by-product heat arise primarily from the engineering cost/efficiency challenges in effectively utilizing small temperature differences to generate other forms of energy. Applications utilizing waste heat include <a href="/wiki/Swimming_pool" title="Swimming pool">swimming pool</a> heating and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paper_mills" title="Paper mills">paper mills</a>. In some cases cooling can also be produced by the use of <a href="/wiki/Absorption_refrigerator" title="Absorption refrigerator">absorption refrigerators</a> for example, in this case it's called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigeneration" title="Trigeneration">trigeneration</a> or CCHP (combined cooling, heat and power).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-heating">Pre-heating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pre-heating">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Waste heat can be forced to heat incoming fluids and objects before being highly heated. For instance outgoing water can give its waste heat to incoming water in a <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a> before heating in homes or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant">power plants</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electrification_of_waste_heat">Electrification of waste heat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Electrification of waste heat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There are many different approaches to transfer thermal energy to electricity, and the technologies to do so have existed for several decades. The <a href="/wiki/Organic_Rankine_cycle" title="Organic Rankine cycle">organic Rankine cycle</a>, offered by companies such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ormat" title="Ormat">Ormat</a>, is a very known approach, whereby an organic substance is used as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Working_medium" title="Working medium">working medium</a> instead of water. The benefit is that this process can utilize lower temperatures for the production of electricity than the regular water steam cycle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> An example of use of the steam <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Cyclone_Waste_Heat_Engine" title="Cyclone Waste Heat Engine">Cyclone Waste Heat Engine</a>. Another established approach is by using a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thermoelectric" title="Thermoelectric">thermoelectric</a>, such as those offered by <a href="/wiki/Alphabet_Energy" title="Alphabet Energy">Alphabet Energy</a>, where a change in temperature across a semiconductor material creates a voltage through a phenomenon known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seebeck_effect" title="Seebeck effect">Seebeck effect</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> A related approach is the use of <a href="/wiki/Thermogalvanic_cell" title="Thermogalvanic cell">thermogalvanic cells</a>, where a temperature difference gives rise to an electric current in an electrochemical cell.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Greenhouses">Greenhouses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Greenhouses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Waste heat (along with carbon dioxide from combustion) can be used to provide heat for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouses" title="Greenhouses">greenhouses</a> particularly in colder climates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Anthropogenic_heat">Anthropogenic heat</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Anthropogenic heat">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ANTHROPOGENIC_HEAT.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="393" data-file-width="358" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/ANTHROPOGENIC_HEAT.png/150px-ANTHROPOGENIC_HEAT.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/ANTHROPOGENIC_HEAT.png/225px-ANTHROPOGENIC_HEAT.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/ANTHROPOGENIC_HEAT.png/300px-ANTHROPOGENIC_HEAT.png 2x" width="150"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ANTHROPOGENIC_HEAT.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Anthropogenic heat</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Anthropogenic heat is heat generated by humans and human activity. The <a href="/wiki/American_Meteorological_Society" title="American Meteorological Society">American Meteorological Society</a> defines it as "Heat released to the atmosphere as a result of human activities, often involving combustion of fuels. Sources include industrial plants, space heating and cooling, human metabolism, and vehicle exhausts. In cities this source typically contributes 15–50 W/m<sup>2</sup> to the local heat balance, and several hundred W/m<sup>2</sup> in the center of large cities in cold climates and industrial areas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p>Estimates of <a href="/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment" title="Human impact on the environment">anthropogenic</a> heat generation can be made by totaling all the <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> used for heating and cooling, running appliances, transportation, and industrial processes, plus that directly emitted by human <a href="/wiki/Metabolism" title="Metabolism">metabolism</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Waste_heat&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Environmental impact">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Anthropogenic heat is a small influence on rural temperatures, and becomes more significant in dense <a href="/wiki/Urban_area" title="Urban area">urban</a> areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> It is one contributor to <a href="/wiki/Urban_heat_island" title="Urban heat island">urban heat islands</a>. Other human-caused effects (such as changes to <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">albedo</a>, or loss of evaporative cooling) that might contribute to urban heat islands are not considered to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropogenic_heat" title="Anthropogenic heat">anthropogenic heat</a> by this definition.</p>
<p>Anthropogenic heat is a much smaller contributor to <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> than are <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">greenhouse gases</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> In 2005, although anthropogenic waste heat flux was significantly high in certain urban areas (and can be high regionally. For example, waste heat flux was +0.39 and +0.68 W/m<sup>2</sup> for the continental United States and western Europe, respectively) globally it accounted for only 1% of the <a href="/wiki/Energy_flux" title="Energy flux">energy flux</a> created by anthropogenic greenhouse gases. Global forcing from waste heat was 0.028 W/m<sup>2</sup> in 2005. This statistic is predicted to rise as urban areas become more widespread.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flanner_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-flanner-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Although waste heat has been shown to have influence on regional climates,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climate_forcing" title="Climate forcing">climate forcing</a> from waste heat is not normally calculated in state-of-the-art global climate simulations. Equilibrium climate experiments show statistically significant continental-scale surface warming (0.4–0.9 °C) produced by one 2100 AHF scenario, but not by current or 2040 estimates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flanner_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-flanner-14">[14]</a></sup> Simple global-scale estimates with different growth rates of anthropogenic heat<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> that have been actualized recently<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> show noticeable contributions to global warming, in the following centuries. For example, a 2% p.a. growth rate of waste heat resulted in a 3 degree increase as a lower limit for the year 2300. Meanwhile, this has been confirmed by more refined model calculations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
