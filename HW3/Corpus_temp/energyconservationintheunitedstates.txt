<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>The United States is the second-largest single consumer of energy in the world. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Department_of_Energy" title="U.S. Department of Energy">U.S. Department of Energy</a> categorizes national energy use in four broad sectors: transportation, residential, commercial, and industrial.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>

<p>Energy usage in transportation and residential sectors (about half of U.S. energy consumption) is largely controlled by individual domestic consumers. Commercial and industrial energy expenditures are determined by businesses entities and other facility managers. National energy policy has a significant effect on energy usage across all four sectors.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">Transportation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_conservation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Transportation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The transportation sector includes all <a href="/wiki/Vehicle" title="Vehicle">vehicles</a> used for personal or freight transportation. Of the energy used in this sector, approximately 65% is consumed by <a href="/wiki/Gasoline" title="Gasoline">gasoline</a>-powered vehicles, primarily personally owned. <a href="/wiki/Diesel_fuel" title="Diesel fuel">Diesel</a>-powered transport (trains, merchant ships, heavy trucks, etc.) consumes about 20%, and air traffic consumes most of the remaining 15%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The two <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">oil supply crisis</a> of the 1970s spurred the creation, in 1975, of the federal <a href="/wiki/Corporate_Average_Fuel_Economy" title="Corporate Average Fuel Economy">Corporate Average Fuel Economy</a> (CAFE) program, which required auto manufacturers to meet progressively higher fleet fuel economy targets. The next decade saw dramatic improvements in fuel economy, mostly the result of reductions in vehicle size and weight which originated in the late 1970s, along with the transition to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Front_wheel_drive" title="Front wheel drive">front wheel drive</a>. These gains eroded somewhat after 1990 due to the growing popularity of <a href="/wiki/Sport_utility_vehicle" title="Sport utility vehicle">sport utility vehicles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">pickup trucks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Minivan" title="Minivan">minivans</a>, which fall under the more lenient "light truck" CAFE standard.</p>
<p>In addition to the CAFE program, the U.S. government has tried to encourage better vehicle efficiency through tax policy. Since 2002, taxpayers have been eligible for income tax credits for gas/electric hybrid vehicles. A "<a href="/wiki/Gas-guzzler" title="Gas-guzzler">gas-guzzler</a>" tax has been assessed on manufacturers since 1978 for cars with exceptionally poor fuel economy. While this tax remains in effect, it generates very little revenue as overall fuel economy has improved.</p>
<p>Another focus in gasoline conservation is reducing the number of miles driven. An estimated 40% of American automobile use is associated with daily <a href="/wiki/Commuting" title="Commuting">commuting</a>. Many urban areas offer <a href="/wiki/Subsidy" title="Subsidy">subsidized</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_transportation" title="Public transportation">public transportation</a> to reduce commuting traffic, and encourage <a href="/wiki/Carpool" title="Carpool">carpooling</a> by providing designated <a href="/wiki/High-occupancy_vehicle_lane" title="High-occupancy vehicle lane">high-occupancy vehicle lanes</a> and lower tolls for cars with multiple riders. In recent years <a href="/wiki/Telecommuting" title="Telecommuting">telecommuting</a> has also become a viable alternative to commuting for some jobs, but in 2003 only 3.5% of workers were telecommuters. Ironically, hundreds of thousands of American and European workers have been replaced by workers in <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> who telecommute from thousands of miles away.</p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fuel_economy-maximizing_behaviors" title="Fuel economy-maximizing behaviors">Fuel economy-maximizing behaviors</a> also help reduce fuel consumption. Among the most effective are moderate (as opposed to aggressive) driving, driving at lower speeds, using cruise control, and turning off a vehicle's engine at stops rather than idling. A vehicle's gas mileage decreases rapidly with increasing highway speeds, normally above 55 miles per hour (though the exact number varies by vehicle). This is because <a href="/wiki/Automotive_aerodynamics" title="Automotive aerodynamics">aerodynamic forces</a> are proportionally related to the square of an object's speed (when the speed is doubled, <a href="/wiki/Drag_(physics)" title="Drag (physics)">drag</a> quadruples). According to the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE), as a rule of thumb, each 5 mph (8.0 km/h) you drive over 60 mph (97 km/h) is similar to paying an additional $0.30 per gallon for gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> The exact speed at which a vehicle achieves its highest efficiency varies based on the vehicle's <a href="/wiki/Drag_coefficient" title="Drag coefficient">drag coefficient</a>, frontal area, surrounding air speed, and the efficiency and gearing of a vehicle's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Drive_train" title="Drive train">drive train</a> and transmission.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Residential_sector">Residential sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_conservation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Residential sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The residential sector refers to all private residences, including single-family homes, apartments, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manufactured_home" title="Manufactured home">manufactured homes</a> and dormitories. Energy use in this sector varies significantly across the country, due to regional climate differences and different regulation. On average, about half of the energy used in U.S. homes is expended on space conditioning (i.e. heating and cooling).</p>
<p>The efficiency of <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnaces</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_conditioner" title="Air conditioner">air conditioners</a> has increased steadily since the energy crises of the 1970s. The 1987 <a href="/wiki/National_Appliance_Energy_Conservation_Act" title="National Appliance Energy Conservation Act">National Appliance Energy Conservation Act</a> authorized the Department of Energy to set minimum efficiency standards for space conditioning equipment and other appliances each year, based on what is "technologically feasible and economically justified". Beyond these minimum standards, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a> (EPA) awards the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a> designation to appliances that exceed industry efficiency averages by an EPA-specified percentage.</p>
<p>Despite technological improvements, many American lifestyle changes have put higher demands on heating and cooling resources. The average size of homes built in the United States has increased significantly, from 1,500 sq ft (140 m<sup>2</sup>) in 1970 to 2,300 sq ft (210 m<sup>2</sup>) in 2005. The single-person household has become more common, as has central air conditioning: 23% of households had central air conditioning in 1978, that figure rose to 55% by 2001.</p>
<p>As furnace efficiency gets higher, appropriate matching of equipment size to distribution system capacity and building load becomes more critical to optimizing equipment ability to maximize efficient operation. Installing much lower output high efficiency replacement equipment offers opportunity for huge comfort and savings gains, but improving the <a href="/wiki/Building_envelope" title="Building envelope">building envelope</a> through air sealing and adding more <a href="/wiki/Building_insulation" title="Building insulation">insulation</a>, advanced windows, etc., should be explored concurrently or before replacement equipment design stage. The <a href="/wiki/Passive_house" title="Passive house">passive house</a> approach produces <a href="/wiki/Superinsulation" title="Superinsulation">superinsulated</a> buildings that approach <a href="/wiki/Zero-energy_building" title="Zero-energy building">zero net energy consumption</a>. Improving the building envelope can also be cheaper than replacing a furnace or air conditioner.</p>
<p>Even lower cost improvements include <a href="/wiki/Weatherization" title="Weatherization">weatherization</a>, which is frequently subsidized by utilities or state/federal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tax_credits" title="Tax credits">tax credits</a>, as are programmable <a href="/wiki/Thermostat" title="Thermostat">thermostats</a>. Consumers have also been urged to adopt a wider indoor temperature range (e.g. 65 °F (18 °C) in the winter, 80 °F (27 °C) in the summer).</p>
<p>One underutilized, but potentially very powerful means to reduce household energy consumption is to provide real-time feedback to homeowners so they can effectively alter their energy using behavior. Low cost <a href="/wiki/Home_energy_monitor" title="Home energy monitor">energy feedback displays</a>, such as the Energy Detective or Wattvision,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> have become available. A study of a similar device deployed in 500 homes in Ontario, Canada, by <a href="/wiki/Hydro_One" title="Hydro One">Hydro One</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> showed an average 6.5% drop in total electricity use when compared with a similarly sized control group.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Standby_power" title="Standby power">Standby power</a> used by consumer electronics and appliances while they are turned off accounts for an estimated 5 to 10% of household electricity consumption, adding an estimated $3 billion to annual energy costs in the USA. "In the average home, 75% of the electricity used to power home electronics is consumed while the products are turned off."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Home_energy_consumption_averages">Home energy consumption averages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_conservation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Home energy consumption averages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Home heating systems, 28.9%</li>
<li>Home cooling systems, 14.0%</li>
<li>Water heating, 12.9%</li>
<li>Lighting, 9.0%</li>
<li>Home electronics, 7.1%</li>
<li>Refrigerators and freezers, 5.9%</li>
<li>Clothing and dish washers, 4.5% (includes clothes dryers, does not include hot water)</li>
<li>Cooking, 3.7%</li>
<li>Computers, 2.2%</li>
<li>Other, 4.4% (includes small electrics, heating elements, motors, pool and hot tub heaters, outdoor grills, and natural gas outdoor lighting)</li>
<li>Non end-user energy expenditure, 5.4%,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Energy usage in some homes may vary widely from these averages. For example, milder regions such as the Southern U.S. and Pacific coast of the USA need far less energy for space conditioning than New York City or Chicago. On the other hand, air conditioning energy use can be quite high in hot-arid regions (Southwest) and hot-humid zones (Southeast) In milder climates such as San Diego, lighting energy may easily consume up to 40% of total energy. Certain appliances such as a waterbed, hot tub, or pre-1990 refrigerator use significant amounts of electricity. However, recent trends in home entertainment equipment can make a large difference in household energy use. For instance a 50" LCD television (average on-time= 6 hours a day) may draw 300 Watts less than a similarly sized plasma system. In most residences no single appliance dominates, and any conservation efforts must be directed to numerous areas in order to achieve substantial energy savings. However, Ground, Air and Water Source <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_Pump" title="Heat Pump">Heat Pump</a> systems, solar heating systems and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Evaporative_coolers&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Evaporative coolers (page does not exist)">evaporative coolers</a> are among the more energy efficient, environmentally clean, and cost-effective space conditioning and domestic hot water systems available (Environmental Protection Agency), and can achieve reductions in energy consumptions of up to 69%.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Best_building_practices">Best building practices</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_conservation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Best building practices">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Current best practices in building design, construction and <a href="/wiki/Retrofitting" title="Retrofitting">retrofitting</a> result in homes that are profoundly more energy conserving than average new homes. This includes insulation and energy-efficient windows and lighting.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Smart ways to construct homes such that minimal resources are used to cooling and heating the house in summer and winter respectively can significantly reduce energy costs.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_sector">Commercial sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_conservation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Commercial sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The commercial sector consists of retail stores, offices (business and government), restaurants, schools and other workplaces. Energy in this sector has the same basic end uses as the residential sector, in slightly different proportions. Space conditioning is again the single biggest consumption area, but it represents only about 30% of the energy use of commercial buildings. Lighting, at 25%, plays a much larger role than it does in the residential sector.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Lighting is also generally the most wasteful component of commercial use. A number of case studies indicate that more efficient lighting and elimination of <a href="/wiki/Over-illumination" title="Over-illumination">over-illumination</a> can reduce lighting energy by approximately fifty percent in many commercial buildings.</p>
<p>Commercial buildings can greatly increase energy efficiency by thoughtful design, with today's building stock being very poor examples of the potential of systematic (not expensive) energy efficient design (Steffy, 1997). Commercial buildings often have professional management, allowing centralized control and coordination of energy conservation efforts. As a result, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluorescent_lighting" title="Fluorescent lighting">fluorescent lighting</a> (about four times as efficient as incandescent) is the standard for most commercial space, although it may produce certain adverse health effects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pijnenburg_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pijnenburg-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Knez_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Knez-15">[15]</a></sup> Potential health concerns can be mitigated by using newer fixtures with electronic ballasts rather than older magnetic ballasts. As most buildings have consistent hours of operation, programmed thermostats and lighting controls are common. However, too many companies believe that merely having a computer controlled <a href="/wiki/Building_automation" title="Building automation">Building automation</a> system guarantees energy efficiency. As an example one large company in Northern California boasted that it was confident its state of the art system had optimized space heating. A more careful analysis by Lumina Technologies showed the system had been given programming instructions to maintain constant 24‑hour temperatures in the entire building complex. This instruction caused the injection of nighttime heat into vacant buildings when the daytime summer temperatures would often exceed 90 °F (32 °C). This mis-programming was costing the company over $130,000 per year in wasted energy (Lumina Technologies, 1997). Many corporations and governments also require the Energy Star rating for any new equipment purchased for their buildings.</p>
<p>Solar heat loading through standard window designs usually leads to high demand for air conditioning in summer months. An example of building design overcoming this excessive heat loading is the <a href="/wiki/Dakin_Building" title="Dakin Building">Dakin Building</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brisbane,_California" title="Brisbane, California">Brisbane, California</a>, where fenestration was designed to achieve an angle with respect to sun incidence to allow maximum reflection of solar heat; this design also assisted in reducing interior <a href="/wiki/Over-illumination" title="Over-illumination">over-illumination</a> to enhance worker efficiency and comfort.</p>
<p>Recent advances include use of occupancy sensors to turn off lights when spaces are unoccupied, and photosensors to dim or turn off electric lighting when natural light is available. In air conditioning systems, overall equipment efficiencies have increased as energy codes and consumer information have begun to emphasise year round performance rather than just efficiency ratings at maximum output. Controllers that automatically vary the speeds of fans, pumps, and compressors have radically improved part-load performance of those devices. For space or water heating, electric <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_pumps" title="Heat pumps">heat pumps</a> consume roughly half the energy required by electric resistance heaters. Natural gas heating efficiencies have improved through use of condensing furnaces and boilers, in which the water vapor in the flue gas is cooled to liquid form before it is discharged, allowing the heat of condensation to be used. In buildings where high levels of outside air are required, heat exchangers can capture heat from the exhaust air to preheat incoming supply air.</p>
<p>A company in Florida tackled the issue of both energy-conservation and enhancing its workplace environment by implementing a conveyor system that is 40-60% quieter than traditional systems, emitting a noise level of only 55-50 decibels, equivalent to a soft-rock radio station. Lighting was addressed by not only programming the lighting console so that isolated lights could be switched on and off in designated areas of the warehouse, but also by enhancing natural lighting through the use of skylights and a high-gloss floor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Industrial_sector">Industrial sector</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Energy_conservation_in_the_United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Industrial sector">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The industrial sector represents all production and processing of goods, including manufacturing, construction, farming, water management and mining.</p>
<p>Increasing costs have forced energy-intensive industries to make substantial efficiency improvements in the past 30 years. For example, the energy used to produce steel and paper products has been cut 40% in that time frame, while petroleum/aluminum refining and cement production have reduced their usage by about 25%. These reductions are largely the result of recycling waste material and the use of <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> equipment for electricity and heating.</p>
<p>Another example for efficiency improvements is the use of products made of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_temperature_insulation_wool" title="High temperature insulation wool">High temperature insulation wool</a> (HTIW) which enables predominantly industrial users to operate thermal treatment plants at temperatures between 800 and 1400 °C. In these high-temperature applications, the consumption of primary energy and the associated CO<sub>2</sub> emissions can be reduced by up to 50% compared with old fashioned industrial installations. The application of products made of High temperature insulation Wool is becoming increasingly important against the background of the dramatic rising cost of energy.</p>
<p>U.S. agriculture has doubled <a href="/wiki/Electrical_energy_efficiency_on_United_States_farms" title="Electrical energy efficiency on United States farms">farm energy efficiency</a> in the last 25 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>The energy required for delivery and treatment of fresh water often constitutes a significant percentage of a region's electricity and natural gas usage (an estimated 20% of California's total energy use is water-related).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> In light of this, some local governments have worked toward a more integrated approach to energy and <a href="/wiki/Water_conservation" title="Water conservation">water conservation</a> efforts.</p>
<p>To conserve energy, some industries have begun using <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_hot_water" title="Solar hot water">solar panels to heat their water</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Unlike the other sectors, total energy use in the industrial sector has declined in the last decade. While this is partly due to conservation efforts, it is also a reflection of the growing trend for U.S. companies to move manufacturing operations overseas.</p>

<p>Part B of Title III of the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Policy_and_Conservation_Act" title="Energy Policy and Conservation Act">Energy Policy and Conservation Act</a> established the <a href="/wiki/Energy_Policy_and_Conservation_Act#Energy_Conservation_Program" title="Energy Policy and Conservation Act">Energy Conservation Program for Consumer Products other than Automobiles</a>, which gives the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">Department of Energy</a> the "authority to develop, revise, and implement minimum energy conservation standards for appliances and equipment." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stat_authority_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-stat_authority-19">[19]</a></sup> As currently implemented, the Department of Energy enforces test procedures and minimum standards for more than 50 products covering residential, commercial and industrial, lighting, and plumbing applications.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-standards_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-standards-20">[20]</a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Energy_Policy_Act_of_2005" title="Energy Policy Act of 2005">Energy Policy Act of 2005</a> included incentives which provide a tax credit of 30% of the cost of the new item with a $500 aggregate limit; the program was initially set to expire at the end of 2007 but was extended to 2010 and the aggregate limit increased to $1,500 by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Energy_Improvement_and_Extension_Act_of_2008" title="Energy Improvement and Extension Act of 2008">Energy Improvement and Extension Act of 2008</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act_of_2009" title="The American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009">The American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009</a>, when it will expire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>The states and local areas (e.g., cities or counties) have various initiatives, and the U.S. Department of Energy funded a database known as DSIRE which provides information on these initiatives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The state of Maryland has set a target of reducing its electricity usage by 15% from 2008 to 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup></p>
<p>By <a href="/wiki/Executive_Order_13514" title="Executive Order 13514">Executive Order 13514</a>, U.S. President <a href="/wiki/Barack_Obama" title="Barack Obama">Barack Obama</a> mandated that by 2015, 15% of existing Federal buildings conform to new energy efficiency standards and 100% of all new <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero-Net-Energy_USA_Federal_Buildings" title="Zero-Net-Energy USA Federal Buildings">Federal buildings be Zero-Net-Energy</a> by 2030.</p>
