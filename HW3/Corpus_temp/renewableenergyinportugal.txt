<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Renewable energy in Portugal</b> was the source for 25.7% of energy consumption in 2013. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2014, 63% of Portugal's electricity needs were supplied by renewable sources. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> In 2015, 50.4% of power produced in Portugal came from renewable sources. The decrease from the year before was caused by lower productivity in the hydroelectric sector because of a dry year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2001, the Portuguese government launched a new <a href="/wiki/Energy_policy" title="Energy policy">energy policy</a> instrument – the E4 Programme (Energy Efficiency and Endogenous Energies), consisting of a set of multiple, diversified measures aimed at promoting a consistent, integrated approach to energy supply and demand. By promoting <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> and the use of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> (endogenous) sources, the programme sought to upgrade the competitiveness of the Portuguese economy and to modernize the country’s social fabric, while preserving the environment by reducing gas emissions, especially the <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">carbon dioxide</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iea-pvps.org_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-iea-pvps.org-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>While from 2002-2007 the main priorities were focused on the introduction of <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> (aiming at progressively replacing oil and coal in the energy balance) and liberalization of the <a href="/wiki/Energy_market" title="Energy market">energy market</a> (by opening this former state-owned sector to competition and private investment), the emphasis shifted for the next 5 years was on energy efficiency (supply and demand sides) and use of endogenous (renewable) energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iea-pvps.org_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-iea-pvps.org-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>During February 2016, an equivalent to 95% of electricity consumed in Portugal was produced by renewable sources such as biomass, hydropower, wind power and solar power. A total of 4139 GWh was produced by these sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> In May 2016, all of Portugal's electricity was produced renewably for a period of over four days, a landmark achievement for a modern European country.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hydro_power">Hydro power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Portugal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hydro power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BarragemLindoso1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1952" data-file-width="2592" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/BarragemLindoso1.jpg/220px-BarragemLindoso1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/BarragemLindoso1.jpg/330px-BarragemLindoso1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/BarragemLindoso1.jpg/440px-BarragemLindoso1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BarragemLindoso1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Alto Lindoso dam, serving the largest hydroelectric power station in the country</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The largest hydroelectric power station is at the Alto Lindoso dam, with a capacity of 630 MW. Portugal has about 100 <a href="/wiki/Small_hydro" title="Small hydro">small hydro</a> systems, with a capacity of 256 MW, which produce 815 GWh/year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_power">Wind power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Portugal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Wind power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Portugal" title="Wind power in Portugal">Wind power in Portugal</a></div>
<p>From March 2007 to December 2010, <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a> nameplate capacity grew from 1,874 to 3,937 MW in (excluding Madeira and Azores) Portugal. The major <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbine</a> manufacturers in the Portuguese market are <a href="/wiki/Enercon" title="Enercon">Enercon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vestas" title="Vestas">Vestas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gamesa_Corporaci%C3%B3n_Tecnol%C3%B3gica" title="Gamesa Corporación Tecnológica">Gamesa</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>The 240 MW <a href="/wiki/Alto_Minho_Wind_Farm" title="Alto Minho Wind Farm">Alto Minho Wind Farm</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Viana_do_Castelo_District" title="Viana do Castelo District">Viana do Castelo</a> district became fully operational in November, 2008.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yahoo_alto_minho_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo_alto_minho-9">[9]</a></sup> At the time of completion it was Europe's largest on-shore wind farm.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tremlett2008_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-tremlett2008-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>Other major <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_farms" title="Wind farms">wind farms</a> which are operating, or under construction, include: <a href="/wiki/Arada-Montemuro_Wind_Farm" title="Arada-Montemuro Wind Farm">Arada-Montemuro Wind Farm</a> (112 MW), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gardunha_Wind_Farm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gardunha Wind Farm (page does not exist)">Gardunha Wind Farm</a> (106 MW), <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pinhal_Interior_Wind_Farm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pinhal Interior Wind Farm (page does not exist)">Pinhal Interior Wind Farm</a> (144 MW) and <a href="/wiki/Ventominho_Wind_Farm" title="Ventominho Wind Farm">Ventominho Wind Farm</a> (240 MW).</p>
<p>Portugal combines wind and hydropower by using nighttime winds to pump water uphill and sending the water back through generators to produce power the next day; the so-called <a href="/wiki/Pumped-storage_hydroelectricity" title="Pumped-storage hydroelectricity">Pumped-storage hydroelectricity</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_power">Solar power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Portugal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Solar power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SolarPowerPlantSerpa.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/SolarPowerPlantSerpa.jpg/220px-SolarPowerPlantSerpa.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/SolarPowerPlantSerpa.jpg/330px-SolarPowerPlantSerpa.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/SolarPowerPlantSerpa.jpg/440px-SolarPowerPlantSerpa.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SolarPowerPlantSerpa.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Serpa solar power plant</div>
</div>
</div>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Solar_power_in_Portugal" title="Solar power in Portugal">Solar power in Portugal</a></div>
<p>A large <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic" title="Photovoltaic">photovoltaic</a> power project, the <a href="/wiki/Serpa_solar_power_plant" title="Serpa solar power plant">Serpa solar power plant</a>, was completed in Portugal, one of Europe's sunniest areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> The 11 megawatt plant covered 150 acres (0.61 km<sup>2</sup>) and employs 52,000 PV panels. The panels are raised 2 metres off the ground allowing grazing to continue. The plant provides enough energy for 8,000 homes and saves an estimated 30,000 tonnes of carbon dioxide emissions per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<p>Not far from there, <a href="/wiki/Moura_Photovoltaic_Power_Station" title="Moura Photovoltaic Power Station">Moura Photovoltaic Power Station</a> is under construction. With more than 376,000 solar modules, it will have an installed capacity of 62MWp when finished. The first stage of construction is scheduled for completion in 2008. A solar panel factory is also being built in the city of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moura_Municipality" title="Moura Municipality">Moura</a>, where there are plans to build a research lab.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geothermal_power">Geothermal power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Portugal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geothermal power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power_in_Portugal" title="Geothermal power in Portugal">Geothermal power in Portugal</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portugal</a>'s main investment for the use of this type of energy is in the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a>. Small scale use of this energy source began in the 1980s in <a href="/wiki/Chaves,_Portugal" title="Chaves, Portugal">Chaves</a> and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=S._Pedro_do_Sul&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="S. Pedro do Sul (page does not exist)">S. Pedro do Sul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Continental_Portugal" title="Continental Portugal">Continental Portugal</a> providing 3 MWt.</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">Azores</a> the use of <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_energy" title="Geothermal energy">Geothermal energy</a> is widespread, with production in 8 of the 9 Islands, collectively producing some 235.5 MWt. In 2003, 25% of the electricity consumed in <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Miguel_Island" title="São Miguel Island">São Miguel</a> was produced by geothermal energy.<a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.energiasrenovaveis.com/html/energias/geo_pt_actualidade.asp" rel="nofollow">[1]</a></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wave_power">Wave power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Portugal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Wave power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1524" data-file-width="3371" height="99" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG/220px-Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG/330px-Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG/440px-Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pelamis_machine_installed_at_the_Agucadoura_Wave_Park.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Agu%C3%A7adoura_Wave_Farm" title="Aguçadoura Wave Farm">Aguçadoura Wave Farm</a> was the world's first commercial <a href="/wiki/Wave_farm" title="Wave farm">wave farm</a> when it opened on 23 September 2008. It was located three miles (5 km) offshore near <a href="/wiki/P%C3%B3voa_de_Varzim" title="Póvoa de Varzim">Póvoa de Varzim</a> north of Porto. The farm used three <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelamis_wave_energy_converter" title="Pelamis wave energy converter">Pelamis</a> wave energy converters to convert the motion of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave" title="Ocean surface wave">ocean surface waves</a> into electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gov.pt_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-gov.pt-15">[15]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> The wave farm was shut down in November 2008, just over two months after the official opening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pelamis_Sinks_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-Pelamis_Sinks-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEO_Exits_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-CEO_Exits-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biogas">Biogas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Portugal&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Biogas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
