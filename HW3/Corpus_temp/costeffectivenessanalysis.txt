<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Cost-effectiveness analysis</b> (<b>CEA</b>) is a form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic" title="Economic">economic</a> <a href="/wiki/Financial_analysis" title="Financial analysis">analysis</a> that compares the relative costs and outcomes (effects) of different courses of action. Cost-effectiveness analysis is distinct from <a href="/wiki/Cost%E2%80%93benefit_analysis" title="Cost–benefit analysis">cost–benefit analysis</a>, which assigns a monetary value to the measure of effect.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEAvCBA_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-CEAvCBA-1">[1]</a></sup> Cost-effectiveness analysis is often used in the field of health services, where it may be inappropriate to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monetize" title="Monetize">monetize</a> health effect. Typically the CEA is expressed in terms of a ratio where the denominator is a gain in health from a measure (years of life, premature births averted, sight-years gained) and the numerator is the cost associated with the health gain.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> The most commonly used outcome measure is <a href="/wiki/Quality-adjusted_life_year" title="Quality-adjusted life year">quality-adjusted life years</a> (QALY).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CEAvCBA_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-CEAvCBA-1">[1]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cost-utility_analysis" title="Cost-utility analysis">Cost-utility analysis</a> is similar to cost-effectiveness analysis. Cost-effectiveness analyses are often visualized on a plane consisting of <a href="/wiki/Quadrant_(plane_geometry)" title="Quadrant (plane geometry)">four-quadrants</a>, the cost represented on the <i>x</i>–axis and the effectiveness on the <i>y</i>– axis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Cost-effectiveness analysis focuses on maximising the average level of an outcome, <a href="/wiki/Distributional_cost-effectiveness_analysis" title="Distributional cost-effectiveness analysis">distributional cost-effectiveness analysis</a> extends the core methods of CEA to incorporate concerns for the distribution of outcomes as well as their average level and make trade-offs between equity and efficiency, these more sophisticated methods are of particular interest when analysing interventions to tackle <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Health_inequality" title="Health inequality">health inequality</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="General_application">General application</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cost-effectiveness_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General application">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The concept of cost effectiveness is applied to the planning and management of many types of organized activity. It is widely used in many aspects of life. In the acquisition of military <a href="/wiki/Tank" title="Tank">tanks</a>, for example, competing designs are compared not only for purchase price, but also for such factors as their operating <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">radius</a>, top speed, <a href="/wiki/Rate_of_fire" title="Rate of fire">rate of fire</a>, armor protection, and caliber and armor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penetration_(weapons)" title="Penetration (weapons)">penetration</a> of their guns. If a tank's performance in these areas is equal or even slightly inferior to its competitor, but substantially less expensive and easier to produce, military planners may select it as more cost effective than the competitor. Conversely, if the difference in price is near zero, but the more costly competitor would convey an enormous battlefield advantage through special ammunition, <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">radar</a> <a href="/wiki/Fire-control_system" title="Fire-control system">fire control</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">laser</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Range_finding" title="Range finding">range finding</a>, enabling it to destroy enemy tanks accurately at extreme ranges, military planners may choose it instead—based on the same cost effectiveness principle.</p>
<p>Cost effectiveness analysis is also applied to many other areas of human activity, including the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economics_of_automobile_usage" title="Economics of automobile usage">economics of automobile usage</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="CEA_in_pharmacoeconomics">CEA in pharmacoeconomics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cost-effectiveness_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: CEA in pharmacoeconomics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In the context of <a href="/wiki/Pharmacoeconomics" title="Pharmacoeconomics">pharmacoeconomics</a>, the cost-effectiveness of a therapeutic or preventive intervention is the ratio of the cost of the intervention to a relevant measure of its effect. Cost refers to the resource expended for the intervention, usually measured in monetary terms such as <a href="/wiki/Dollar" title="Dollar">dollars</a> or <a href="/wiki/Pound_sterling" title="Pound sterling">pounds</a>. The measure of effects depends on the intervention being considered. Examples include the number of people cured of a disease, the mm Hg reduction in diastolic <a href="/wiki/Blood_pressure" title="Blood pressure">blood pressure</a> and the number of symptom-free days experienced by a patient. The selection of the appropriate effect measure should be based on clinical judgment in the context of the intervention being considered.</p>
<p>A special case of CEA is <a href="/wiki/Cost%E2%80%93utility_analysis" title="Cost–utility analysis">cost–utility analysis</a>, where the effects are measured in terms of years of full health lived, using a measure such as <a href="/wiki/Quality-adjusted_life_year" title="Quality-adjusted life year">quality-adjusted life years</a> or <a href="/wiki/Disability-adjusted_life_year" title="Disability-adjusted life year">disability-adjusted life years</a>. Cost-effectiveness is typically expressed as an <a href="/wiki/Incremental_cost-effectiveness_ratio" title="Incremental cost-effectiveness ratio">incremental cost-effectiveness ratio</a> (ICER), the ratio of change in costs to the change in effects. A complete compilation of cost-utility analyses in the peer reviewed medical literature is available from the <a href="/wiki/Cost-Effectiveness_Analysis_Registry" title="Cost-Effectiveness Analysis Registry">Cost-Effectiveness Analysis Registry</a> website.</p>
<p>A 1995 study of the cost-effectiveness of over 500 life-saving medical interventions found that the median cost per intervention was $42,000 per life-year saved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> A 2006 systematic review found that industry-funded studies often concluded with cost effective ratios below $20,000 per QALY and low quality studies and those conducted outside the US and EU were less likely to be below this threshold. While the two conclusions of this article may indicate that industry-funded ICER measures are lower methodological quality than those published by non-industry sources, there is also a possibility that, due to the nature of retrospective or other non-public work, publication bias may exist rather than methodology biases. There may be incentive for an organization not to develop or publish an analysis that does not demonstrate the value of their product. Additionally, peer reviewed journal articles should have a strong and defendable methodology, as that is the expectation of the peer-review process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="CEA_for_energy_efficiency_investments">CEA for energy efficiency investments</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cost-effectiveness_analysis&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: CEA for energy efficiency investments">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>CEA has been applied to energy efficiency investments in buildings to calculate the value of energy saved in $/kWh. The energy in such a calculation is virtual in the sense that it was never consumed but rather saved due to some energy efficiency investment being made. Such savings are sometimes called <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negawatt" title="Negawatt">negawatts</a></i>. The benefit of the CEA approach in energy systems is that it avoids the need to guess future energy prices for the purposes of the calculation, thus removing the major source of uncertainty in the appraisal of energy efficiency investments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
