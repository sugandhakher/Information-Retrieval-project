<p>The <b>U.S. Green Building Council</b> (<b>USGBC</b>), co-founded by current CEO Rick Fedrizzi, Mike Italiano, and David Gottfried in 1993, is a private 501(c)3, membership-based non-profit organization that promotes sustainability in buildings design, construction, and operation. USGBC is best known for its development of the <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design</a> (LEED) green building rating systems and its annual Greenbuild International Conference and Expo, the world’s largest conference and expo dedicated to green building. USGBC was one of eight national councils that helped found the <a href="/wiki/World_Green_Building_Council" title="World Green Building Council">World Green Building Council</a> (WorldGBC), of which USGBC CEO Rick Fedrizzi is the current chair, in 1999.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Through its partnership with the <a href="/wiki/Green_Business_Certification_Inc." title="Green Business Certification Inc.">Green Business Certification Inc.</a> (GBCI), USGBC offers a suite of LEED professional credentials that denote expertise in the field of green building. USGBC incentivizes LEED certification by awarding extra certification points to building projects completed with a LEED-certified professional on staff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design_.28LEED.29">Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Green_Building_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design (LEED)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Leadership_in_Energy_and_Environmental_Design" title="Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design">LEED Green Building Rating System</a> (LEED) is a program that provides third-party verification of green buildings. The LEED rating systems address both a wide variety of buildings types, including commercial buildings, homes, neighborhoods, retail, healthcare, and schools, as well as every phase of the building lifecycle including design, construction, operations and maintenance.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berkeley_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-3">[3]</a></sup> Projects may earn one of four levels of LEED certification (Certified, Silver, Gold or Platinum) by achieving a given number of point-based credits within the rating system.</p>
<p>Development of LEED began in 1993, spearheaded by <a href="/wiki/Natural_Resources_Defense_Council" title="Natural Resources Defense Council">Natural Resources Defense Council</a> (NRDC) senior scientist <a href="/wiki/Robert_K._Watson" title="Robert K. Watson">Robert K. Watson</a>. As founding chairman of the LEED Steering Committee, Watson led a broad-based consensus process which included non-profit organizations, government agencies, architects, engineers, developers, builders, product manufacturers and other industry leaders until 2006. The LEED initiative was supported by a strong USGBC Board of Directors, chaired by Steven Winter from 1999 to 2003, and very active staff, including Nigel Howard. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup>At that time, USGBC’s current Senior Vice President of LEED, Scot Horst, became chair of the LEED Steering Committee before joining USGBC staff. Early LEED committee members also included USGBC co-founder Mike Italiano, architects Bill Reed and Sandy Mendler, builder Gerard Heiber and Myron Kibbe and engineer Richard Bourne. As interest in LEED grew, in 1996, engineers Tom Paladino and Lynn Barker co-chaired the newly formed LEED technical committee.</p>
<p>Beginning with its launch in 2000, LEED has grown from one rating system for new construction to a comprehensive system of nine interrelated rating systems covering all aspects of the development and construction process.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-berkeley_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-berkeley-3">[3]</a></sup> Since its inception, LEED has grown from six volunteers on one committee to more than 200 volunteers on nearly 20 committees and nearly 200 professional staff.</p>
<p>USGBC was awarded the American Architectural Foundation’s Keystone Award in 2012. The <a href="/wiki/National_Building_Museum" title="National Building Museum">National Building Museum</a> presented the USGBC with its 2009 <a href="/wiki/Honor_Award" title="Honor Award">Honor Award</a> (themed "Visionaries in Sustainability"), citing the organization's "exceptional achievement in establishing and integrating green building standards" in its LEED systems as one of the reasons for selection.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Honor_Award_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Honor_Award-5">[5]</a></sup> The museum also awarded USGBC with its <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Henry_C._Turner_Prize" title="Henry C. Turner Prize">Henry C. Turner Prize</a> in 2005 for its leadership and innovation in the construction industry, specifically for LEED.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turner_Prize_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Turner_Prize-6">[6]</a></sup> To date, it is the only organization to have received two awards from the Building Museum.</p>
<p>LEED standards have been criticized for not actually creating energy efficient buildings. In 2013, The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Examiner" title="Washington Examiner">Washington Examiner</a> analyzed energy efficiency data of New York City buildings and found that LEED-certified buildings actually performed worse than buildings in general.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> An analysis by <a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">USA Today</a> found that building makers target LEED’s easiest points—those that don’t necessarily increase the energy efficiency of a building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legislation">Legislation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=U.S._Green_Building_Council&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legislation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On May 23, 2013, Rep. <a href="/wiki/David_McKinley" title="David McKinley">David McKinley</a> introduced the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Better_Buildings_Act_of_2014_(H.R._2126;_113th_Congress)" title="Better Buildings Act of 2014 (H.R. 2126; 113th Congress)">Better Buildings Act of 2014 (H.R. 2126; 113th Congress)</a> into the <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">United States House of Representatives</a>. The bill would amend federal law aimed at improving the <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> of commercial office buildings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbo2126_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbo2126-9">[9]</a></sup> The bill would also create a program called "Tenant Star" similar to the existing <a href="/wiki/Energy_Star" title="Energy Star">Energy Star</a> program.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgbc1_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-usgbc1-10">[10]</a></sup> The U.S. Green Building Council was involved in organizing and supporting this bill.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usgbc1_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-usgbc1-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>The U.S. Green Building Council also supported the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Streamlining_Energy_Efficiency_for_Schools_Act_of_2014_(H.R._4092;_113th_Congress)" title="Streamlining Energy Efficiency for Schools Act of 2014 (H.R. 4092; 113th Congress)">Streamlining Energy Efficiency for Schools Act of 2014 (H.R. 4092; 113th Congress)</a>, a bill that would require the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Energy" title="United States Department of Energy">United States Department of Energy</a> to establish a centralized clearinghouse to disseminate information on federal programs, incentives, and mechanisms for financing energy-efficient retrofits and upgrades at schools.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cbo4092_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-cbo4092-11">[11]</a></sup> The U.S. Green Building Council said that the bill "aims to make important improvements to existing federal policies."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGBCsupport_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-USGBCsupport-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
