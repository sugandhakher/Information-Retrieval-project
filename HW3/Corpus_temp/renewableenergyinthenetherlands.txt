<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>Despite the historic usage of wind power to drain water and grind grain, the <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">Netherlands</a> today lags behind most <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">EU</a> countries in the production of energy from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable sources</a>. The leading renewable sources in the country are <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind</a>, <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar</a> and both geothermal and aerothermal power (mostly from <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_heat_pump" title="Geothermal heat pump">ground source</a> and <a href="/wiki/Air_source_heat_pumps" title="Air source heat pumps">air source heat pumps</a>). In 2014, the Netherlands produced only 5.5% of its total energy from renewables, a small rise from 3.7% in 2010 and just 1% in 1990.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stat2010_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Stat2010-1">[1]</a></sup> Among the EU countries, only Malta and Luxembourg had lower percentages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>The low take up of renewable energy may be partially explained by the flat and often sub-sea level landscape and subsequent limits to <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a> resources as well as little natural <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geothermal" title="Geothermal">geothermal</a> potential,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> although hydro poor resource countries such as <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Denmark" title="Renewable energy in Denmark">Denmark</a> have still managed to make renewables the focus of their energy needs. In 2015, Dutch wind turbines had a total nameplate capacity of 3,431 MW.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Most of the tiny contribution made to electricity generation by hydroelectricity came from three power plants.</p>
<p>A large part of the renewable electricity sold in the Netherlands comes from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Norway" title="Renewable energy in Norway">Norway</a>, a country which generates almost all its electricity from hydropower plants. In the Netherlands, household consumers can choose to buy <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable electricity</a>. Since 2008, the amount of renewable energy used by household users has been increasing, rising from 38% in 2008 to 41% by 2009. and up to 44% by mid 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>One area in which the Netherlands is a relative leader is in the adoption of electric plug in vehicles. In 2015 <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicles_in_the_Netherlands" title="Plug-in electric vehicles in the Netherlands">PEV vehicles in the Netherlands</a> represented 9.74% of car sales,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TopSix2015_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-TopSix2015-5">[5]</a></sup> making it the world's second highest share after <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy_in_Norway" title="Renewable energy in Norway">Norway</a>. Electric vehicles are able to run on renewable electricity with zero emissions and have the potential to provide grid power storage facilities.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>An interesting source of heat recovery used in the Netherlands is sourced from freshly milked milk, or warm milk. However at 0.3% of total renewable energy production<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stat2010_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Stat2010-1">[1]</a></sup> (2010 figures) this source is not likely to accelerate energy transition in the country. Warm milk is still not mentioned in the EU Renewable Energy Directive, nor in international energy statistics and so is not included is gross final consumption figures. It does however provide Dutch farmers with plenty of hot water.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_trends_in_renewable_energy">Recent trends in renewable energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_Netherlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Recent trends in renewable energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Netherlands has a minimum target of 14% of renewable energy use by 2020. <p>Actual overall renewable energy use grew from 4.3% in 2009 to 5.5% by 2014. The minimum trajectory planned for 2013-2014 was 5.9% and for 2015-2016 7.6% of total energy use. The Netherlands is regarded as amongst the most likely countries to miss <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewable_Energy_Directive" title="Renewable Energy Directive">2020 national renewable energy targets</a> as outlined by the Renewable Energy Directive.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_power">Wind power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_Netherlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wind power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>2015 was a record year for new wind turbine installations totalling 586 MW bringing the totalled installed capacity to 3,431 MW by year end. 180 MW of the new installations were offshore.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup> The Dutch government has a target of 6,000 MW of onshore wind power by 2020 and 4,450 MW of offshore wind power by 2023.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-:0-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, the Netherlands had 1973 wind turbines including 98 in two offshore windfarms. Flevoland was the leading province for wind energy with Groningen second in capacity and production.The wind capacity installed at end 2010 will, in a normal wind year, produced 4.1% of electricity, when the equivalent value for <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Germany" title="Wind power in Germany">Germany</a> was 9.4% and <a href="/wiki/Wind_power_in_Portugal" title="Wind power in Portugal">Portugal</a> 14%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_power">Solar power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_Netherlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Solar power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By 2015 year end cumulative installed capacity of solar PV power reached a preliminary estimate of 1.57 GW with 450 MW added in that year alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biomass">Biomass</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_Netherlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Biomass">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Sources of <a href="/wiki/Biomass" title="Biomass">biomass</a> in the Netherlands include the <a href="/wiki/Waste-to-energy" title="Waste-to-energy">biogenic fraction of waste</a> that is burned in waste incineration plants. Waste wood is also collected for use in the Netherlands and other EU countries. Manure is used to produce <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pellet_fuel" title="Pellet fuel">wood pellets</a> are co-fired in electricity plants. 590 kton of pellets were imported, mostly from the United States of America and around 140 kton from Dutch sources contributing 12 to 13 PJ of primary energy to co-firing in energy plants in 2013-2014. Biofuels are produced in the Netherlands for both domestic and export markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-:1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rapeseed" title="Rapeseed">Rapeseed</a> and corn crops were hardly used in 2014 to produce biofuels in Holland with reasons cited being the high price of corn and resistance to using food crops for fuel production. Rapeseed is used for well over 1% of the imported biofuels (<a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a>) while corn is used for 11% (<a href="/wiki/BioEthanol_for_Sustainable_Transport" title="BioEthanol for Sustainable Transport">bioethanol</a>). Germany was the largest supplier of rape seed in 2014 (53%), followed by Romania (13%). For corn the largest supplier in 2014 was Ukraine (39%), followed by France (24%). Some maize fodder is fermented for biofuel production in Holland.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-:1-13">[13]</a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Warm_milk">Warm milk</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_Netherlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Warm milk">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2010, 740,000 dairy cows (about half of the country's total) provided 277 TJ of heat energy avoiding 18,000 tons of CO2 emissions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stat2010_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Stat2010-1">[1]</a></sup> According to industry sources for every litre of milk cooled, 0.7 litres of warm water is produced. Water pumped through the plate <a href="/wiki/Heat_exchanger" title="Heat exchanger">heat exchanger</a> reaches 50 °C to 55 °C. The energy recovered from 1000 litres of milk per day over a year generates heat equal to: 13,100 kWh of electrical energy, 1,900 litres of oil, 1,650 m³ of natural gas or 950 kg of propane gas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_change">Climate change</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_Netherlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate change">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to the ex Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Jan_Peter_Balkenende" title="Jan Peter Balkenende">Jan Peter Balkenende</a>, the Netherlands use annually 1-1.5 billion € (0.3% of national income) to protect against the risks of the sea level rise. Many areas are under sea level in the Netherlands and are protected by dam and dikes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> The Netherlands supported in 2010 raising the European Union emission restrictions from 20% to 30%; however, the Netherlands has only committed to reaching the minimum 14% goal for itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stat2010_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Stat2010-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_trends_1990-2011">Historical trends 1990-2011</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_the_Netherlands&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Historical trends 1990-2011">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The main sources of renewable energy up to 2011 were from biomass and wind power. Solar power was marginal with only 143 MW installed capacity by 2011. Energy from hydroelectric sources in 2011 was only marginally greater than that provided from heat extracted from warm milk.</p>
