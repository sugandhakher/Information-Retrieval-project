<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>The <b>annual fuel utilization efficiency</b> (<b>AFUE</b>; pronounced 'A'-'Few' or 'A'-'F'-'U'-'E') is a <a href="/wiki/Thermal_efficiency" title="Thermal efficiency">thermal efficiency</a> measure of combustion equipment like <a href="/wiki/Furnace" title="Furnace">furnaces</a>, <a href="/wiki/Boiler" title="Boiler">boilers</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_heater" title="Water heater">water heaters</a>. The AFUE differs from the true 'thermal efficiency' in that it is not a steady-state, peak measure of conversion efficiency, but instead attempts to represent the actual, season-long, average efficiency of that piece of equipment, including the operating transients.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It is a dimensionless ratio of useful energy output to energy input, expressed as a percentage. For example, a 90% AFUE for a gas furnace means it outputs 90 BTUs of useful heating for every 100 BTUs of natural gas input (where the rest may be wasted heat in the exhaust). A higher AFUE means higher efficiency.</p>
<p>The method for determining the AFUE for residential furnaces is the subject of <a href="/wiki/ASHRAE" title="ASHRAE">ASHRAE</a> Standard 103. A furnace with a thermal efficiency (η<sub>th</sub>) of 78% may yield an AFUE of only 64% or so, for example, under the standard's test conditions. When estimating annual or seasonal energy used by combustion devices, the AFUE is the better efficiency measure to use in the calculations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> But for an instantaneous fuel consumption rate, the thermal efficiency may be better.</p>
<p>Note that the theoretical limit for a conventional furnace's instantaneous efficiency is 100%, whereas a heat pump used for building heating may exceed 100%. For example, a <a href="/wiki/Coefficient_of_performance" title="Coefficient of performance">COP</a> of 1.5 is equivalent to 150%. Heat pumps are readily available for electric and gas sources.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> So from a theoretical perspective, in some use cases the name "efficiency" may be misleading.</p>
