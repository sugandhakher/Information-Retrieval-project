<p><b>John A. "Skip" Laitner</b> (born August 23, 1947) is an American-born economist, author and lecturer. He focuses on developing a more robust technology and behavioral characterization of <a href="/wiki/Efficient_energy_use" title="Efficient energy use">energy efficiency</a> resources for use in energy and climate economic policy models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Laitner currently serves as a Senior Fellow (formerly Director of Economic and Social Analysis) for the <a href="/wiki/American_Council_for_an_Energy-Efficient_Economy" title="American Council for an Energy-Efficient Economy">American Council for an Energy-Efficient Economy</a> (ACEEE).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> He now leads a team of consultants, the Economic and Human Dimensions Research Associates<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> based in Tucson, Arizona.</p>
<p>Author of more than 280 reports, journal articles, and book chapters, Laitner has more than 40 years of involvement in the environmental, energy and economic policy arenas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> His expertise includes benefit-cost assessments, behavioral assessments, resource costs and constraints, and the net employment and macroeconomic impacts of energy and climate policy scenarios. His most immediate research, building on the work of <a href="/wiki/Robert_Ayres_(scientist)" title="Robert Ayres (scientist)">Robert U. Ayres</a> and Benjamin Warr, is to examine the links between energy inefficiency and a productive economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In a new book chapter, Laitner provides a time series dataset that suggests the United States may be only 14 percent energy efficient, and that it is this level of inefficiency which may constrain the future development of a more robust economy.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_A._Laitner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><b>2006 to 2012</b>: Director of Economic and Social Analysis for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ACEEE" title="ACEEE">ACEEE</a>. In that capacity, he was responsible for a range of benefit-cost assessments of energy policies as they affect both climate and energy policies and the macroeconomy, including energy prices, net employment, and <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">gross domestic product</a> (GDP) impacts.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1997 to 2006</b>: Senior Economist for Technology Policy within U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="Environmental Protection Agency">Environmental Protection Agency</a>’s Office of Atmospheric Programs. Laitner was responsible for analysis and development of policy options for a variety of energy, climate change, and air pollution problems. This focused on the analysis and assessment of climate change policy options designed to provide further understanding of the macroeconomic benefits of energy efficiency and renewable energy at the national and regional level through the better linking of technology costing models and macroeconomic or general equilibrium models.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1985 to 1998</b>: Founder and principal of the consulting group, Economic Research Associates. Clients included a variety of municipalities and state government agencies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<p><b>1993 to 1995</b>: Senior Economist and Program Manager for Energy Efficiency and Economic Development, a program initiated by ACEEE. The program focused on the macroeconomic and employment impacts of energy efficiency initiatives.</p>
<p><b>1983 to 1986</b>: Chief of the Research Division for the Nebraska Energy Office.</p>
<p><b>1977 to 1983</b>: Co-founder of the Community Action Research Group, an economic and legislative consulting firm based in Ames, Iowa, serving as both the Executive Director and the Director of Research.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards_and_recognition">Awards and recognition</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_A._Laitner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Awards and recognition">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Citation of Excellence</i>: The 1997 paper co-authored with Dr. <a href="/wiki/Stephen_DeCanio" title="Stephen DeCanio">Stephen DeCanio</a>, "<a class="external text" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0040162597000024" rel="nofollow">Modeling technological change in energy demand forecasting: a generalized approach</a>," in <i>Technological Forecasting &amp; Social Change</i>, Vol. 55, No. 3, received the ANBAR Electronic Intelligence Citation of Excellence, Highest Quality Rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._EPA" title="U.S. EPA">U.S. EPA</a> Gold Medal Award</i>: The U.S. EPA's highest honor for working with a team of economists that completed "complex and rigorous analysis of greenhouse gas mitigation options, leading to President Clinton's announcement of an aggressive climate change policy." March 1998.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<p><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined_Heat_and_Power" title="Combined Heat and Power">Combined Heat and Power</a> (CHP) Champion</i>: For policy and analytical work in support of development of combined heat and power (CHP) technologies over the last five years, the US Combined Heat and Power Association gave Laitner an award to acknowledge his contributions to the industry. September 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
