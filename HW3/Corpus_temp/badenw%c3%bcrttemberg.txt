<p><b>Baden-Württemberg</b> (<span class="nowrap"><span class="IPA nopopups"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_English" title="Help:IPA for English">/<span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="/ˈ/ primary stress follows">ˈ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɑː/ 'a' in 'father'">ɑː</span><span title="'d' in 'dye'">d</span><span title="/ən/ 'on' in 'button'">ən</span></span> <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted"><span title="'v' in 'vie'">v</span><span title="/ɜːr/ 'ir' in 'bird'">ɜːr</span><span title="'t' in 'tie'">t</span><span title="/əm/ 'm' in 'rhythm'">əm</span><span title="/ˌ/ secondary stress follows">ˌ</span><span title="'b' in 'buy'">b</span><span title="/ɜːr/ 'ir' in 'bird'">ɜːr</span><span title="'g' in 'guy'">ɡ</span></span>/</a></span></span>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> <small>German pronunciation:</small> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_German" title="Help:IPA for German">[ˌbaːdn̩ˈvʏʁtm̩bɛʁk]</a></span>; <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr"><i>Bade-Wurtemberg</i></span>) is a <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> located in the southwest, east of the Upper <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a>. It is Germany’s third largest state in terms of size and population, with an area of 36,410 square kilometres (14,060 sq mi) and 10.7 million inhabitants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Our_State_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Our_State-4">[4]</a></sup> The state capital and largest city is <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sobriquet" title="Sobriquet">sobriquet</a> <i>Ländle</i> ("small land" or "dear land" in the local dialect) is sometimes used as a synonym for Baden-Württemberg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/History_of_Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="History of Baden-Württemberg">History of Baden-Württemberg</a></div>
<p>Today's Baden-Württemberg is formed from the historical territories of <a href="/wiki/Baden" title="Baden">Baden</a>, Prussian <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Hohenzollern" title="Province of Hohenzollern">Hohenzollern</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Württemberg">Württemberg</a> part of <a href="/wiki/Swabia" title="Swabia">Swabia</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planet_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Planet-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 100 AD, The <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a> invaded and occupied Württemberg, constructing a <a href="/wiki/Limes" title="Limes">limes</a> (fortified boundary zone) along its northern borders. Over the course of the third century AD, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">Alamanni</a> forced the Romans to retreat beyond the <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> and <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> rivers. In 496 AD the <a href="/wiki/Alemanni" title="Alemanni">Alemanni</a> themselves succumbed to a <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Frankish</a> invasion led by <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">Clovis I</a>.</p>
<p>After World War II, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allies" title="Allies">Allied forces</a> established three federal states in the territory of modern-day Baden-Württemberg: <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg-Hohenzollern" title="Württemberg-Hohenzollern">Württemberg-Hohenzollern</a>, <a href="/wiki/South_Baden" title="South Baden">Baden</a>, and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg-Baden" title="Württemberg-Baden">Württemberg-Baden</a>. <a href="/wiki/South_Baden" title="South Baden">Baden</a> and <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg-Baden" title="Württemberg-Baden">Württemberg-Baden</a> were occupied by <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> respectively. In 1949, each state became a founding member of the <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">Federal Republic of Germany</a>, with Article 118 of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_constitution" title="German constitution">German constitution</a> providing an accession procedure. On 16 December 1951, <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg-Baden" title="Württemberg-Baden">Württemberg-Baden</a>, Württemberg-Hohenzollern and <a href="/wiki/Baden" title="Baden">Baden</a> voted in favor of a joint merger via <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">referendum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Our_State_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Our_State-4">[4]</a></sup> Baden-Württemberg officially became a <a href="/wiki/States_of_Germany" title="States of Germany">state</a> in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> on 25 April 1952.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Our_State_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-Our_State-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">Geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Baden-Württemberg shares borders with <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> (region of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alsace-Champagne-Ardenne-Lorraine" title="Alsace-Champagne-Ardenne-Lorraine">Alsace-Champagne-Ardenne-Lorraine</a>), <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">Switzerland</a> (cantons of <a href="/wiki/Basel-Landschaft" title="Basel-Landschaft">Basel-Landschaft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Basel-Stadt" title="Basel-Stadt">Basel-Stadt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Aargau" title="Aargau">Aargau</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Z%C3%BCrich" title="Canton of Zürich">Zürich</a>, <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Schaffhausen" title="Canton of Schaffhausen">Schaffhausen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Thurgau" title="Thurgau">Thurgau</a>), and the German states of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhineland_Palatinate" title="Rhineland Palatinate">Rhineland Palatinate</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hessen" title="Hessen">Hessen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Bavaria" title="Bavaria">Bavaria</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Our_State_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-Our_State-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Most of the major cities of Baden-Württemberg straddle the banks of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neckar_River" title="Neckar River">Neckar River</a>, which runs downstream (from southwest to the center, then northwest) through the state past <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BCbingen" title="Tübingen">Tübingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heilbronn" title="Heilbronn">Heilbronn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Rhine" title="Rhine">Rhine</a> (German: <span lang="de"><i>Rhein</i></span>) forms the western border as well as large portions of the southern border. The <a href="/wiki/Black_Forest" title="Black Forest">Black Forest</a> (Schwarzwald), the main mountain range of the state, rises east of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_Rhine_valley" title="Upper Rhine valley">Upper Rhine valley</a>. The high plateau of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swabian_Alb" title="Swabian Alb">Swabian Alb</a>, between the Neckar, the Black Forest, and the Danube, is an important European <a href="/wiki/Drainage_divide" title="Drainage divide">watershed</a>. Baden-Württemberg shares <a href="/wiki/Lake_Constance" title="Lake Constance">Lake Constance</a> (Bodensee, also known regionally as the <a href="/wiki/Swabia" title="Swabia">Swabian</a> Sea) with Switzerland (the border is the middle of the lake) and it shares the foothills of the <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">Alps</a> (known as the <a href="/wiki/Allg%C3%A4u" title="Allgäu">Allgäu</a>) with Bavaria and the Austrian <a href="/wiki/Vorarlberg" title="Vorarlberg">Vorarlberg</a> (but Baden-Württemberg does not border Austria over land).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> (<i>Donau</i>) river has its source in Baden-Württemberg near the town of <a href="/wiki/Donaueschingen" title="Donaueschingen">Donaueschingen</a>, in a place called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Furtwangen" title="Furtwangen">Furtwangen</a> in the Black Forest. <a href="/wiki/T%C3%A4felberg" title="Täfelberg">Täfelberg</a> is a mountain located in the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Black_Forest" title="Northern Black Forest">Northern Black Forest</a>iands 565.2 metres above <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">sea level</a>.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics">Politics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Politics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Politics of Baden-Württemberg">Politics of Baden-Württemberg</a></div>
<p>The state <a href="/wiki/Landtag_of_Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Landtag of Baden-Württemberg">parliament of Baden-Württemberg</a> is the <b>Landtag</b> (Eng. <a href="/wiki/Landtag#Modern_legislatures" title="Landtag">state assembly</a>).</p>
<p>The politics of Baden-Württemberg have traditionally been dominated by the conservative <a href="/wiki/Christian_Democratic_Union_of_Germany" title="Christian Democratic Union of Germany">Christian Democratic Union of Germany</a> (CDU), who until 2011 had led all but one government since the establishment of the state in 1952. In the <i>Landtag</i> elections held on 27 March 2011 voters replaced the Christian Democrats and centre-right <a href="/wiki/Free_Democratic_Party_(Germany)" title="Free Democratic Party (Germany)">Free Democrats</a> coalition by a <a href="/wiki/Alliance_%2790/The_Greens" title="Alliance '90/The Greens">Greens</a>-led alliance with the <a href="/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany" title="Social Democratic Party of Germany">Social Democrats</a> which secured a four-seat majority in the state parliament.</p>
<p>From 1992 to 2001, the <a href="/wiki/The_Republicans_(Germany)" title="The Republicans (Germany)">Republicans</a> party held seats in the <i>Landtag</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">Economy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Although Baden-Württemberg has relatively few natural resources compared to other regions of Germany,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Our_State_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-Our_State-4">[4]</a></sup> the state is among the most prosperous<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planet_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-Planet-6">[6]</a></sup> and wealthiest regions in Europe with a generally low unemployment rate historically. A number of well-known enterprises are headquartered in the state, for example <a href="/wiki/Daimler_AG" title="Daimler AG">Daimler AG</a>, <a href="/wiki/Porsche" title="Porsche">Porsche</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bosch_GmbH" title="Robert Bosch GmbH">Robert Bosch GmbH</a> (automobile industry), <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zeiss_AG" title="Carl Zeiss AG">Carl Zeiss AG</a> (optics), and <a href="/wiki/SAP_SE" title="SAP SE">SAP SE</a> (largest software enterprise in Europe) and <a href="/wiki/Heidelberger_Druckmaschinen" title="Heidelberger Druckmaschinen">Heidelberger Druckmaschinen</a> (precision mechanical engineering). In spite of this, Baden-Württemberg's economy is dominated by small and medium-sized enterprises. Although poor in workable natural resources (formerly lead, zinc, iron, silver, copper, and salts) and still rural in many areas, the region is heavily industrialised. In 2003, there were almost 8,800 manufacturing enterprises with more than 20 employees, but only 384 with more than 500. The latter category accounts for 43% of the 1.2 million persons employed in industry. The <i>Mittelstand</i> or mid-sized company is the backbone of the Baden-Württemberg economy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> Medium-sized businesses and a tradition of branching out into different industrial sectors have ensured specialization over a wide range. A fifth of the "old" Federal Republic's industrial gross value added is generated by Baden-Württemberg. Turnover for manufacturing in 2003 exceeded 240,000 million, 43% of which came from exports. The region depends to some extent on global economic developments, though the great adaptability of the region's economy has generally helped it through crises. Half of the employees in the manufacturing industry are in mechanical and electrical engineering and automobile construction. This is also where the largest enterprises are to be found. The importance of the precision mechanics industry also extends beyond the region's borders, as does that of the optical, clock making, toy, metallurgy and electronics industries. The textile industry, which formerly dominated much of the region, has now all but disappeared from Baden-Württemberg. Research and development (R&amp;D) is funded jointly by the State and industry. In 2001, more than a fifth of the 100,000 or so persons working in R&amp;D in Germany were located in Baden-Württemberg, most of them in the <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a> area.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup> Baden-Württemberg is also one of the <a href="/wiki/Four_Motors_for_Europe" title="Four Motors for Europe">Four Motors for Europe</a>.</p>
<p>A study performed in 2007 by the PR campaign "Initiative for New Social Market Economy" (German: <span lang="de"><i>Initiative Neue Soziale Marktwirtschaft</i></span> (INSM)) and the trade newspaper "Wirtschaftswoche" awarded Baden-Württemberg for being the "economically most successful and most dynamic state" among the 16 states.</p>

<p>Baden-Württemberg is a popular holiday destination. Main sights include the capital and biggest city, <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>, modern and historic at the same time, with its urban architecture and atmosphere (and famously, its inner city parks and historic <a href="/wiki/Wilhelma" title="Wilhelma">Wilhelma</a> zoo), its castles (such as <a href="/wiki/Castle_Solitude" title="Castle Solitude">Castle Solitude</a>), its (car and art) museums as well as a rich cultural programme (theatre, opera) and <a href="/wiki/Mineral_spring" title="Mineral spring">mineral spring</a> baths in Stuttgart-Bad Cannstatt (also the site of a Roman <a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">Castra</a>); it is the only major city in Germany with vineyards in an urban territory.</p>
<p>The residential (court) towns of <a href="/wiki/Ludwigsburg" title="Ludwigsburg">Ludwigsburg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe" title="Karlsruhe">Karlsruhe</a>, the spas and casino of luxurious <a href="/wiki/Baden-Baden" title="Baden-Baden">Baden-Baden</a>, the medieval architecture of <a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">Ulm</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ulm_M%C3%BCnster" title="Ulm Münster">Ulm Münster</a> is the tallest church in the world), the vibrant, young, but traditional university towns of <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BCbingen" title="Tübingen">Tübingen</a> with their old castles looking out above the river Neckar, are popular smaller towns. Sites of former monasteries such as the ones on <a href="/wiki/Reichenau_Island" title="Reichenau Island">Reichenau Island</a> and at <a href="/wiki/Maulbronn" title="Maulbronn">Maulbronn</a> (both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Sites" title="World Heritage Sites">World Heritage Sites</a>) as well as <a href="/wiki/Bebenhausen_Abbey" title="Bebenhausen Abbey">Bebenhausen Abbey</a> are to be found. Baden-Württemberg also boasts rich old <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_Imperial_Cities" title="Free Imperial Cities">Free Imperial Cities</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Biberach_an_der_Riss" title="Biberach an der Riss">Biberach</a>, <a href="/wiki/Esslingen_am_Neckar" title="Esslingen am Neckar">Esslingen am Neckar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Heilbronn" title="Heilbronn">Heilbronn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ravensburg" title="Ravensburg">Ravensburg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Reutlingen" title="Reutlingen">Reutlingen</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Schw%C3%A4bisch_Hall" title="Schwäbisch Hall">Schwäbisch Hall</a>, as well as the southernmost and sunniest city of Germany, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freiburg" title="Freiburg">Freiburg</a>, close to Alsace and Switzerland, being an ideal base for exploring the heights of the nearby <a href="/wiki/Black_Forest" title="Black Forest">Black Forest</a> (e.g., for <a href="/wiki/Skiing" title="Skiing">skiing</a> in winter or for hiking in summer) with its traditional villages and the surrounding wine country of the Rhine Valley of South Baden.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planet_6-2"><a href="#cite_note-Planet-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>The countryside of the lush Upper Neckar valley (where <a href="/wiki/Rottweil" title="Rottweil">Rottweil</a> is famous for its carnival (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swabian%E2%80%93Alemannic_Fastnacht" title="Swabian–Alemannic Fastnacht">Fastnacht</a>)) and the pristine <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">Danube</a> valley <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swabian_Alb" title="Swabian Alb">Swabian Alb</a> (with <a href="/wiki/Hohenzollern_Castle" title="Hohenzollern Castle">Hohenzollern Castle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sigmaringen_Castle" title="Sigmaringen Castle">Sigmaringen Castle</a>), as well as the largely pristine <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Swabian_Forest&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Swabian Forest (page does not exist)">Swabian Forest</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_Rhine_Valley" title="Upper Rhine Valley">Upper Rhine Valley</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Lake_Constance" title="Lake Constance">Lake Constance</a> (German: <span lang="de"><i>Bodensee</i></span>), where all kinds of water sports are popular, with the former Imperial, today border town of <a href="/wiki/Konstanz" title="Konstanz">Konstanz</a> (where the <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Constance" title="Council of Constance">Council of Constance</a> took place), the <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">Neolithic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">Bronze Age</a> village at <a href="/wiki/Unteruhldingen" title="Unteruhldingen">Unteruhldingen</a>, the flower island of <a href="/wiki/Mainau" title="Mainau">Mainau</a>, and the hometown of the <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin" title="Zeppelin">Zeppelin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friedrichshafen" title="Friedrichshafen">Friedrichshafen</a> a.o., are especially popular for outdoor activities in the summer months.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Planet_6-3"><a href="#cite_note-Planet-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In spring and autumn (April/May and September/October), beer festivals (fun fairs) take place at the <a href="/wiki/Cannstatter_Wasen" title="Cannstatter Wasen">Cannstatter Wasen</a> in Stuttgart. The <a href="/wiki/Cannstatter_Volksfest" title="Cannstatter Volksfest">Cannstatter Volksfest</a>, in the autumn, is the second largest such festival in the world after the Munich <a href="/wiki/Oktoberfest" title="Oktoberfest">Oktoberfest</a>. In late November and early December <a href="/wiki/Christmas_market" title="Christmas market">Christmas markets</a> are a tourist magnet in all major towns, with the largest being in Stuttgart during the three weeks prior to Christmas.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Bertha_Benz_Memorial_Route" title="Bertha Benz Memorial Route">Bertha Benz Memorial Route</a> is a 194 km signposted scenic route from <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a> via <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wiesloch" title="Wiesloch">Wiesloch</a> to <a href="/wiki/Pforzheim" title="Pforzheim">Pforzheim</a> and back, which follows the route of the world's first long-distance <a href="/wiki/Road_trip" title="Road trip">journey</a> by automobile which <a href="/wiki/Bertha_Benz" title="Bertha Benz">Bertha Benz</a> undertook in August 1888.</p>

<p>Baden-Württemberg is home to some of the oldest, most renowned, and prestigious universities in Germany, such as the universities of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Heidelberg" title="University of Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> (founded in 1386, the oldest university within the territory of modern Germany), <a href="/wiki/University_of_Freiburg" title="University of Freiburg">Freiburg</a> (founded in 1457), and <a href="/wiki/University_of_T%C3%BCbingen" title="University of Tübingen">Tübingen</a> (founded in 1477). It also contains three of the eleven German 'excellence universities' (Heidelberg, Tübingen, and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Konstanz" title="University of Konstanz">Konstanz</a> and formerly, Freiburg and <a href="/wiki/Karlsruhe_Institute_of_Technology" title="Karlsruhe Institute of Technology">Karlsruhe</a>).</p>
<p>Other university towns are <a href="/wiki/University_of_Mannheim" title="University of Mannheim">Mannheim</a> and <a href="/wiki/University_of_Ulm" title="University of Ulm">Ulm</a>. Furthermore, two universities are located in the state capital <a href="/wiki/Stuttgart" title="Stuttgart">Stuttgart</a>, the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hohenheim" title="University of Hohenheim">University of Hohenheim</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Stuttgart" title="University of Stuttgart">University of Stuttgart</a>. Ludwigsburg is home to the renowned national film school <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filmakademie_Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Filmakademie Baden-Württemberg">Filmakademie Baden-Württemberg</a> (Film Academy Baden-Wuerttemberg). The private <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=International_University_in_Germany&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="International University in Germany (page does not exist)">International University in Germany</a> was situated in <a href="/wiki/Bruchsal" title="Bruchsal">Bruchsal</a>, but closed in 2009. There is still another private university, located in Friedrichshafen, <a href="/wiki/Zeppelin_University" title="Zeppelin University">Zeppelin University</a>.</p>
<p>Furthermore, there are more than a dozen <a href="/wiki/Fachhochschule" title="Fachhochschule">Fachhochschulen</a>, i.e., universities of applied sciences, as well as <i>Pädagogische Hochschulen</i>, i.e., teacher training colleges, and other institutions of tertiary education in Baden-Württemberg. (a.o. in <a href="/wiki/Aalen" title="Aalen">Aalen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biberach_an_der_Riss" title="Biberach an der Riss">Biberach an der Riss</a>, Esslingen, Karlsruhe, Ludwigsburg, <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BCrtingen" title="Nürtingen">Nürtingen</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pforzheim" title="Pforzheim">Pforzheim</a>, Ravensburg-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weingarten_(W%C3%BCrttemberg)" title="Weingarten (Württemberg)">Weingarten</a>, Reutlingen, several in Stuttgart, Schwäbisch Hall). Pforzheim University is one of the oldest Fachhochschulen in Germany which is renowned and highly ranked for its Engineering and MBA programs.</p>
<p>The state has the highest density of academic institutions of any territorial state (i.e., excluding Berlin and Hamburg) in Germany.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">Dialects</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Dialects">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Alemannic_separatism" title="Alemannic separatism">Alemannic separatism</a></div>
<p>Two dialect groups of German are spoken in Baden-Württemberg in various variants: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alemannic_dialects" title="Alemannic dialects">Alemannic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Franconian_languages" title="Franconian languages">Franconian</a> dialects. In central and southern Württemberg, the Alemannic dialect of <a href="/wiki/Swabian_German" title="Swabian German">Swabian</a> is spoken (slightly differing even within the area, e.g., between <a href="/wiki/Upper_Swabia" title="Upper Swabia">Upper Swabia</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swabian_Alb" title="Swabian Alb">Swabian Alb</a>, and the central <a href="/wiki/Neckar" title="Neckar">Neckar</a> Valley of the Stuttgart region). In <a href="/wiki/South_Baden" title="South Baden">South Baden</a>, the local dialects are <a href="/wiki/Low_Alemannic_German" title="Low Alemannic German">Low Alemannic</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Alemannic" title="High Alemannic">High Alemannic</a> (i.e., variants of what is also <a href="/wiki/Swiss_German" title="Swiss German">Swiss German</a>). In the northern part of Baden, i.e., the former <i>Kurpfalz</i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electorate_of_the_Palatinate" title="Electorate of the Palatinate">Electorate of the Palatinate</a>) with the former capitals of <a href="/wiki/Heidelberg" title="Heidelberg">Heidelberg</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mannheim" title="Mannheim">Mannheim</a>, the idiom is <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhine_Franconian" title="Rhine Franconian">Rhine Franconian</a> (i.e., <a href="/wiki/Palatine_German_language" title="Palatine German language">Palatinate German</a>), while in the Northeast <a href="/wiki/East_Franconian_German" title="East Franconian German">East Franconian</a> is spoken.</p>
<p>The same or similar Alemannic dialects are also spoken in the neighbouring regions, especially in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swabia_(administrative_region)" title="Swabia (administrative region)">Bavarian Swabia</a>, Alsace (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alsatian_language" title="Alsatian language">Alsatian</a>), German-speaking Switzerland (Swiss German), and the Austrian <a href="/wiki/Vorarlberg" title="Vorarlberg">Vorarlberg</a>, while the other Franconian dialects range from the Netherlands over the <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">Rhineland</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorraine_(region)" title="Lorraine (region)">Lorraine</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Hesse" title="Hesse">Hesse</a> up to northern Bavaria <a href="/wiki/Franconia" title="Franconia">Franconia</a>.</p>
<p>A variant of the Alemannic German of Baden developed into the <a href="/wiki/Colonia_Tovar_dialect" title="Colonia Tovar dialect">Colonia Tovar dialect</a>, spoken by descendants of immigrants from Baden who went to <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">Venezuela</a> in 1843.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">Demographics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Baden-W%C3%BCrttemberg&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Demographics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The population of Baden-Württemberg is 10,486,660 (2014), of which 5,354,105 are female and 5,132,555 are male. In 2006, the birth rate of 8.61 per 1000 was almost equal to the death rate of 8.60 per 1000. 14.87 percent of the population was under the age of 15, whereas the proportion of people aged 65 and older was at 18.99 per cent (2008). The <a href="/wiki/Dependency_ratio" title="Dependency ratio">dependency ratio</a> - the ratio of people aged under 15 and over 64 in comparison to the working age population (aged 15–64) - was 512 per 1000 (2008).</p>
<p>Baden-Württemberg has long been a preferred destination of immigrants. As of 2013, almost 28% of its population had a migration background as defined by the Federal Statistical Office of Germany; this number clearly surpassed the German average of 21% and was higher than in any other German state with the exception of the city states of Hamburg and Bremen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup> As of 2014, 9,355,239 of the population held German citizenship, whereas 1,131,421 were <a href="/wiki/Alien_(law)" title="Alien (law)">foreign nationals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p>

<p>Northern and most of central Württemberg has been traditionally <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant" title="Protestant">protestant</a> (particularly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran" title="Lutheran">Lutheran</a>) since the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">Reformation</a> in 1534 (with its centre at the famous <a href="/wiki/T%C3%BCbinger_Stift" title="Tübinger Stift">Tübinger Stift</a>). The former <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electorate_of_the_Palatinate" title="Electorate of the Palatinate">Electorate of the Palatinate</a> (Northwestern Baden) with its capital Heidelberg was shaped by <a href="/wiki/Calvinism" title="Calvinism">Calvinism</a> before being integrated into Baden. <a href="/wiki/Upper_Swabia" title="Upper Swabia">Upper Swabia</a>, and the Upper Neckar Valley up to the bishop seat of <a href="/wiki/Rottenburg_am_Neckar" title="Rottenburg am Neckar">Rottenburg</a>, and Southern Baden (the Catholic archbishop has its seat in Freiburg) have traditionally been bastions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholicism" title="Roman Catholicism">Roman Catholicism</a>.</p>
