<p>In <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">geography</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">ecology</a>, <b>anthropization</b> is the conversion of open spaces, landscapes, and <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">natural environments</a> by human action.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Description">Description</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthropization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Anthropic erosion is the process of human action degrading <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">terrain</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">soil</a>.</p>
<p>An area may be classified as anthropized even though it looks natural, such as <a href="/wiki/Grassland" title="Grassland">grasslands</a> that have been <a href="/wiki/Deforestation" title="Deforestation">deforested</a> by humans. It can be difficult to determine how much a site has been anthropized in the case of <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">urbanization</a> because one must be able to estimate the state of the landscape before significant human action.</p>
