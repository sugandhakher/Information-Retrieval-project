<p><a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">Norway</a> is a heavy producer of <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a>, first of all due to good resources in <a href="/wiki/Hydropower" title="Hydropower">hydropower</a>. Over 99% of the electricity production in mainland Norway is covered by hydropower plants. The total production of electricity from hydropower plants amounted to 135.3 TWh in 2007<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> There is also a large potential in <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a>, <a href="/wiki/Offshore_wind_power" title="Offshore wind power">offshore wind power</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nvesea_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nvesea-2">[2]</a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Wave_power" title="Wave power">wave power</a>, as well as production of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bio-energy" title="Bio-energy">bio-energy</a> from wood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Norway has limited resources in solar energy, but is one of the world's largest producers of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_grade_silicon" title="Solar grade silicon">solar grade silicon</a> and silicon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_cells" title="Solar cells">solar cells</a>.</p>
<p>The system for <a href="/wiki/Guarantee_of_origin" title="Guarantee of origin">Guarantees of Origin</a> was implemented by the EU Renewable Energy <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Directive_2009/28/EC" title="Directive 2009/28/EC">Directive 2009/28/EC</a>. In 2010 the average electricity consumption mix of a Norwegian household was 36% renewable.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>As per the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Renewables_Directive" title="Renewables Directive">2009 Renewables Directive</a> (later added in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EEA_Agreement" title="EEA Agreement">EEA Agreement</a>), Norway has established a national goal for renewable energy - 67.5% of gross final consumption of energy supplied by renewable sources by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_power">Wind power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Wind power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vallersund_handelssted.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2592" data-file-width="3888" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Vallersund_handelssted.jpg/220px-Vallersund_handelssted.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Vallersund_handelssted.jpg/330px-Vallersund_handelssted.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Vallersund_handelssted.jpg/440px-Vallersund_handelssted.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vallersund_handelssted.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Wind turbine in <a href="/wiki/Bjugn" title="Bjugn">Bjugn</a></div>

<p>In 2012 Norway had a <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind power</a> electricity production of 1.6 GWh. The following year it approved spending 20 billion NOK to triple its wind power capacity of ca. 700 MW to more than 2 GW by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In August 2016 construction of the 1 GW <a href="/wiki/Fosen_Vind" title="Fosen Vind">Fosen Vind</a> project began.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Transport">Transport</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Norway&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transport">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote" role="note">See also: <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicles_in_Norway" title="Plug-in electric vehicles in Norway">Plug-in electric vehicles in Norway</a></div>
<p>In the transport sector the share of renewables has increased from 1.3% to 4% between 2005-2010, and currently Norway has one of the highest numbers of <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicles_in_Norway" title="Plug-in electric vehicles in Norway">electric cars</a> per capita in the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> The government's initial goal of 50,000 electric cars on Norwegian roads was reached on 20 April 2015, more than two years earlier than expected. By reaching a stock of 50,000 electric cars, the <a href="/wiki/Market_penetration" title="Market penetration">market penetration</a> of pure electric vehicles achieved 2% of all passenger cars registered in Norway.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EVs50K_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-EVs50K-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nor50K_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nor50K-9">[9]</a></sup> The segment's penetration passed 3% in December 2015.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NorwayNTP2016_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NorwayNTP2016-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ZSW2015_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-ZSW2015-11">[11]</a></sup> With about 90,000 pure electric vehicles registered by mid-September 2016, the all-electric segment achieved a market penetration of 3.5% of all light-duty vehicles on Norway's roads.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norsk092016_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norsk092016-12">[12]</a></sup></p>

The <a href="/wiki/Electric_car" title="Electric car">all-electric</a> <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Leaf" title="Nissan Leaf">Nissan Leaf</a> (left) and the <a href="/wiki/Tesla_Model_S" title="Tesla Model S">Tesla Model S</a> (right) were the two best selling <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_car" title="Plug-in electric car">plug-in electric cars</a> in Norway in 2014.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OFV2014_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-OFV2014-13">[13]</a></sup></div>

<p>The stock of light-duty <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicle" title="Plug-in electric vehicle">plug-in electric vehicles</a> registered in Norway passed the 100,000 unit milestone in April 2016, making the country the fourth largest plug-in market in the world after the U.S., China and Japan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norvege100K_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norvege100K-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norsk100K_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Norsk100K-15">[15]</a></sup> As of April 2016<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Norway&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, the Norwegian fleet of plug-in electric vehicles consist of about 81,500 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/All-electric_car" title="All-electric car">all-electric</a> passenger and light-duty vehicles, almost 17,100 <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_hybrid" title="Plug-in hybrid">plug-in hybrids</a>, and over 2,000 all-electric commercial vans. The total stock includes almost 12,000 used imported electric cars.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Norsk100K_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Norsk100K-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>In February 2016, the government opened for public discussion until 1 July 2016 the proposed National Transport Plan 2018-2029 (NTP). The plan explains that the transportation sector accounts for emissions of about 16.5 million <a href="/wiki/Ton" title="Ton">tons</a> of CO<sub>2</sub>, which is about one third of the total <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions" title="Greenhouse gas emissions">greenhouse gas emissions</a> produced domestically in Norway. And road traffic, including both private cars and heavy vehicles, account for about 10 million tons of CO<sub>2</sub>. The NTP set policies and actions to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from private cars, trucks, ships, aircraft and construction equipment by about one half until 2030.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTP01_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-NTP01-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTP02_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-NTP02-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>In order to achieve this objective, among others, the NTP sets the goal that all new cars, buses and light commercial vehicles in 2025 should be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_emission_vehicles" title="Zero emission vehicles">zero emission vehicles</a>, this is, all-electric and <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_vehicle" title="Hydrogen vehicle">hydrogen vehicles</a>. By 2030, heavy-duty vans, 75% of new long-distance buses, and 50% of new trucks must be zero emission vehicles. Also, by 2030, 40% of all ships in short sea shipping should be using biofuels or be low- or zero-emission ships. The proposed strategy states that until zero-emission vehicles take over, all internal combustion engine cars sold be plug-in hybrids, and wherever possible, biofuels must be used. Also, government agencies should as far as possible make use of biofuels, low- and zero-emission technologies in private and hired vehicles and vessels. The plan also calls to support the deployment of zero emission vehicles, but also for the reduction of the existing incentives, and proposes to invest more in public transport, walking and cycling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTP01_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-NTP01-16">[16]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTP02_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-NTP02-17">[17]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NTP03_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-NTP03-18">[18]</a></sup></p>
