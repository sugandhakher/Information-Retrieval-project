<p>The <b>Deutsche Gesellschaft für Internationale Zusammenarbeit (GIZ) GmbH</b> or just <b>GIZ</b> in short, is a <a href="/wiki/Gesellschaft_mit_beschr%C3%A4nkter_Haftung" title="Gesellschaft mit beschränkter Haftung">company</a> that specializes in international development. The German name of the company translates to English as "German Corporation for International Cooperation", however the German title of the organization purposefully has no official translation in the company's publications. GIZ is owned by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Federal_Government" title="German Federal Government">German Federal Government</a> and operates in more than 130 countries. Its headquarters are located in <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eschborn" title="Eschborn">Eschborn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Profile_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Profile-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>GIZ was established on 1 January 2011 through a merger of the three German organisations: <a href="/wiki/Deutscher_Entwicklungsdienst" title="Deutscher Entwicklungsdienst">Deutscher Entwicklungsdienst</a> (DED), Deutsche Gesellschaft für Technische Zusammenarbeit (GTZ), and <a href="/wiki/InWEnt" title="InWEnt">Internationale Weiterbildung und Entwicklung</a> (InWEnt).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>GIZ works in a variety of fields, namely: economic development and employment; government, democracy and poverty reduction; education, health and social security; environment and infrastructure; and agriculture, fisheries and food. GIZ works primarily with states, state agencies, and the private sector (and not normally with, for example, non-governmental organizations, universities or research organizations).</p>

<p>GIZ mainly operates on behalf of the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Ministry_for_Economic_Cooperation_and_Development" title="Federal Ministry for Economic Cooperation and Development">Federal Ministry for Economic Cooperation and Development</a> (BMZ). Further clients are other departments of the government, international donors like the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a>, <a href="/wiki/World_Bank" title="World Bank">World Bank</a> or the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a>, partner countries and the private sector. The cooperation with private enterprises is an emerging field, promoted under the name of sustainable development. The GIZ is set up with International Services (IS) and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_Private_Partnership" title="Public Private Partnership">Public Private Partnership</a> (PPP)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> in this area.</p>
<p>GIZ works on a public-benefit basis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Profile_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Profile-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Facts_and_figures">Facts and figures</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deutsche_Gesellschaft_f%C3%BCr_Internationale_Zusammenarbeit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Facts and figures">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With a turnover of <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">€</a> 2.1 billion in 2012, some €1.87 billion came from projects and programmes for public clients, such as the BMZ, other German federal ministries or the EU. Roughly 90 percent of GIZ's turnover was under contracts from the <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Germany" title="Politics of Germany">German Federal Government</a>. The GIZ made 18.7 percent (€230 million) in 2012 from contracts from other clients such as financing institutions or private sector companies. €956 million of its turnover have been channeled through to third-party-consultancies.</p>
<p>As of 2012, GIZ has about 16,000 employees working in more than 130 countries, including about 1,900 at its head offices in <a href="/wiki/Eschborn" title="Eschborn">Eschborn</a> (near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" title="Frankfurt am Main">Frankfurt am Main</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Bonn" title="Bonn">Bonn</a>. About 70 percent of its staff are local employees in the partner countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fields_of_activity">Fields of activity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Deutsche_Gesellschaft_f%C3%BCr_Internationale_Zusammenarbeit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fields of activity">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>GIZ's main activities in its partner countries are focusing on sustainable development and resource management:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Economic development and employment (including services such as vocational training, economic policy advice, financial systems development or private sector promotion)</li>
<li>Government, democracy and poverty reduction (including services and working fields such as development-oriented emergency aid, peace building and crisis prevention, governance, sustainable urban development or structural poverty reduction)</li>
<li>Education, health and social security (including working fields such as education, development-oriented drug control, promotion of children and young people or <a href="/wiki/HIV/AIDS" title="HIV/AIDS">HIV/AIDS</a> prevention)</li>
<li>Environment and infrastructure (including activities such as waste, energy and water management, <a href="/wiki/Sustainable_sanitation" title="Sustainable sanitation">sustainable sanitation</a>, natural resource management, transport and mobility, implementing international environmental regimes or environmental policy, climate change-related <a href="/wiki/Capacity_building" title="Capacity building">capacity building</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></li>
<li>Agriculture, fisheries and food (including services such as coastal zone management, land management, market-oriented farming and food systems, policy advice on agriculture and rural development or securing livelihoods in marginal rural areas)</li>
</ul>
<p>GIZ has been involved in the creation of various networks, associations and portals, and may carry out or support secretariat functions for some of these for a limited period of time. Examples of such networks and associations that have had some GIZ involvement include:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="/wiki/REN21" title="REN21">Renewable Energy Policy Network for the 21st Century (REN21)</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/BIOPAT_%E2%80%93_Patrons_for_Biodiversity" title="BIOPAT – Patrons for Biodiversity">BIOPAT</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Alumniportal_Deutschland" title="Alumniportal Deutschland">Alumniportal Germany</a></li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Sustainable_Sanitation_Alliance" title="Sustainable Sanitation Alliance">Sustainable Sanitation Alliance</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>GIZ works closely with the German government-owned development bank <a href="/wiki/KfW" title="KfW">KfW</a> which is based in Frankfurt. In this collaboration, GIZ has the role of what is being called "technical cooperation" by the German agencies, and KfW has the role of "financial cooperation".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>GIZ is currently represented in the SuRe® Stakeholder Council.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> SuRe® – The Standard for Sustainable and Resilient Infrastructure is a global voluntary standard which integrates key criteria of sustainability and resilience into infrastructure development and upgrade. SuRe® is developed by GIB Foundation and Natixis as part of a multi-stakeholder process and will be compliant with ISEAL guidelines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup></p>
