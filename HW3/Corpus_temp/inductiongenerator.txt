<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>An <b>induction generator</b> or <i>asynchronous generator</i> is a type of <a href="/wiki/Alternating_current" title="Alternating current">alternating current</a> (AC) <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">electrical generator</a> that uses the principles of <a href="/wiki/Induction_motor" title="Induction motor">induction motors</a> to produce power. Induction generators operate by mechanically turning their rotors faster than synchronous speed. A regular AC asynchronous motor usually can be used as a generator, without any internal modifications. Induction generators are useful in applications such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mini_hydro" title="Mini hydro">mini hydro</a> power plants, wind turbines, or in reducing high-pressure gas streams to lower pressure, because they can recover energy with relatively simple controls.</p>
<p>An induction generator usually draws its excitation power from an electrical grid; sometimes, however, they are self-excited by using phase-correcting capacitors. Because of this, induction generators cannot usually "<a href="/wiki/Black_start" title="Black start">black start</a>" a de-energized distribution system.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Principle_of_operation">Principle of operation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Induction_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Principle of operation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An induction generator produces electrical power when its rotor is turned faster than the <i>synchronous speed</i>. For a typical four-pole motor (two pairs of poles on stator) operating on a 60 Hz electrical grid, the synchronous speed is 1800 rotations per minute (rpm). The same four-pole motor operating on a 50 Hz grid will have a synchronous speed of 1500 RPM. The motor normally turns slightly slower than the synchronous speed; the difference between synchronous and operating speed is called "slip" and is usually expressed as per cent of the synchronous speed. For example, a motor operating at 1450 RPM that has a synchronous speed of 1500 RPM is running at a slip of +3.3%.</p>
<p>In normal motor operation, the stator flux rotation is faster than the rotor rotation. This causes the stator flux to induce rotor currents, which create a rotor flux with magnetic polarity opposite to stator. In this way, the rotor is dragged along behind stator flux, with the currents in the rotor induced at the slip frequency.</p>
<p>In generator operation, a <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">prime mover</a> (turbine or engine) drives the rotor above the synchronous speed (negative slip). The stator flux still induces currents in the rotor, but since the opposing rotor flux is now cutting the stator coils, an active current is produced in stator coils and the motor now operates as a generator, sending power back to the electrical grid.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Excitation">Excitation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Induction_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Excitation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>An induction machine requires externally supplied armature current; it cannot start on its own as a generator. Because the rotor field always lags behind the <a href="/wiki/Stator" title="Stator">stator</a> field, the induction machine always "consumes" reactive power, regardless of whether it is operating as a generator or a motor.</p>
<p>A source of excitation current for magnetizing flux (reactive power) for the stator is still required, to induce rotor current. This can be supplied from the electrical grid or, once it starts producing power, from the generator itself.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Active_power">Active power</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Induction_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Active power">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Active power delivered to the line is proportional to slip above the synchronous speed. Full rated power of the generator is reached at very small slip values (motor dependent, typically 3%). At synchronous speed of 1800 rpm, generator will produce no power. When the driving speed is increased to 1860 rpm (typical example), full output power is produced. If the prime mover is unable to produce enough power to fully drive the generator, speed will remain somewhere between 1800 and 1860 rpm range.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Required_capacitance">Required capacitance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Induction_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Required capacitance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A capacitor bank must supply reactive power to the motor when used in stand-alone mode. The reactive power supplied should be equal or greater than the reactive power that the machine normally draws when operating as a motor.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Torque_vs._slip">Torque vs. slip</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Induction_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Torque vs. slip">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The basic fundamental of induction generators is the conversion between mechanical energy to electrical energy. This requires an external torque applied to the rotor to turn it faster than the synchronous speed. However, indefinitely increasing torque doesn't lead to an indefinite increase in power generation. The rotating magnetic field torque excited from the armature works to counter the motion of the rotor and prevent over speed because of induced motion in the opposite direction. As the speed of the motor increases the counter torque reaches a max value of torque (breakdown torque) that it can operate until before the operating conditions become unstable. Ideally, induction generators work best in the stable region between the no-load condition and maximum torque region.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Maximum_pass-through_current">Maximum pass-through current</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Induction_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Maximum pass-through current">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In practice and without taking this notion into account, many users unsuccessfully apply the principles to the actual deployment.</p>
<p>It's not in popular belief; that in almost every case, under the same active grid voltage, the power that the generator produces is greater than the power it consumes when it is at the motor,fully loaded state; its rated power. Sometimes the differences are in multiple folds. Higher the power means higher the amperage.</p>
<p>For prolong operation, and implied in its guaranteed, each motor has its “maximum pass-through current”. This amperage value; the <a href="/wiki/Current_density" title="Current density">current density</a>; is derived from the maximum pass-through current property of the internal copper <a href="/wiki/Magnet_wire" title="Magnet wire">magnet wire</a> and the combined configuration of their connections. Without opening up the unit to examine the internal setting of the copper wires, a division of the wattage of its rated power by its rated voltage can give users some senses of how much that value is.</p>
<p>Therefore, claims of making a unit generates more power than its rated should get a closer examination.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Grid_and_stand-alone_connections">Grid and stand-alone connections</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Induction_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Grid and stand-alone connections">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Kondensatorerregter-asynchrongenerator-mit-last.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>

<p>In induction generators, the reactive power required to establish the air gap magnetic flux is provided by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_factor_correction_unit" title="Power factor correction unit">capacitor bank</a> connected to the machine in case of stand-alone system and in case of grid connection it draws reactive power from the grid to maintain its air gap flux. For a grid-connected system, frequency and voltage at the machine will be dictated by the electric grid, <u>since</u> it is very small compared to the whole system. For stand-alone systems, frequency and voltage are complex function of machine parameters, capacitance used for excitation, and load value and type.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_of_induction_generators.5B1.5D">Limitations of induction generators<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Induction_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Limitations of induction generators[1]">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An induction generator cannot generate reactive power. Actually it requires reactive power from supply line to furnish its excitation, since it has no means for establishing air gap flux with stator open circuited. Operation of an induction generator requires a synchronous machine, whether generator or motor, on the line to supply the induction generator with its needed excitation power. This requirement limits the use of induction generators to specialty applications.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_induction_generators">Use of induction generators</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Induction_generator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Use of induction generators">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Induction generators are often used in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_turbines" title="Wind turbines">wind turbines</a> and some <a href="/wiki/Micro_hydro" title="Micro hydro">micro hydro</a> installations due to their ability to produce useful power at varying rotor speeds. Induction generators are mechanically and electrically simpler than other generator types. They are also more rugged, requiring no brushes or <a href="/wiki/Commutator_(electric)" title="Commutator (electric)">commutators</a>.</p>
<p>Induction generators are particularly suitable for wind generating stations as in this case speed is always a variable factor. Unlike synchronous motors, induction generators are load-dependent and cannot be used alone for grid frequency control.</p>
