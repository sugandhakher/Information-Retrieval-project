<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Power-to-X</b> (also <b>P2X</b> and <b>P2Y</b>)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lund-etal-2015_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-lund-etal-2015-1">[1]</a></sup> has two meanings. First, power-to-X refers to a number of <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> <a href="/wiki/Energy_transformation" title="Energy transformation">conversion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Energy_storage" title="Energy storage">energy storage</a>, and reconversion pathways that utilize surplus <a href="/wiki/Electric_power" title="Electric power">electric power</a>, typically during periods where fluctuating <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> generation exceeds load.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acatech-2016_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-acatech-2016-2">[2]</a></sup> Second, power-to-X refers to conversion technologies that allow for the decoupling of power from the electricity sector for use in other sectors (such as transport or chemicals), possibly using power that has been provided by additional investments in generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acatech-2016_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-acatech-2016-2">[2]</a></sup> The term power-to-x is widely used in <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">Germany</a> and may have originated there.</p>
<p>The <b>X</b> in the terminology can refer to one of the following: power-to-<a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a>, power-to-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedstock" title="Feedstock">chemicals</a>, power-to-<a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>, <a href="/wiki/Power_to_gas" title="Power to gas">power-to-gas</a>, power-to-<a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">heat</a>, <a href="/wiki/Power_to_gas#Power_to_hydrogen" title="Power to gas">power-to-hydrogen</a>, power-to-<a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fuel" title="Synthetic fuel">liquid</a>, <a href="/wiki/Power_to_gas#Power_to_methane" title="Power to gas">power-to-methane</a>, power-to-mobility, power-to-power, and power-to-<a href="/wiki/Syngas" title="Syngas">syngas</a>.</p>
<p>Collectively power-to-X schemes which use surplus power fall under the heading of flexibility measures and are particularly useful in energy systems with high shares of renewable generation and/or with strong <a href="/wiki/Low-carbon_economy" title="Low-carbon economy">decarbonization</a> targets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lund-etal-2015_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-lund-etal-2015-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acatech-2016_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-acatech-2016-2">[2]</a></sup> A large number of pathways and technologies are encompassed by the term. In 2016 the German government funded a €30<span class="nowrap"> </span>million first-phase research project into power-to-X options.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rwth-2016_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-rwth-2016-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity_storage_concepts">Electricity storage concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power-to-X&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Electricity storage concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Surplus electric power can be converted to other forms of <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">energy</a> for storage and reconversion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sternberg-etal-2015_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-sternberg-etal-2015-4">[4]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agora-2014_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-agora-2014-5">[5]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sterner-etal-2014_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-sterner-etal-2014-6">[6]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ausfelder-etal-2016_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-ausfelder-etal-2016-7">[7]</a></sup> Direct current <a href="/wiki/Electrolysis" title="Electrolysis">electrolysis</a> can be used to produce <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">hydrogen</a> which can, in turn, be converted to <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">methane</a> (CH<sub>4</sub>) via <a href="/wiki/Methanation" title="Methanation">methanation</a>. These fuels can be stored and used to produce electricity again, hours to months later. Reconversion technologies include <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine#Industrial_gas_turbines_for_power_generation" title="Gas turbine">gas turbines</a>, <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">CCGT plant</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell" title="Fuel cell">fuel cells</a>. Power-to-power refers to the round-trip reconversion efficiency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sternberg-etal-2015_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-sternberg-etal-2015-4">[4]</a></sup> Electrolysis is expensive and power-to-gas processes need substantial full-load hours (say 30%) to be economic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acatech-2016_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-acatech-2016-2">[2]</a></sup> Grid-dedicated <a href="/wiki/Grid_energy_storage#Batteries" title="Grid energy storage">battery storage</a> is not normally considered a power-to-X concept.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Sector_coupling_concepts">Sector coupling concepts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Power-to-X&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sector coupling concepts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Hydrogen and methane can also be used as downstream <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuels</a>, feed into the <a href="/wiki/Natural_gas" title="Natural gas">natural gas</a> grid, or used to make or <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_fuel" title="Synthetic fuel">synthetic fuel</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-koenig-etal-2014_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-koenig-etal-2014-8">[8]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-foit-etal-2016_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-foit-etal-2016-9">[9]</a></sup> Alternatively they can be used as a chemical <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feedstock" title="Feedstock">feedstock</a>, as can <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">ammonia</a> (<a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">NH<sub>3</sub></a>).</p>
<p>Power-to-heat involves contributing to the heat sector, either by <a href="/wiki/Joule_heating" title="Joule heating">resistance heating</a> or via a <a href="/wiki/Heat_pump" title="Heat pump">heat pump</a>. Back-up immersion heating of both <a href="/wiki/Water_heating" title="Water heating">domestic hot water</a> and <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> offers a cheap way of using surplus renewable energy and will often displace carbon-intensive fossil fuels for the task.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-acatech-2016_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-acatech-2016-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Power-to-mobility refers to the charging of <a href="/wiki/Battery_(electricity)" title="Battery (electricity)">battery</a> <a href="/wiki/Electric_vehicle" title="Electric vehicle">electric vehicles</a> (EV). Given the expected uptake of EVs, dedicated dispatch will be required.</p>
