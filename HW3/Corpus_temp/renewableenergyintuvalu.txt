<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Renewable energy in Tuvalu</b> is a growing sector of the country's energy supply. <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu" title="Tuvalu">Tuvalu</a> has committed to becoming the first country to get 100% of its <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> from <a href="/wiki/Renewable_energy" title="Renewable energy">renewable energy</a> sources by 2020. This is considered possible because of the small size of the population of Tuvalu and its abundant solar energy resources due to its tropical location. It is somewhat complicated because Tuvalu consists of nine inhabited islands. The Tuvalu National Energy Policy (TNEP) was formulated in 2009, and the Energy Strategic Action Plan defines and directs current and future energy developments so that Tuvalu can achieve the ambitious target of <a href="/wiki/100%25_renewable_energy" title="100% renewable energy">100% renewable energy</a> for power generation by 2020.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTMN_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-FTMN-1">[1]</a></sup> The program is expected to cost 20 million US dollars and is supported by the e8, a group of 10 electric companies from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/G8" title="G8">G8</a> countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPP15_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-SPP15-2">[2]</a></sup> The Government of Tuvalu worked with the e8 group to develop the Tuvalu Solar Power Project, which is a 40 kW grid-connected solar system that is intended to provide about 5% of <a href="/wiki/Funafuti" title="Funafuti">Funafuti</a>’s peak demand, and 3% of the Tuvalu Electricity Corporation's annual household consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPP15_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-SPP15-2">[2]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tuvalu participates in the <a href="/wiki/Alliance_of_Small_Island_States" title="Alliance of Small Island States">Alliance of Small Island States</a> (AOSIS), which is a coalition of small island and low-lying coastal countries that have concerns about their vulnerability to the adverse effects of global climate change. Under the <a href="/wiki/Majuro_Declaration" title="Majuro Declaration">Majuro Declaration</a>, which was signed on 5 September 2013, Tuvalu has commitment to implement power generation of 100% renewable energy (between 2013 and 2020), which is proposed to be implemented using Solar PV (95% of demand) and biodiesel (5% of demand). The feasibility of wind power generation will be considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MD050913_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-MD050913-3">[3]</a></sup> In November 2015 Tuvalu committed to reduction of emissions of green-house gases from the electricity generation (power) sector to almost zero emissions by 2025.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNFCCC15_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-UNFCCC15-4">[4]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tuvalu.27s_carbon_footprint">Tuvalu's carbon footprint</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tuvalu's carbon footprint">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tuvalu's power has come from electricity generation facilities that use imported diesel brought in by ships. The Tuvalu Electricity Corporation (TEC) on the main island of <a href="/wiki/Funafuti" title="Funafuti">Funafuti</a> operates the large power station (2000 kW).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHK2_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-AHK2-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>Funafuti's power station comprises three 750kVA diesel generators with 11kV operating voltage, which was installed in 2007. Total power output is 1,800 kW. The old generators have remained offline (1920 kW) but are available as back-up to the main system. The cost of diesel is subsidised by approximately 40% of the annual fuel consumption through the Japan Non Project Grant Assistance (NPGA), although this subsidy may end, which will expose the true cost of diesel generation of electricity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTMN_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-FTMN-1">[1]</a></sup></p>
<p>Seven of the eight outer islands are powered by 48 - 80 kW each diesel generators with a total generating capacity per island averaging 176 kW, although Vaitupu generates 208 kW and Nukulaelae generates 144 kW. Niulakita operates individual DC home solar systems. In the other islands the diesel generators are run for 12–18 hours per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTMN_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-FTMN-1">[1]</a></sup> For the small power stations on the outlying islands, fuel has to be transferred to 200 liter (55 gallon) barrels and offloaded from the ships. A small project to power the inter-island telecommunications systems by photovoltaics began in 1979 but was mismanaged.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>A project installed hundreds of small household solar systems as well as solar powered medical refrigerators beginning in the early 1980s but poor training and management led to installation and maintenance problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-EC-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>Tuvalu, barely above sea level at any point, is concerned over <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">global warming</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">sea level rise</a> and see its use of renewable energy as a moral example for others whose influence is greater. <a href="/wiki/Kausea_Natano" title="Kausea Natano">Kausea Natano</a>, Tuvalu's minister for public utilities and industries in the <a href="/wiki/Telavi_Ministry" title="Telavi Ministry">Telavi Ministry</a> stated this as "We thank those who are helping Tuvalu reduce its carbon footprint as it will strengthen our voice in upcoming international negotiations. And we look forward to the day when our nation offers an example to all -- powered entirely by natural resources such as the sun and the wind."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tuvalu_Energy_Sector_Development_Project_.28ESDP.29">Tuvalu Energy Sector Development Project (ESDP)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tuvalu Energy Sector Development Project (ESDP)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2014 the Tuvalu Electricity Corporation (TEC) began implementing a Master Plan for Renewable Energy and Energy Efficiency (MPREEE) through the Tuvalu Energy Sector Development Project (ESDP), which builds on the Tuvalu National Energy Policy, 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TEC15_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-TEC15-9">[9]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commitment_under_the_Majuro_Declaration_2013">Commitment under the Majuro Declaration 2013</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Commitment under the Majuro Declaration 2013">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Sopoaga_Ministry" title="Sopoaga Ministry">Sopoaga Ministry</a> led by <a href="/wiki/Enele_Sopoaga" title="Enele Sopoaga">Enele Sopoaga</a> made a commitment under the <a href="/wiki/Majuro_Declaration" title="Majuro Declaration">Majuro Declaration</a>, which was signed on 5 September 2013, to implement power generation of 100% renewable energy (between 2013 and 2020). This commitment is proposed to be implemented using Solar PV (95% of demand) and biodiesel (5% of demand). The feasibility of wind power generation will be considered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MD050913_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-MD050913-3">[3]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_energy">Solar energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solar energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 2007, Tuvalu was getting 2% of its energy from solar,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SHRS_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-SHRS-10">[10]</a></sup> through 400 small systems managed by the Tuvalu Solar Electric Co-operative Society. These were installed beginning in 1984 and, in the late 1990s, 34% of families in the outer islands had a PV system (which generally powered 1-3 lights and perhaps a few hours a day of radio use).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EC_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-EC-7">[7]</a></sup> Each of the eight islands had a medical center with a PV-powered vaccine refrigerator and each island's solar technician had a larger PV system which ran a household refrigerator. Followup on the installations showed no deterioration of the PV panels but switches and light fixtures had suffered damage or failed from the salt air.</p>
<p>The implementation of the Tuvalu Solar Power Project in 2008-9, involved the installation of a 40 kW grid-connected solar system that is intended to provide about 5% of <a href="/wiki/Funafuti" title="Funafuti">Funafuti</a>’s peak demand, and 3% of TEC’s annual household consumption.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SPP15_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-SPP15-2">[2]</a></sup> The first large scale system in Tuvalu was a 40 kW <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Photovoltaic_panel" title="Photovoltaic panel">solar panel</a> installation on the roof of <a href="/wiki/Tuvalu_Sports_Ground" title="Tuvalu Sports Ground">Tuvalu Sports Ground</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSG2009_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-TSG2009-11">[11]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNNart_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-CNNart-12">[12]</a></sup> This grid-connected 40 kW solar system was established in 2008 by the E8 and Japan Government through Kansai Electric Company (Japan) and contributes 1% of electricity production on Funafuti.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FTMN_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-FTMN-1">[1]</a></sup> Future plans include expanding this plant to 60 kW.</p>
<p>A 46 kW solar installation with battery storage at the <a href="/wiki/Motufoua_Secondary_School" title="Motufoua Secondary School">Motufoua Secondary School</a> on <a href="/wiki/Vaitupu" title="Vaitupu">Vaitupu</a> island was brought online on 27 November 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> At the date of installation it was described as the largest diesel-solar photovoltaic (PV) hybrid electricity system in the South Pacific.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECO_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-ECO-14">[14]</a></sup> Prior to the installment of the system the residential school relied upon a diesel powered generator, which needed to be turned off during the night. The hybrid system systems saves thousands of dollars in diesel costs and provides the school with a 24-hour supply of energy, with up to 200 kW per day.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ECO_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-ECO-14">[14]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SMA_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-SMA-15">[15]</a></sup></p>
<p>Funding for further PV solar system grid-tied systems was announced in late 2011 for Funafuti, with the funding provided by the Pacific Environment Community (PEC) Fund.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup> In 2015 a New Zealand aid programme resulted in Solarcity and Infratec Renewables, two New Zealand companies, installing photovoltaic panels on government buildings on Funafuti. These PV panels are expected to generate 170 kW of electricity and are estimated to deliver 5 percent of the energy requirements for Funafuti and will reduce Tuvalu's dependence on diesel by up to 62,000 litres.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RNZI04_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-RNZI04-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
<p>A non-profit, Alofa Tuvalu, is promoting <a href="/wiki/Solar_water_heating" title="Solar water heating">solar water heating</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_oven" title="Solar oven">solar ovens</a> as well as investigating producing <a href="/wiki/Biogas" title="Biogas">biogas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biodiesel" title="Biodiesel">biodiesel</a> and ethanol.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2014 Tuvalu signed an agreement with MASDAR, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UAE" title="UAE">UAE</a> Government company, which will provided US$3 million in aid to help Tuvalu solarize the outer islands, so as to reduce reliance on fossil fuel for electricity generation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MAS_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-MAS-20">[20]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SS230114_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-SS230114-21">[21]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2014 <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> and the <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">European Union</a> agreed to provide finance to the Government of Tuvalu to install battery-backed solar photovoltaic (PV) systems on the outer islands.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TVNZ04_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-TVNZ04-22">[22]</a></sup> The 191kWp project will provide the islands with 24 hours-a-day electricity and allow Tuvalu to save up to 120,000 litres of diesel per year, which will amount to a reduction in spending on diesel of about AU$200,000 per year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JET280314_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-JET280314-23">[23]</a></sup> This project will result in the construction of four small scale solar-hybrid systems that are to be located on <a href="/wiki/Vaitupu" title="Vaitupu">Vaitupu</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nanumanga" title="Nanumanga">Nanumanga</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nanumea" title="Nanumea">Nanumea</a> and <a href="/wiki/Niutao" title="Niutao">Niutao</a>. From January to March 2015 Powersmart, a New Zealand company, implemented German solar power technology to build the new Vaitupu powerhouse;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSP23_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-TSP23-24">[24]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSP6_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-TSP6-25">[25]</a></sup> with the next solar-hybrid system being built on Nanumaga in September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSP11_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-TSP11-26">[26]</a></sup> The 2015 installation program continued with Nanumea in October and Niutao in November.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSP14_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-TSP14-27">[27]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wind_energy">Wind energy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Renewable_energy_in_Tuvalu&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wind energy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">Wind power</a> is also mentioned as a future electricity source.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> Tuvalu's commitment, as part of the <a href="/wiki/Majuro_Declaration" title="Majuro Declaration">Majuro Declaration</a>, is to implement power generation of 100% renewable energy (between 2013 and 2020). The feasibility of wind power generation will be considered as part of this commitment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MD050913_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-MD050913-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
