<p><b>Steam</b> is <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">water</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">gas</a> phase, which is formed when water <a href="/wiki/Boiling" title="Boiling">boils</a>. Steam is invisible; however, "steam" often refers to wet steam, the visible <a href="/wiki/Mist" title="Mist">mist</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aerosol" title="Aerosol">aerosol</a> of water droplets formed as this <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_vapour" title="Water vapour">water vapour</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Condenses" title="Condenses">condenses</a>. At lower pressures, such as in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_atmosphere" title="Upper atmosphere">upper atmosphere</a> or at the top of high mountains, water boils at a lower temperature than the nominal 100 °C (212 °F) at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure" title="Standard temperature and pressure">standard temperature and pressure</a>. If heated further it becomes <a href="/wiki/Superheated_steam" title="Superheated steam">superheated steam</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Enthalpy_of_vaporization" title="Enthalpy of vaporization">enthalpy of vaporization</a> is the energy required to turn water into the gaseous form when it increases in <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">volume</a> by 1,700 times at standard temperature and pressure; this change in volume can be converted into <a href="/wiki/Work_(physics)" title="Work (physics)">mechanical work</a> by <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam engines</a> such as reciprocating piston type engines and <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbines</a>, which are a sub-group of steam engines. Piston type steam engines played a central role to the <a href="/wiki/Industrial_Revolution" title="Industrial Revolution">Industrial Revolution</a> and modern steam turbines are used to generate more than 80% of the world's <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a>. If liquid water comes in contact with a very hot surface or depressurizes quickly below its <a href="/wiki/Vapor_pressure" title="Vapor pressure">vapor pressure</a>, it can create a <a href="/wiki/Steam_explosion" title="Steam explosion">steam explosion</a>. Steam explosions have been responsible for many foundry accidents, and may also have been responsible for much of the damage to the plant in the <a href="/wiki/Chernobyl_disaster" title="Chernobyl disaster">Chernobyl disaster</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_steam_and_conversions">Types of steam and conversions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of steam and conversions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Steam is traditionally created by heating a boiler via burning coal and other fuels, but it is also possible to create steam with solar energy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> Water vapor that includes water droplets is described as <i>wet steam</i>. As wet steam is heated further, the droplets evaporate, and at a high enough temperature (which depends on the pressure) all of the water evaporates and the system is in <a href="/wiki/Vapor%E2%80%93liquid_equilibrium" title="Vapor–liquid equilibrium">vapor–liquid equilibrium</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Superheated_steam" title="Superheated steam">Superheated steam</a> is steam at a temperature higher than its <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">boiling point</a> for the pressure, which only occurs where all liquid water has evaporated or has been removed from the system.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_table" title="Steam table">Steam tables</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> contain thermodynamic data for water/steam and are often used by engineers and scientists in design and operation of equipment where thermodynamic cycles involving steam are used. Additionally, thermodynamic <a href="/wiki/Phase_diagram" title="Phase diagram">phase diagrams</a> for water/steam, such as a temperature-entropy diagram or a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mollier_diagram" title="Mollier diagram">Mollier diagram</a> shown in this article, may be useful. Steam charts are also used for analysing thermodynamic cycles.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">Uses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Uses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural">Agricultural</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Agricultural">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">agriculture</a>, steam is used for <a href="/wiki/Soil_steam_sterilization" title="Soil steam sterilization">soil sterilization</a> to avoid the use of harmful chemical agents and increase soil health.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Domestic">Domestic</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Domestic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Steam's capacity to transfer heat is also used in the home: for cooking vegetables, steam cleaning of fabric, carpets and flooring, and for heating buildings. In each case, water is heated in a boiler, and the steam carries the energy to a target object. Steam is also used in ironing clothes to add enough humidity with the heat to take wrinkles out and put intentional creases into the clothing.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Electricity_generation_.28and_cogeneration.29">Electricity generation (and cogeneration)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electricity generation (and cogeneration)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>About 90% of all electricity is <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">generated</a> using steam as the <a href="/wiki/Working_fluid" title="Working fluid">working fluid</a>, nearly all by <a href="/wiki/Steam_turbine" title="Steam turbine">steam turbines</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wiser_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wiser-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
<p>In electric generation, steam is typically condensed at the end of its expansion cycle, and returned to the boiler for re-use. However, in <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a>, steam is piped into buildings through a <a href="/wiki/District_heating" title="District heating">district heating</a> system to provide heat energy after its use in the electric generation cycle. The world's biggest steam generation system is the <a href="/wiki/New_York_City_steam_system" title="New York City steam system">New York City steam system</a>, which pumps steam into 100,000 buildings in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> from seven cogeneration plants.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup></p>

<p>In other industrial applications steam is used for <a href="/wiki/Energy_storage" title="Energy storage">energy storage</a>, which is introduced and extracted by heat transfer, usually through pipes. Steam is a capacious reservoir for thermal energy because of water's high <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">heat of vaporization</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Fireless_locomotive#Steam" title="Fireless locomotive">Fireless steam locomotives</a> were <a href="/wiki/Steam_locomotive" title="Steam locomotive">steam locomotives</a> that operated from a supply of steam stored on board in a large tank resembling a conventional locomotive's boiler. This tank was filled by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Process_steam&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Process steam (page does not exist)">process steam</a>, as is available in many sorts of large factory, such as <a href="/wiki/Paper_mill" title="Paper mill">paper mills</a>. The locomotive's propulsion used pistons and connecting rods, as for a typical steam locomotive. These locomotives were mostly used in places where there was a risk of fire from a boiler's firebox, but were also used in factories that simply had a plentiful supply of steam to spare.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lifting_gas">Lifting gas</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lifting gas">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Owing to its low molecular mass, steam is an effective <a href="/wiki/Lifting_gas" title="Lifting gas">lifting gas</a>, providing approximately 60% as much lift as helium and twice as much as hot air. It is not flammable, unlike hydrogen, and is cheap and abundant, unlike helium. The required heat, however, leads to condensation problems and requires an insulated envelope. These factors have limited its use thus far to mostly demonstration projects.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">Steam engines</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steam_turbines" title="Steam turbines">steam turbines</a> use the expansion of steam to drive a <a href="/wiki/Piston" title="Piston">piston</a> or <a href="/wiki/Turbine" title="Turbine">turbine</a> to perform <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mechanical_work" title="Mechanical work">mechanical work</a>. The ability to return condensed steam as water-liquid to the boiler at high pressure with relatively little expenditure of pumping power is important. <a href="/wiki/Condensation" title="Condensation">Condensation</a> of steam to water often occurs at the low-pressure end of a steam turbine, since this maximizes the <a href="/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency" title="Energy conversion efficiency">energy efficiency</a>, but such wet-steam conditions must be limited to avoid excessive turbine blade erosion. Engineers use an idealised <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_cycle" title="Thermodynamic cycle">thermodynamic cycle</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Rankine_cycle" title="Rankine cycle">Rankine cycle</a>, to model the behavior of steam engines. Steam turbines are often used in the production of electricity.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sterilization">Sterilization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Sterilization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>An <a href="/wiki/Autoclave" title="Autoclave">autoclave</a>, which uses steam under pressure, is used in microbiology laboratories and similar environments for <a href="/wiki/Sterilization_(microbiology)" title="Sterilization (microbiology)">sterilization</a>.</p>
<p>Steam, especially dry (highly superheated) steam, may be used for antimicrobial cleaning even to the levels of sterilization. Steam is a non-toxic antimicrobial agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Steam_in_piping">Steam in piping</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Steam in piping">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Steam is used in <a href="/wiki/Piping" title="Piping">piping</a> for utility lines. It is also used in jacketing and tracing of piping to maintain the uniform temperature in pipelines and vessels.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wood_treatment">Wood treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Wood treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Steam is used in the process of wood bending, killing insects and increasing plasticity.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Concrete_treatment">Concrete treatment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Concrete treatment">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Steam is used to accentuate drying especially in prefabricates.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Care should be taken since concrete produces heat during hydration and additional heat from the steam could be detrimental to hardening reaction processes of the concrete.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cleaning">Cleaning</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Steam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Cleaning">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Used in cleaning of fibers and other materials, sometimes in preparation for painting. Steam is also useful in melting hardened grease and oil residues, so it is useful in cleaning kitchen floors and equipment and internal combustion engines and parts. Among the advantages of using steam versus a hot water spray are the facts that steam can operate at higher temperatures and it uses substantially less water per minute.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
