<p><b>Pearl Street Station</b> was the first central <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">power plant</a> in the United States. It was located at 255-257 <a href="/wiki/Pearl_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Pearl Street (Manhattan)">Pearl Street</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a> on a site measuring 50 by 100 feet (15 by 30 m),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Josephson_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Josephson-1">[1]</a></sup> just south of <a href="/wiki/Fulton_Street_(Manhattan)" title="Fulton Street (Manhattan)">Fulton Street</a> and fired by coal. It began with one <a href="/wiki/Direct_current" title="Direct current">direct current</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_generator" title="Electrical generator">generator</a>, and it started <a href="/wiki/Electricity_generation" title="Electricity generation">generating electricity</a> on September 4, 1882, serving an initial load of 400 lamps at 82 customers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> By 1884, Pearl Street Station was serving 508 customers with 10,164 lamps.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Josephson_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Josephson-1">[1]</a></sup> The station was built by the <a href="/wiki/Edison_Illuminating_Company" title="Edison Illuminating Company">Edison Illuminating Company</a>, which was headed by <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Edison" title="Thomas Edison">Thomas Edison</a>. The station was originally powered by custom-made Porter-Allen high-speed steam engines designed to provide 175 <a href="/wiki/Horsepower" title="Horsepower">horsepower</a> at 700 rpm,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> but these proved to be unreliable with their sensitive governors. They were removed and replaced with new engines from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armington_%26_Sims" title="Armington &amp; Sims">Armington &amp; Sims</a> that proved to be much more suitable for Edison's dynamos.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<p>Pearl Street Station not only holds the distinction of being the world's first central power plant, but it was also the world's first <a href="/wiki/Cogeneration" title="Cogeneration">cogeneration</a> plant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> While the steam engines provided grid electricity, Edison made use of the thermal byproduct by distributing steam to local manufacturers, and warming nearby buildings on the same Manhattan block.</p>
<p>The station burned down in 1890, destroying all but one <a href="/wiki/Dynamo" title="Dynamo">dynamo</a> that is now kept in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenfield_Village_Museum" title="Greenfield Village Museum">Greenfield Village Museum</a> in Dearborn, Michigan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 1929 the Edison Company constructed three scale working models of the station. When a button was pushed, a motor turned the engines, generators, and other equipment in the model. A set of lamps connected to labelled buttons identified the various areas of the building. Cut-outs in the side of the model building allowed examination of the boilers on the first level, reciprocating steam engines and dynamos on the reinforced second level, and the control and test gear on the third and fourth levels. The models were constructed to a scale of one-half inch to the foot and were 62 inches long, 34 inches high and 13 inches wide. The models still exist and are on display at the <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">Smithsonian Institution</a> <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_American_History" title="National Museum of American History">National Museum of American History</a> in Washington, at the <a href="/wiki/Consolidated_Edison" title="Consolidated Edison">Consolidated Edison</a> Learning Center in Long Island City, New York and at the <a href="/wiki/The_Henry_Ford" title="The Henry Ford">Henry Ford Museum</a> in Dearborn, Michigan. Up to 31 people worked on constructing the models which took about 6 months to complete.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup></p>
