<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p><b>Security of the person</b> is a basic entitlement guaranteed by the <i><a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">Universal Declaration of Human Rights</a></i>, adopted by the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">United Nations</a> in 1948. It is also a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_right" title="Human right">human right</a> explicitly mentioned and protected by the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Canada" title="Constitution of Canada">Constitution of Canada</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_South_Africa" title="Constitution of South Africa">Constitution of South Africa</a> and other laws around the world.</p>
<p>In general, the right to the security of one's person is associated with <a href="/wiki/Liberty" title="Liberty">liberty</a> and includes the right, if one is imprisoned unlawfully, to the remedy of <i><a href="/wiki/Habeas_corpus" title="Habeas corpus">habeas corpus</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Security of person can also be seen as an expansion of rights based on prohibitions of <a href="/wiki/Torture" title="Torture">torture</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cruel_and_unusual_punishment" title="Cruel and unusual punishment">cruel and unusual punishment</a>. Rights to security of person can guard against less lethal conduct, and can be used in regard to prisoners' rights.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Nations">United Nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Security_of_person&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: United Nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The right to security of the person is guaranteed by Article 3 of the <i>Universal Declaration of Human Rights</i>. In this article, it is combined with the <a href="/wiki/Right_to_life" title="Right to life">right to life</a> and liberty. In full, the article reads, "Everyone has the right to life, liberty and security of person."</p>
<p>The United Nations treaty, the <a href="/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Civil_and_Political_Rights" title="International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights">International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights</a> (1966), also recognizes a right to security of person. Article 9 states that "Everyone has the right to liberty and security of person," and the section prohibits "arbitrary arrest or detention." The section continues, "No one shall be deprived of his liberty except on such grounds and in accordance with such procedure as are established by law."</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Canada">Canada</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Security_of_person&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Canada">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The right to security of the person was recognized in <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">Canada</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Bill_of_Rights" title="Canadian Bill of Rights">Canadian Bill of Rights</a> in 1960. Section 1(a) of this law recognized "the right of the individual to life, liberty, security of the person and enjoyment of property, and the right not to be deprived thereof except by <a href="/wiki/Due_process" title="Due process">due process</a> of law." However, the Bill of Rights was a statute and not part of the Constitution.</p>
<p>In 1982, a right to security of the person was added to the Constitution. It was included in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Section_Seven_of_the_Canadian_Charter_of_Rights_and_Freedoms" title="Section Seven of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms">section 7</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Charter_of_Rights_and_Freedoms" title="Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms">Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms</a>, which stipulates that "Everyone has the right to life, liberty and security of the person and the right not to be deprived thereof except in accordance with the principles of <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_justice" title="Fundamental justice">fundamental justice</a>." Security of the person in section 7 consists of rights to privacy of the body and its health<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup> and of the right protecting the "psychological integrity" of an individual. That is, the right protects against significant government-inflicted harm (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(medicine)" title="Stress (medicine)">stress</a>) to the mental state of the individual. (<i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blencoe_v._B.C._(Human_Rights_Commission)" title="Blencoe v. B.C. (Human Rights Commission)">Blencoe v. B.C. (Human Rights Commission)</a></i>, 2000)</p>
<p>This right has generated significant case law, as <a href="/wiki/Abortion_in_Canada" title="Abortion in Canada">abortion in Canada</a> was legalized in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R._v._Morgentaler" title="R. v. Morgentaler">R. v. Morgentaler</a></i> (1988) after the Supreme Court found the <a href="/wiki/Therapeutic_Abortion_Committee" title="Therapeutic Abortion Committee">Therapeutic Abortion Committees</a> breached women's security of person by threatening their health. Some judges also felt control of the body was a right within security of the person, breached by the abortion law. In <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Dismantle_v._The_Queen" title="Operation Dismantle v. The Queen">Operation Dismantle v. The Queen</a></i> (1985) <a href="/wiki/Cruise_missile" title="Cruise missile">cruise missile</a> testing was unsuccessfully challenged as violating security of the person for risking <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_warfare" title="Nuclear warfare">nuclear war</a>. In <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chaoulli_v._Quebec_(Attorney_General)" title="Chaoulli v. Quebec (Attorney General)">Chaoulli v. Quebec (Attorney General)</a></i> (2005), some Supreme Court justices even considered <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a>'s ban on private <a href="/wiki/Health_care" title="Health care">health care</a> to breach security of the person, since delays in medical treatment could have physical and stressful consequences.</p>
<p>There has been discussion within the <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_Canada" title="Supreme Court of Canada">Supreme Court</a> and among academics as to whether security of the person also guarantees some economic rights. Theoretically, security of the person would be breached if the government limits a person's ability to make an income, by denying <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_welfare_provision" title="Social welfare provision">welfare</a>, taking away property essential to one's profession, or denying licenses. However, section 7 is primarily concerned with legal rights, so this reading of economic rights is questionable. Many economic issues could also be <a href="/wiki/Political_question" title="Political question">political questions</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">South Africa</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Security_of_person&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: South Africa">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1996 the government of <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> adopted a constitutional <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitution_of_South_Africa_Chapter_2:_Bill_of_Rights" title="Constitution of South Africa Chapter 2: Bill of Rights">Bill of Rights</a> which recognized a right to security of the person in section 12. Here, it was combined with a "right to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freedom_(political)" title="Freedom (political)">freedom</a>." Section 12 went on to define security of the person and the right to freedom more thoroughly, including within it bodily control and reproductive control, freedom from torture and cruel and unusual punishment and a right to trial. In full, section 12 reads,</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<dl>
<dd>12. (1) Everyone has the right to freedom and security of the person, which includes the right
<dl>
<dd>(a) not to be deprived of freedom arbitrarily or without just cause;</dd>
<dd>(b) not to be detained without trial;</dd>
<dd>(c) to be free from all forms of violence from either public or private sources;</dd>
<dd>(d) not to be tortured in any way; and</dd>
<dd>(e) not to be treated or punished in a cruel, inhuman or degrading way.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>(2) Everyone has the right to bodily and psychological integrity, which includes the right
<dl>
<dd>(a) to make decisions concerning reproduction;</dd>
<dd>(b) to security in and control over their body; and</dd>
<dd>(c) not to be subjected to medical or scientific experiments without their informed consent.</dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Turkey">Turkey</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Security_of_person&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Turkey">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_Turkey" title="Constitution of Turkey">Constitution of Turkey</a> guarantees security of person, along with the right to liberty, in Article 19, enacted in 1982 and amended in 2001. The article spells out limits to these rights in the form of rulings of courts under the law, allowing for mental institutions and institutions for addicts, <a href="/wiki/Extradition" title="Extradition">extradition</a>, etc. The article also limits arrest and detention to cases in which a judge allows it, where there is not enough time for this, or the person is seen being responsible for a crime. A person will then be told why they have been arrested, and their <a href="/wiki/Next_of_kin" title="Next of kin">next of kin</a> will also be told of the arrest. Finally, the article allows for government compensation if these rights are violated.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">New Zealand</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Security_of_person&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: New Zealand">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Bill_of_Rights_Act" title="New Zealand Bill of Rights Act">New Zealand Bill of Rights Act</a>, adopted in 1990, guarantees "Life and security of the person" in sections 8 through 11. Section 8 guarantees a right to life except when deprived in accordance with fundamental justice, while section 9 prohibits cruel and unusual punishment. Section 10 prohibits a person being subjected to medical treatment against his or her will. Finally, section 11 gives a <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealander</a> the right to not take medical treatment.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom">United Kingdom</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Security_of_person&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United Kingdom">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Security of person is mentioned in Schedule I Article 5 of the <a href="/wiki/Human_Rights_Act_1998" title="Human Rights Act 1998">Human Rights Act 1998</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> This version is the latest incarnation of the Act, though there have been minor edits since.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> This new act represents one aspect of <a href="/wiki/Tony_Blair" title="Tony Blair">Tony Blair</a>'s "Constitutional Reform".</p>
