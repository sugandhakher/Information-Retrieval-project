<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>A product's <b>service life</b> is its period of use in service. It has been defined as "a product's total life in use from the point of sale to the point of discard" and contrasted with a product´s replacement life,"the period after which the initial purchaser returns to the shop for a replacement."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> Not to be confused with <a href="/wiki/Shelf_life" title="Shelf life">shelf life</a>, which deals with storage time, it is the time that any manufactured item can be expected to be 'serviceable' or <a href="/wiki/Product_support" title="Product support">supported</a> by its <a href="/wiki/Original_equipment_manufacturer" title="Original equipment manufacturer">manufacturer</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Determining a product´s expected service life consists of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Product_Life_Cycle_Management" title="Product Life Cycle Management">business policy</a>, using tools and calculations from <a href="/wiki/Maintainability" title="Maintainability">maintainability</a> and <a href="/wiki/Reliability_engineering" title="Reliability engineering">reliability analysis</a>. Service life is a unique commitment made by the item's manufacturer and is usually specified as a median. Actual service life is the maximal recorded life of a product.</p>
<p>Service life is different from a predicted life, or <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTBF" title="MTBF">MTTF/MTBF</a> (Mean Time to Failure/Mean Time Between Failures)/<a href="/wiki/Maintenance-free_operating_period" title="Maintenance-free operating period">MFOP</a> (maintenance-free operating period). Predicted life is useful such that a manufacturer may estimate, by hypothetical modeling and calculation, a general rule for which it will honor <a href="/wiki/Warranty" title="Warranty">warranty</a> claims, or planning for mission fulfillment. The difference between service life and predicted life is most clear when considering mission time and reliability in comparison to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MTBF" title="MTBF">MTBF</a> and service life. For example: A <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missile</a> system can have a mission time of less than one minute, a service life of 20 years, active MTBF of 20 minutes, dormant MTBF of 50 years and a reliability of .999999.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumer</a> item will have different expectations about service and <a href="/wiki/Car_longevity" title="Car longevity">longevity</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> based upon factors such as use, cost, and quality.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Product_strategy">Product strategy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Service_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Product strategy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Product_lifecycle_management" title="Product lifecycle management">Manufacturers</a> will commit to very conservative service life, usually 2 to 5 years for most commercial and consumer products (for example <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">computer</a> <a href="/wiki/Peripheral" title="Peripheral">peripherals</a> and <a href="/wiki/Electronic_component" title="Electronic component">components</a>). However, for large and expensive <a href="/wiki/Durable_good" title="Durable good">durable goods</a>, the items are not <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consumable" title="Consumable">consumable</a>, and service lives and maintenance activity will factor large in the service life. Again, an airliner might have a mission time of 11 hours, a predicted active MTBF of 10,000 hours with maintenance (or 15,000 hours without maintenance), a reliability of .99999 and a service life of 40 years.</p>
<p>The most common model for item lifetime is the <a href="/wiki/Bathtub_curve" title="Bathtub curve">bathtub curve</a>, a plot of the varying <a href="/wiki/Failure_rate" title="Failure rate">failure rate</a> as a function of time. During early life, the bathtub shows increased failures, usually witnessed during <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Product_development" title="Product development">product development</a>. The middle portion of the bathtub, or 'useful life', is a slightly inclined, nearly <a href="/wiki/Exponential_distribution" title="Exponential distribution">constant failure rate</a> period where the consumer enjoys the benefit conferred by the product. As the time increases further, the curve reaches a period of increasing failures, modeling the product's wearout phase.</p>
<p>For an individual product, the component parts may each have independent service lives, resulting in several bathtub curves. For instance, a tire will have a service life partitioning related to the <a href="/wiki/Retread" title="Retread">tread</a> and the casing.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Service_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For maintainable items, those wear-out items that are determined by logistical analysis to be provisioned for <a href="/wiki/Spare_part" title="Spare part">sparing</a> and replacement will assure a longer service life than manufactured items without such planning. A simple example is automotive <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tires" title="Tires">tires</a> - failure to plan for this wear out item would limit <a href="/wiki/Car_longevity" title="Car longevity">automotive service life</a> to the extent of a single set of tires.</p>
<p>An individual tire's life follows the <a href="/wiki/Bathtub_curve" title="Bathtub curve">bathtub curve</a>, to boot. After installation, there is a not-small probability of failure which may be related to material or workmanship or even to the process for mounting the tire which may introduce some small damage. After the initial period, the tire will perform, given no defect introducing event such as encountering a road hazard (a nail or a <a href="/wiki/Pothole" title="Pothole">pothole</a>), for a long duration relative to its expected service life which is a function of several variables (design, material, process). After a period, the failure probability will rise; for some tires, this will occur after the tread is worn out. Then, a secondary market for tires puts a <a href="/wiki/Retread" title="Retread">retread</a> on the tire thereby extending the service life. It is not uncommon for an 80,000-mile tire to perform well beyond that limit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
<p>It may be difficult to obtain reliable longevity data about many <a href="/wiki/Consumer" title="Consumer">consumer</a> products as, in general, efforts at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Actuarial" title="Actuarial">actuarial</a> analysis are not taken to the same extent as found with that needed to support <a href="/wiki/Insurance" title="Insurance">insurance</a> decisions. However, some attempts to provide this type of information have been made. An example is the collection of estimates for household components provided by the Old House Web<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> which gathers data from the Appliance Statistical Review and various institutes involved with the homebuilding trade.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="BOL_and_EOL">BOL and EOL</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Service_life&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: BOL and EOL">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For certain products, such as those that cannot be serviced during their operational life for technical reasons, a manufacturer may calculate a product's expected performance at both the beginning of operational life (BOL) and <a href="/wiki/End-of-life_(product)" title="End-of-life (product)">end of operational life</a> (EOL). Batteries and other components that degrade over time may affect the operation of a product. The performance of <a href="/wiki/Mission_critical" title="Mission critical">mission critical</a> components is therefore calculated for EOL, with the components exceeding their <a href="/wiki/Specification_(technical_standard)" title="Specification (technical standard)">specification</a> at BOL. For example, with spaceflight hardware, which must survive in the harsh environment of space, the capacity to generate electricity from solar panels or <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator" title="Radioisotope thermoelectric generator">radioisotope thermoelectric generator</a> is likely to reduce throughout a mission, but must still meet a specific requirement at EOL in order to complete the mission. A spacecraft may also have a BOL mass that is greater than its EOL mass as propellant is depleted during its operational life.</p>
