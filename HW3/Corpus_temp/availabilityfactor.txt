<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>The <b>availability factor</b> of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Power_plant" title="Power plant">power plant</a> is the amount of time that it is able to produce <a href="/wiki/Electricity" title="Electricity">electricity</a> over a certain period, divided by the amount of the time in the period. Occasions where only partial capacity is available may or may not be deducted. Where they are deducted, the metric is titled <i>equivalent availability factor</i> (EAF). The availability factor should not be confused with the <a href="/wiki/Capacity_factor" title="Capacity factor">capacity factor</a>. The capacity factor for a period will always be less than the availability factor for the same period. The difference depends on the choice to run the plant or not.</p>
<p>The availability of a power plant varies greatly depending on the type of <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a>, the design of the plant and how the plant is operated. Everything else being equal, plants that are run less frequently have higher availability factors because they require less maintenance. Most <a href="/wiki/Thermal_power_station" title="Thermal power station">thermal power stations</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Coal" title="Coal">coal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_power" title="Geothermal power">geothermal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_power" title="Nuclear power">nuclear power</a> plants, have availability factors between 70% and 90%. Newer plants tend to have significantly higher availability factors, but preventive maintenance is as important as improvements in design and technology. <a href="/wiki/Gas_turbine" title="Gas turbine">Gas turbines</a> have relatively high availability factors, ranging from 80% to 99%. Gas turbines are commonly used for <a href="/wiki/Peaking_power_plant" title="Peaking power plant">peaking power plants</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Co-generation" title="Co-generation">co-generation</a> plants and the first stage of <a href="/wiki/Combined_cycle" title="Combined cycle">combined cycle</a> plants.</p>
<p>Historically the term only referred to the power plant, as fossil fuel was always available. With the increased impact of renewable energy, the topic of no "fuel" (water, wind, sunlight) may confuse the terminology. The availability factor of <a href="/wiki/Wind_power" title="Wind power">wind</a> and <a href="/wiki/Solar_power" title="Solar power">solar power</a> plants depends on whether or not periods when the plant is operational, but there is no <a href="/wiki/Wind" title="Wind">wind</a> or <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">sunlight</a>, are counted as available, unavailable, or disregarded.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> If they are only counted as available during favorable times, photovoltaic plants have an availability factor approaching or equal to 100%. Modern <a href="/wiki/Wind_turbine" title="Wind turbine">wind turbines</a> also have very high availability factors, about 98%. However, solar and wind plants have relatively low <a href="/wiki/Capacity_factor" title="Capacity factor">capacity factors</a>. Wind capacity factors range from 20â€“40% and solar capacity factors in Arizona are about 19%. This makes wind and solar availability factors much lower if times when sunlight or wind are not available are deducted from the plants availability.</p>
